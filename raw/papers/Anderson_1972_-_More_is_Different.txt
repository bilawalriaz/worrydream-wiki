# "More Is Different" by P. W. Anderson (1972)
# Extracted from web_extract of KIT PDF (scanned original, text unavailable)
# Source: Science, Volume 177, Number 4047, 4 August 1972

The article opposes the common but fallacious assumption that because everything obeys fundamental laws ("reductionism"), only the study of those laws is fundamental.

"The reductionist hypothesis may still be a topic for controversy among philosophers, but among the great majority of active scientists I think it is accepted without question... The main fallacy in this kind of thinking is that the reductionist hypothesis does not by any means imply a 'constructionist' one: The ability to reduce everything to simple fundamental laws does not imply the ability to start from those laws and reconstruct the universe."

Intensive vs. Extensive Research (Weisskopf's framework):
- Intensive: Seeks fundamental laws (e.g., high-energy physics, particle physics).
- Extensive: Applies known laws to explain phenomena (e.g., solid-state physics, biology).
- Anderson argues that extensive research can be just as fundamental in nature.

The Hierarchy of Sciences:
Sciences can be arranged linearly, where the elementary entities of science X obey the laws of science Y.
- Example: elementary particle physics -> many-body physics -> chemistry -> molecular biology -> cell biology -> physiology -> psychology -> social sciences
- Crucial Point: This hierarchy does not mean science X is "just applied Y." Each level requires entirely new laws, concepts, and creative generalizations.

"Psychology is not applied biology, nor is biology applied chemistry."

The Concept of Broken Symmetry:
This is the key mechanism explaining why "more is different." At each level of complexity, new properties emerge that are not simple extrapolations from the level below.

Key Example: The Ammonia Molecule (NH3):
1. Symmetrical Laws, Unsymmetrical Structure: The laws of physics are symmetric (parity), but the molecule's pyramid shape appears asymmetric (dipole moment).
2. Resolution via Quantum Tunneling: The nitrogen atom tunnels rapidly through the hydrogen plane, inverting the pyramid. The true stationary state is a symmetric superposition of both orientations, preserving the overall symmetry of the laws.
3. Scale & Complexity Break the Symmetry: In larger, heavier molecules (e.g., PH3, PF3), inversion slows or becomes unmeasurable. In very complex systems like sugar molecules, inversion is effectively impossible. Here, the symmetry of the system becomes "broken."

General Implications of Broken Symmetry:
- Physics is the study of symmetry.
- The internal structure of matter need not reflect the symmetry of its total state.
- Large systems often spontaneously adopt states with lower symmetry than the fundamental laws suggest (e.g., crystals, superconductors).

Superconductivity as a Prime Example:
- A spectacular case of broken symmetry where a new, unexpected macroscopic quantum state emerges.
- Understanding it was impossible to derive synthetically from fundamental laws due to its complex, collective nature.
- This led to technological advances (e.g., the Josephson effect).

Implications for Complex Systems & Life:
As we ascend the hierarchy, we encounter higher levels of broken symmetry and complexity:
1. Information-Bearing Crystallinity: Regular spatial structure that can be "read" (e.g., DNA, lines of type).
2. Temporal Ordering: Regularity in time (pulsing, oscillation), crucial for energy extraction, information handling, and development in living systems.
3. Functional Structure & Specialization: Hierarchy and specialization of function.
4. Increasing Complication: Beyond a certain point, the focus shifts from decreasing symmetry to increasing complication.

"There are more levels of organization between human ethology and DNA than there are between DNA and quantum electrodynamics, and each level can require a whole new conceptual structure."

Conclusion: The "One-Way Street":
The relationship between a complex system and its parts is intellectually a one-way street: analysis is fruitful, but synthesis from first principles is often impossible.

"Marx said that quantitative differences become qualitative ones, but a dialogue in Paris in the 1920's sums it up even more clearly:
FITZGERALD: The rich are different from us.
HEMINGWAY: Yes, they have more money."
