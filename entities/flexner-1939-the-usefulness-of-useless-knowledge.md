# Flexner 1939 - The Usefulness of Useless Knowledge

## Core Thesis

Abraham Flexner's central argument is a pointed, historically grounded defense of curiosity-driven, non-instrumental research as the ultimate, though unpredictable, engine of societal progress. The thesis is not merely that "useless" knowledge is good, but that it is *fundamentally necessary* and that the practical utility it eventually generates is a *byproduct*, not a goal. A crucial nuance is his implicit critique of a narrowing societal definition of "usefulness." He argues that the value placed on immediate application risks crippling the very intellectual environment that produces transformative, long-term utility. The "uselessness" is not a permanent state but a temporal phase; the work is initially deemed useless precisely because its implications are invisible to contemporary pragmatic eyes. The true "useful men" are not the inventors who apply existing knowledge, but the [[maxwell-2006-tracing-the-dynabook|Maxwell]], Faraday, and Hertz figures who create the new paradigms from which invention springs.

## Historical Context

Flexner wrote in 1939, on the cusp of World War II. The essay reflects two powerful historical currents. First, it engages with the longstanding debate about "liberal" versus "useful" education, a tension stretching back to the classical tradition versus the rise of the polytechnic institute. Flexner himself had been instrumental in reforming medical education along rigorous [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] lines, making his defense of pure science particularly authoritative.

Second, and more urgent, the essay is a direct response to the growing pressure for science to justify itself through immediate, tangible benefits, especially in the face of economic depression (the memory of the 1930s) and looming geopolitical crisis. The "current of daily life" he references is the clamor for social and economic reform, which he explicitly supports but warns is becoming dangerously dominant. The essay is a pre-emptive strike against the weaponization of science for nationalist ends (which he saw in Nazi Germany) and the potential defunding of [[goldman-1975-the-need-for-basic-research-in-industry|basic research]] in favor of narrow, applied goals. The state of the field was one of increasing specialization and pressure for return on investment, threatening the open-ended inquiry that Flexner champions.

## Key Contributions

1.  **The "Flexner Model" of Research Funding:** The essay provided the philoso[[goldman-1975-the-need-for-basic-research-in-industry|phical justifi]]cation for the post-WWII funding model for basic research, epitomized by [[owens-1986-vannevar-bush-and-the-differential-analyzer|Vannevar Bush]]'s "Science, the Endless Frontier" (1945). It argued for the societal trust required to fund researchers without demanding immediate deliverables, establishing the principle that curiosity itself is a public good.
2.  **The Historical Case Study as Polemic:** Flexner pioneered a powerful rhetorical method: using compelling, narrative case[[maxwell-2006-tracing-the-dynabook| studie]]s from the history of science ([[maxwell-2006-tracing-the-dynabook|Maxwell]], Faraday, Einstein, Gauss) to demonstrate the unpredictable, non-linear path from "useless" theory to world-changing technology. He shifted the argument from abstract principle to concrete historical evidence.
3.  **Redefining "Usefulness" in the Long Term:** He introduced a potent, counter-intu[[maxwell-2006-tracing-the-dynabook|itive d]]istinction between the *technologist* (Marconi) and the *scientist* ([[maxwell-2006-tracing-the-dynabook|Maxwell]]), arguing that the latter's contribution is orders of magnitude more valuable, despite being less visible and immediate. This reframed societal notions of utility across a much longer timescale.

## Methodology

Flexner's methodology is **historical polemic**. It is an argument by analogy and induction, structured as follows:
1.  **Anecdotal Opening:** He uses a personal conversation with George Eastman to dramatize the conflict between immediate, practical definitions of usefulness and the deeper, foundational kind he advocates.
2[[maxwell-2006-tracing-the-dynabook|.  **Ex]]emplary Case Studies:** He presents a sequence of historical vignettes, each designed to [[mueller-prove-2002-vision-and-reality-of-hypertext|prove]] his thesis. [[maxwell-2006-tracing-the-dynabook|Maxwell]]/Hertz for radio, Faraday for electricity, Gauss for relativity, Einstein for [[bush-2010-quantum-mechanics-writ-large|quantum mechanics]]. The selection is deliberate, covering physics, mathematics, and their technological offshoots.
3.  **Analogical Extension:** He broadens the argument from science to the humanities and art in the final, truncated section, suggesting the same principle of "useless" cultivation applies to spiritual and aesthetic life.
4.  **Direct Rebuttal:** He explicitly engages with and rebuts the prevailing sentiment of his day ("ours is a materialistic age"), conceding the importance of social science but cautioning against its monopolizing intellectual[[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966| energy.
T]]he argument is not empirical in a modern [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] sense; it is a *historiographic* and *philosophical* argument aimed at persuading funders, educators, and the public.

## Influence

The influence of this essay is profound and directly traceable:

*   **Post-WWII Science Policy:** It is the intellectual cornerstone of the model for funding[[goldman-1975-the-need-for-basic-research-in-industry| agencies like]] the National Science Foundation (NSF) and the National Institutes of Health (NIH). The concept of "basic research" as distinct from "applied research" or "development" gained immense institutional power after the war, heavily influenced by Flexner's logic.
*   **The Institute for Advanced Study (IAS):** As its founding director, Flexner built the IAS as the physical embodiment of his thesis—a place where mathematicians and scientists (like Einstein) could pursue curiosity without the pressure of teaching or immediate application. The model influenced countless research universities and think tanks.
*   **Cultural Defense of Science:** The essay became a canonical text for scientists and advocates defending fundamental research against budget cuts or demands for immediate ROI. It provided a ready-made, eloquent rebuttal to such pressures.
* [[owens-1986-vannevar-bush-and-the-differential-analyzer|  **Citation ]]Lineage:** The ideas are explicitly echoed in [[vannevar-bush-symposium-1995-closing-panel|Vannevar]] [[bush-1945-as-we-may-think-with-engelbart-notes|Bush]]'s 1945 report, and by figures like I.I. Rabi in advocating for the model of the national laboratories and the NSF. It remains a staple in science policy and philosophy of science syllabi.

## Modern Relevance

Flexner's argument is acutely relevant in several contemporary domains:

*   **AI & Machine Learning Research:** The field is riven by the [[smith-r-1987-experiences-with-the-alternate-reality-kit-an-example-of-the-tension-between-literalism-and-magic|tension between]] Flexner's model (pursuing fundamental understanding of intelligence, learning, and reasoning, even without a clear application) and the dominant industrial model of rapid, applied iteration aimed at products and benchmarks. Flexner would caution that over-emphasis on benchmarks and short-term metrics stifles the discovery of transformative new paradigms, like the leap from symbolic AI to neural networks which had long periods of "uselessness."
*   **Funding for Blue-Sky Research:** In an era of increased scrutiny on research impact (using metrics like "societal benefit"), Flexner's essay is a powerful counter-argument. It defends the need for protected spaces for exploratory work in fields like quantum computing, theoretical biology, and AI alignment, where the most critical breakthroughs may be years away.
*   **Knowledge Management & Innovation:** Modern corporate R&D labs (like the legendary Bell Labs, which Flexner's model inspired) often struggle to balance short-term product development with the long-term, curiosity-driven research that Flexner defends. The essay argues that the latter is the true source of disruptive innovation.
*   **Education Reform:** The pressure in STEM education to focus immediately on job skills mirrors the trend Flexner warned against. He would argue for curricula that cultivate the curiosity and foundational thinking that allows students to adapt to future, unforeseen technological landscapes, rather than training for today's specific tools.

## Key Quotes

1.  **"Curiosity, which may or may not eventuate in something useful, is probably the outstanding characteristic of modern thinking... and it must be absolutely unhampered."**
    *   *Analysis:* This is the essay's philosophical core. Flexner identifies curiosity as a self-sufficient, modern virtue. The phrase "absolutely unhampered" is a direct demand for intellectual freedom from external agendas, be they political, commercial, or social.

2.  **"Institutions of learning should be devoted to the cultivation of curiosity and the less they are deflected by considerations of immediacy of application, the more likely they are to contribute not only to human welfare but to the equally important satisfaction of intellectual interest..."**
    *   *Analysis:* Here Flexner makes the institutional claim. He argues that the goals of contributing to welfare *and* satisfying intellectual interest are not in conflict; in fact, focusing on the latter is the best way to achieve the former in t[[maxwell-2006-tracing-the-dynabook|he long]] run. It’s a rebuttal to the false dichotomy between "useful" and "beautiful" knowledge.

3.  **"Who were the useful men? Not Marconi, but Clerk [[maxwell-2006-tracing-the-dynabook|Maxwell]] and Heinrich Hertz."**
    *   *Analysis:* The essay's most memorable and subversive statement. It completely inverts conventional, short-term definitions of utility. The value lies in creating new conceptual frameworks, not in applying existing ones. This re-evaluation of historical credit is radical.

4.  **"At no period of his unmatched career was he interested in utility. He was absorbed in disentangling the riddles of the universe... In the end, utility resulted, but it was never a criterion to which his ceaseless experimentation could be subjected."**
    *   *Analysis:* A description of Faraday that serves as the ideal type for Flexner's scientist. The key word is "criterion." Utility is presented not as a goal, but as a prohibited evaluative metric that would "restrict... restless curiosity." This is a defense of research methodol[[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|ogy free f]]rom utilitarian judgment.

5.  **"We may look at this question from two points of view: the [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] and the humanistic or spiritual... the world were emptied of some of the useless things that give it spiritual significance."**
    *   *Analysis:* This reveals the essay's broader humanistic scope. Flexner isn't just defending physics; he's defending a culture that values activities for their intrinsic, meaning-giving prope[[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|rties. The]] "useless" things—art, philosophy, beauty—are what constitute a "full life," and their cultivation runs parallel to and is interdependent with [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] inquiry.
