# Buxton 2005 - Interaction at Lincoln Laboratory in the 1960

## Core Thesis
The paper's central argument is twofold. First, it contends that the foundational work in human-computer interaction (HCI) and interactive computing conducted at MIT's Lincoln Laboratory in the 1960s, centered on the TX-2 computer, has been unjustly forgotten, with only Ivan [[sutherland-2012-the-tyranny-of-the-clock-short|Sutherland]]'s Sketchpad achieving lasting fame. Second, and more importantly, it argues that the most critical innovation of this period was not any single project, but the **sustained research culture** that enabled them. This culture—characterized by freedom, mentorship, a commitment to building complete tools from concept to use, and a focus on creating an intimate, interactive computing environment—is presented as a timeless model for fostering transformative innovation. The paper is a polemic against the erosion of this history and a call to re-examine these underdeveloped ideas and cultural practices for modern relevance.

## Historical Context
The work emerged in the early 1960s, a period dominated by large, remote, batch-processing mainframes managed by priesthoods of technicians. The dominant paradigm, as Wesley [[clark-w-1986-the-linc-was-early-and-small|Clark]] noted, was the "computer not as tool, but as demigod." Lincoln Laboratory, funded by the Department of Defense for [[air-force-1960-air-force-office-of-scientific-research|air]] defense research, provided a unique environment. Its technological lineage began with the Whirlwind computer (1951), which was interactive and displayed real-time gra[[clark-w-1986-the-linc-was-early-and-small|phics]]. This DNA was carried forward by Wesley [[clark-d-1988-the-design-philosophy-of-the-darpa-internet-protocols|Clark]] into the TX-0 and TX-2. These machines were designed as accessible, interactive platforms with graphical displays, light pens, and rapid context switching. The problem being solved was twofold: technologically, how to make computers responsive and graphical instruments for direct human manipulation; and culturally, how to create an environment where researchers could freely experiment with this new paradigm without the constraints of batch processing or narrow, short-term goals.

## Key Contributions
The paper does not introduce new technical systems but makes several meta-contributions:
1.  **Historical Reclamation:** It explicitly names the const[[clark-w-1986-the-linc-was-early-and-small|ellat]]ion of "unsung" projects and figures (Ron Baecker's *Genesys* animation system, Wesley [[clark-d-1988-the-design-philosophy-of-the-darpa-internet-protocols|Clark]]'s [[linc-1965-convocation-on-the-mississippi|LINC]] and [[kay-1975-personal-computing|personal computing]] philosophy, Larry Roberts' networking and 3D graphics work) that formed the fertile bedrock from which later icons like Sketchpad emerged.
2.  **Cultural Articulation:** It provides the first clear, enumerated articulation of the Lincoln Lab research culture as a model for innovation, listing specific tenets (freedom to pursue ideas, supporting the full "food chain," no short-term payoff pressure, mentoring, tool-building[[sutherland-2012-the-tyranny-of-the-clock-short|).
3.  **R]]eframing "Easy" Work:** It elevates Ivan [[sutherland-1963-sketchpad-a-man-machine-graphical-communication-system|Sutherland]]'s observation that successful research often involves picking problems that are "easy enough to do" and positioning this as a function of environment. The culture of Lincoln Lab made hard problems *seem* easier by providing the necessary tools, collaboration, and support.
4.  **Argument for Latent Potential:** It posits that much of this early work, particularly in real-time animation and [[negroponte-1976-an-idiosyncratic-systems-approach-to-interactive-graphics|interactive graphics]], has not been fully absorbed or built upon, meaning its potential remains "largely underdeveloped" and relevant today.

## Methodology
The methodology is that of a **historical retrospective and testimonial panel**[[sutherland-2012-the-tyranny-of-the-clock-short|. Buxton, ]]as organizer, uses archival film footage and first-person narratives from key protagonists ([[sutherland-1963-sketchpad-a-man-machine-graphical-communication-system|Sutherland]], [[clark-d-1988-the-design-philosophy-of-the-darpa-internet-protocols|Clark]], Baecker, [[richardson-1992-looking-at-proteins|Richardson]]) to reconstruct the past. The argument is built on:
-   **Eyewitness Testimony:** Primary source accounts from the builders themselves.
-   **Technological Genealogy:** Tracing the direct lineage of ideas and hardware from Whirlwind -> TX-0 -> TX-2 -> DEC PDP-1 -> Xerox [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]] -> modern PCs.
-   **Cultural Analysis:** Inferring the principles of a successful research environment from the observed outcomes and the reflections of its participants.
-   **Polemical Framing:** Buxton uses the historical account to critique the present, arguing that the community has forgotten its roots and that these forgotten roots offer solutions to contemporary research challenges.

## Influence
The immediate influence of the panel itself was to raise awareness at a major HCI conference (CHI 2005). Its deeper influence is in its role as a preservative and reinterpretation of history. The work [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|it c]]hronicles had direct, monumental influence:
-   **Xerox P[[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|ARC:]]** The graphical [[perkins-1997-inventing-the-lisa-user-interface|user interface]], mouse, and bitmapped display at [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]] are direct descendants of the Lincoln Lab environment and tools.
-   **The Internet:** Larry Roberts' work on packet-switching networks, built on Lincoln's TX-2, led to ARPANET.
-   **Modern HCI & Graphics:** The foundations of [[hutchins-1985-direct-manipulation-interfaces|direct manipulation]], computer-aided design (CAD), and interactive animation were l[[linc-1965-convocation-on-the-mississippi|aid ]]here. Baecker's *Genes[[clark-w-1986-the-linc-was-early-and-small|ys* p]]ioneered principles of direct animation [[kay-1975-personal-computing|and user-centered ]]design.
-   **Personal Computing:** Wesley [[clark-d-1988-the-design-philosophy-of-the-darpa-internet-protocols|Clark]]'s [[linc-1965-convocation-on-the-mississippi|LINC]] and his philosophy of the computer as "lab equipment" anticipated the [[kay-1972-a-personal-computer-for-children-of-all-ages|personal computer]] by over a decade.
-   **Cultural Legacy:** The paper its[[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|elf ]]has been cited in discussions of research management and the history of computing, particularly in arguments for long-term, mission-driven research as practiced at places like [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]] and Bell Labs.

## Modern Relevance
The paper's relevance is strikingly contemporary. It speaks directly to:
1.  **AI Research Culture:** In the age of rapid, benchmark-driven AI development, Lincoln Lab's model of long-term, unconstrained research with deep mentorship is a cautionary counterpoint. It argues for balancing short-term wins with investments in the "environment" (tools, data, collaborative culture) that allows paradigm-shifting breakthroughs.
2.  **Knowledge Work & Collaboration Tools:** Baecker's *Genesys*, with its "p-curves" for dynamic chalk-talk dialogues, is prescient. The need for fluid, shared visual thinking tools on large-format displays is a central challenge in today's remote/hybrid work and collaborative AI systems. The paper suggests that solutions may lie in forgotten interactive techniques.
3.  **Hardware/Software Co-Design:** The TX-2 was designed *for* interaction. This holistic approach is now revisited in fields like edge AI and custom ML hardware (TPUs), where the tool is shaped by the intended human use.
4.  **The Value of Long-Term Research:** As corporations and governments debate research funding, Lincoln Lab se[[linc-1965-convocation-on-the-mississippi|rves]] as a case study. Sketchpad did not emerge until 12 years after the[[clark-w-1986-the-linc-was-early-and-small| lab']]s founding. The paper is an argument for patience and for funding research cultures, not just projects.
