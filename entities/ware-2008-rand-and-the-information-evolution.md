# Ware 2008 - RAND and the Information Evolution

## Core Thesis
The central argument of Willis Ware's memoir is not a theoretical proposition but a historical claim: the RAND Corporation, through its unique post-war research culture and specific projects like the JOHNNIAC computer and the JOSS programming language, was a crucial, early catalyst in the transition from viewing computers as mere number-crunching machines to understanding "information" as a fundamental, strategic entity. The nuance lies in the timing and context. Ware argues that this conceptual shift—the "Information Evolution"—was not yet recognized as such when it happened. RAND's contribution was in creating the environment where, driven by the practical demands of Cold War strategic analysis, researchers intuitively grasped the primacy of information long before it became a dominant societal narrative. The book is a corrective to that historical gap, positioning RAND as the seedbed for both computer science as a discipline and the modern information society.

## Historical Context
The work emerges from the immediate post-WWII period, defined by the Cold War, the nascent space race, and a flood of military-funded research. Before RAND, computing was in its infancy; machines like ENIAC were programmed via plugboards and were seen as giant calculators for ballistics and atomic physics. The problem RAND addressed was not technical but cognitive: how to model, simulate, and strategize about immensely complex geopolitical conflicts. This required a leap from computation (calculating) to computation as *information processing*—organizing, manipulating, and interpreting vast datasets to inform policy. RAND was founded in 1948 by the US [[air-force-1960-air-force-office-of-scientific-research|Air]] [[air-force-1960-air-force-office-of-scientific-research|Force]] specifically to create a "think tank" that could bridge military strategy and [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] research, a novel institutional form. The field itself lacked definition; there were no "computer science" departments. RAND's early work on computing, therefore, occurred in a vacuum of established theory, forcing a more pragmatic, application-driven exploration of what these machines could *do* beyond arithmetic.

## Key Contributions
1.  **Institutional Model for Applied Research:** RAND pioneered the model of an independent, non-profit research institution performing long-term, high-risk analysis for government sponsors, creating a protected space for fundamental inquiry. This model was replicated globally.
2.  **The JOHNNIAC and JOSS:** The operation of its own computer (JOHNNIAC) led directly to the development of the JOHNNIAC Open Shop System (JOSS), an interactive, time-shared programming environment. JOSS was a major step towards user-friendly computing and established RAND as a leader in programming language design and human-computer interaction.
3.  **Early Recognition of Information Security and Privacy:** As computing moved toward networking (with projects like the AN/FSQ-32 and connections to SAGE), RAND was among the first to systematically confront the problems of system security, authentication, and data privacy, laying early groundwork for computer ethics and cybersecurity.
4.  **Fostering Computer Science as a Discipline:** By treating computing research as a core, legitimate activity—not just a support function—RAND helped legitimize the field. Its staff, like John [[mccarthy-1979-history-of-lisp|McCarthy]] (LISP) and others, moved into academia, carrying with them the seeds of a new academic discipline.
5.  **Conceptual Shift to Information Primacy:** The book’s opening aphorism—"Energy permits things to exist; information, to behave purposefully"—encapsulates the core intellectual contribution. RAND's projects collectively demonstrated that the true power of computing was in enabling purposeful, intelligent behavior through information control.

## Methodology
The work is an **institutional and personal memoir**, a historical narrative structured as a series of essays and vignettes. It is not an academic paper with a formal argument. Its authority derives from Ware's position as an insider and chronicler (he joined RAND in 1952 and spent his career there). The methodology is **archival and anecdotal**, pieced together from surviving RAND reports, internal memos, and personal memory. It traces themes (security, programming, hardware) chronologically and through specific projects, aiming to reconstruct the intellectual atmosphere and the accidental nature of the "Information Evolution." It is a **foundational history**, seeking to document and claim a legacy for an institution and its people in the broader narrative of technology.

## Influence
The direct influence is profound within the military-academic complex and the nascent computer science community. RAND reports became seminal texts. Alumni of its computer department populated the early computer science faculties of universities like Stanford, MIT, and UCLA, directly transmitting RAND's approach to research and teaching. The time-sharing concepts from JOSS influenced subsequent systems like MULTICS and UNIX. The work on networking and security provided essential, early frameworks for the ARPANET/Internet. More broadly, the book itself, as a late-life institutional history, serves as a primary source for the sociology of science and the history of computing, influencing how the field understands its own origins beyond the traditional narrative of university labs and commercial firms like IBM.

## Modern Relevance
The memoir is strikingly relevant to contemporary issues in AI, cybersecurity, and organizational knowledge management.
1.  **AI and Foundational Models:** RAND's shift from computing-as-calculation to computing-as-information-processing prefigures the current paradigm shift in AI from narrow, rule-based systems to large language models that operate on a fundamental level of statistical information and semantic pattern recognition.
2.  **The "Information" vs. "AI" Narrative:** Ware's insistence that we now live in an "information society" frames current debates about AI as the *next* evolution. Understanding the prior evolution (from machines to information) is critical context for the current one.
3.  **Cybersecurity and Privacy:** RAND's early, urgent work on securing networked computer systems for the military directly presages today's global challenges of cybersecurity, data privacy, and state-sponsored hacking. The institutional problems they faced—balancing access, security, and functionality—remain unchanged.
4.  **Research Culture:** The RAND model—of a small, elite, interdisciplinary group with long-term goals, protected from immediate commercial or political pressures—is often cited (e.g., Xerox [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]], early Google) as a blueprint for innovative tech research, offering a counterpoint to today's fast-paced, metrics-driven corporate R&D.
5.  **Knowledge Work:** The book is a case study in how a knowledge organization operates and preserves its intellectual legacy, relevant to any institution grappling with documentation, archival practices, and institutional memory in the digital age.

## Key Quotes

1.  **"Energy permits things to exist; information, to behave purposefully."**
    *Analysis: This aphorism is the book's thesis in miniature. It reframes the world not in terms of matter and energy (physics) but as a hierarchy where information is the controlling layer that gives rise to purpose, strategy, and intelligence—the very things RAND was built to model.*

2.  **"When the events and achievements in this document were unfolding, the centrality of information in the scheme of things was only vaguely recognized and little discussed."**
    *Analysis: This sets up the historical claim. The "Information Evolution" was not a self-conscious movement but an emergent property of solving hard problems. Ware's task is to retrospectively identify and articulate this subconscious shift.*

3.  **"The department’s life span paralleled the evolution and growth of a commercial computing industry and the concurrent rise of computer science as an accepted discipline in academia."**
    *Analysis: Positions RAND's internal history as a microcosm of the entire field's origin story. It argues that institutional history and disciplinary history are inseparable in this case.*

4.  **"The many projects that they [programmers] have undertaken are so varied that it would not be possible to even catalog them... Theirs is a story unto itself."**
    *Analysis: A revealing admission and a classic historian's dilemma. It highlights how the foundational, creative labor of programmers and operators, which made the theoretical and hardware advances possible, is often lost to formal history because it was too varied, practical, and ubiquitous to document.*

5.  **"There are only memories."**
    *Analysis: This stark statement underscores the fragility of institutional knowledge and the primary-source nature of Ware's memoir. It’s a warning about the historical record and an argument for the necessity of such personal accounts, even if imperfect.*

6.  **"RAND, as well as a generous grant by Paul Baran, supported production and publication of this document."**
    *Analysis: The fact that Paul Baran (pioneer of packet switching, the core of the Internet) funded the book is symbolically crucial. It shows that the community understood this history as foundational to their own work (the Internet) and needed it preserved, creating a direct lineage from RAND's early information work to the modern networked world.*
