# [[jaynes-1990-probability-in-quantum-theory|Jaynes]] 1990 - Scattering of Light by Free Electrons

## Core Thesis

The paper's central, provocative thesis is that the standard interpretation of [[bush-2010-quantum-mechanics-writ-large|quantum mechanics]] (the Copenhagen interpretation) is a "system of epicycles"—an empirically flexible but physically empty mathematical formalism that has been mistaken for a fundamental theory. [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]] argues that this interpretive framework is "schizoid," simultaneously claiming to be a complete description of reality while being unable to describe *what is really happening* in any specific case (e.g., radioactive decay, light emission). He contends that this stagnation stems from the premature dismissal of Erwin Schrödinger's more physical, wave-mechanical interpretation of the electron [[cramer-2020-symmetry-transactions-and-the-mechanism-of-wave-function-collapse|wave function]] in favor of Werner Heisenberg's abstract, matrix-based formalism. The paper's specific proposal is to revive Schrödinger's view by invoking the stabilizing effects of *Zitterbewegung* (a rapid, trembling motion predicted by the Dirac equation) and to suggest modern experiments (like the scattering of light by free electrons) that could test this alternative framework. The nuance is not merely that QM is incomplete, but that its current success has actively prevented the search for the deeper, nonlinear "chromosomes and DNA" of physics.

## Historical Context

The paper emerges from a long-simmering frustration among physicists, dating back to the foundational debates of the 1920s and 30s. By 1990, quantum electrodynamics (QED) was fantastically successful, yet its interpretive foundations remained contentious. [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]] positions himself in the lineage of dissenters like Einstein, Schrödinger, and later David Bohm, who were uneasy with the Copenhagen interpretation's abandonment of objective realism and deterministic causality. The immediate context is a 1990 workshop on "The Electron," reflecting a renewed interest in foundational questions about this core particle. [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]] contrasts the state of [[jaynes-1990-probability-in-quantum-theory|quantum theory]] with "physical theories" like Newtonian mechanics, general relativity, and Mendelian genetics, which he argues were deduced from clear, constraining principles. In contrast, quantum formalism grew from pragmatic, trial-and-error adjustments (e.g., incorporating spin via Pauli matrices, quantizing fields à la Dirac) to fit experimental results. The historical problem being addressed is the interpretive and conceptual quagmire that followed this pragmatic development, which [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]] believes has led to a 60-year freeze in basic understanding since the 1927 Solvay Congress.

## Key Contributions

1.  **The Epicycle Metaphor for Quantum Theory:** [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]] forcefully repurposes the historical analogy[[bush-2010-quantum-mechanics-writ-large| of Ptolemaic epi]]cycles to critique modern [[froehner-1993-quantum-mechanics-how-weird-for-bayesians|quantum mechanics]]. He argues that the QM formalism is so mathematically flexible (like adding endless epicycles) that it can accommodate *any* phenomenology, rendering it "empty of physical content." The contribution is not the metaphor itself, but its systematic application to highlight the lack of constraining first principles in QM's development.
2.  **Diagnosis of "Schizoid" Interpretation:** He provides a sharp diagnosis of the Copenhagen interpretation's logical contradictions: it claims completeness yet is observationally incomplete; it rejects asking "what is really happening" while practitioners still intuit a reality it cannot describe. He distinguishes Bohr's original, more epistemological project from the "currently taught" orthodoxy, which he sees as a distortion.
3.  **Revival of Schrödinger's Interpretation via *Zitterbewegung*:** The paper's constructive proposal is to re-examine Schrödinger's wave-function-as-reality view, which was historically sidelined. [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]] suggests that the rapid oscillatory motion (*Zitterbewegung*) inherent in the Dirac equation could provide the missing "stabilizing effects" that Schrödinger's picture lacked, potentially offering a more intuitive, physical ontology for the electron.
4.  **Call for Foundational Experimentation:** While theoretical, the paper's concrete contribution is its suggestion that experiments on the scattering of light by free electrons, now feasible with new technology, could serve as a direct test to decide between the standard formalism and a Schrödinger-*Zitterbewegung* based theory. This moves the argument from pure philosophy to a proposed empirical arbiter.

## Methodology

[[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]] employs a **polemical and historical-conceptual methodology**. The structure is that of a philosophical critique and a research manifesto, not a typical theoretical physics paper. He begins with a sweeping historical analogy (epicycles vs. chromosomes) to frame the entire problem. He then contrasts "physical theories" (built from principles) with "empirical technology" (QM/QED, built from trial-and-error). The core of the argument is a logical and conceptual analysis of the Copenhagen interpretation's contradictions. The paper is deeply interdisciplinary, drawing on history of science, philosophy of science, and foundational physics. It is fundamentally **rhetorical**, designed to persuade physicists to question their foundational assumptions and consider a long-abandoned alternative. The methodology is the application of clear-eyed, first-principles reasoning to a field he sees as mired in dogma and mathematical formalism.

## Influence

Within mainstream physics, this paper had limited direct influence, as its critique is too fundamental and its proposal too radical for a field focused on calculation and empirical confirmation within the existing paradigm. However, it is a sig[[bush-2010-quantum-mechanics-writ-large|nificant document]] in the **ongoing debate about the foundations and inte[[bush-2010-quantum-mechanics-writ-large|rpretation of qua]]ntum mechanics**. It is cited by researchers and phi[[froehner-1993-quantum-mechanics-how-weird-for-bayesians|losophers explori]]ng alternative interpretations, relational quantum mechanics, and the epistemology of physics. Its strongest influence is likely among a community of "heterodox" physicists and philosophers (like those in the "ontological interpretation" or "Bohmian mechanics" camps) who share its dissatisfaction with the Copenhagen orthodoxy. In a broader intellectual sense, the paper serves as a powerful exemplar of the "cargo [[feynman-1974-cargo-cult-science|cult science]]" critique applied to a flagship [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] discipline, aligning it with [[feynman-1974-cargo-cult-science|Feynman]]'s skeptical ethos. It enables future work by articulating, with passion and clarity, the precise nature of the foundational dissatisfaction and pointing toward a concrete experimental path to resolve it.

Large language models and other complex ML systems are supreme examples of incredibly flexible, empirically successful "mathematical shells" that can mimic understanding. The question [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]] poses—does their flexibility make them "empty of physical content"?—is a central philosophical question for AI. 3.  **Knowledge Management and Education:** The paper is a masterclass in the distinction between operational knowledge ("technology") and foundational understanding ("theory"). This is a core challenge in knowledge management and education: how to foster deep, transferable understanding rather than mere proficiency with successful but opaque tools and formalisms. [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]] champions the "chromosomes and DNA" level of knowledge over the "growing grapes and breeding horses" level.
4.  **The Hyperflash Ethos:** The paper embodies a spirit of fundamental questioning that is central to intellectual movements seeking to re-examine the foundations of their disciplines. It is a call to step back from the immense practical success of a technology (whether QM or modern computing) to ask if we truly understand what we are working with, and to be brave enough to propose that our foundational models might be "epicycles."

## Key Quotes

1.  > "A mathematical system that is flexible enough to represent any phenomenology, is empty of physical content. Indeed, the real content of any physical theory lies precisely in the constraints that it imposes on phenomena; the stronger the constraints, the more cogent and useful the theory."
    *   **Analysis:** This is the paper's philosophical core. It defines a "theory" not by its predictive power, but by its explanatory constraint. It attacks the idea that empirical success alone validates a theoretical interpretation.

2.  > "Because of their empirical origins, QM and QED are not physical theories at all. In contrast, Newtonian celestial mechanics, Relativity, and Mendelian genetics are physical theories, because their mathematics was developed by reasoning out the consequences of clearly stated physical principles which constrained the possibilities."
    *   **Analysis:** A bold and categorizing claim. It separates the history of QM (bottom-up, empirical) from that of other great theories (top-down, principled), arguing this methodological difference leads to a fundamental deficiency in QM's conceptual clarity.

3.  > "Those who have not perceived this have pointed to its empirical success to justify a claim that all phenomena must be described in terms of Hilbert spaces, energy levels, etc. This claim... have captured the minds of physicists for over sixty years."
    *   **Analysis:** This directly challenges the dogma that the success of the formalism proves its necessity for describing reality. It frames the dominance of the Copenhagen interpretation as a sociological phenomenon, a captured mindset, rather than an inevitable logical conclusion.

4.  > "In spite of the fact that the experimenter can measure details about individual decays that the theory cannot predict, those who speculate about the deeper immediate cause of each individual decay are considered incompetent, and the currently taught physical interpretation claims that QM is already a complete description of reality."
    *   **Analysis:** Highlights the "schizoid" contradiction [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]] identifies: the theory admits practical incompleteness while dogmatically asserting logical completeness, and it actively suppresses the search for deeper causes that a physicist ought to pursue.

5.  > "The trouble is that [Bohr's epistemological project] is far from the only legitimate goal of physics; yet for 60 years Bohr's teachings have been perverted into attempts to deprecate and discourage any further thinking aimed at finding the causes underlying microphenomena."
    *   **Analysis:** Shows [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]]' nuanced understanding of history. He defends Bohr's original, more modest project while condemning its later institutionalized transformation into an anti-realist orthodoxy that stifles inquiry.
