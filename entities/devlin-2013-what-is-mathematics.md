# Devlin 2013 - What is mathematics

## Core Thesis
Keith Devlin argues that the common understanding of mathematics, as taught in schools and held by the general public, is fundamentally outdated and incomplete. The thesis is not merely that curricula are behind the times, but that they perpetuate a conception of mathematics centered on calculation and procedure (the "how"), while obscuring its modern, revolutionary nature as a discipline focused on understanding abstract structures and patterns (the "why" and "what"). The nuance lies in framing this as a historical accident and a critical failure of communication. The true essence of modern mathematics, he contends, is the study of abstract patterns using a specialized language of symbols. This study shifted from a "doing" to an "understanding" in the 19th century, a revolution invisible to the outside world but which now underpins most of modern science, engineering, and computing. The paper is a polemic for re-educating the public, especially those in technical fields, about this conceptual reality.

## Historical Context
Devlin situates his argument against a background of two divergent histories: the public's perception of math and the actual evolution of the discipline. The public view is frozen in time, largely reflecting developments up to the 17th century (Euclidean geometry, algebra, calculus). This is the math of "mathematically rich subjects" taught to engineers and computer scientists before they enter the modern research world. Meanwhile, the field itself underwent a profound crisis and transformation in the 19th century. Mathematicians encountered counter-intuitive results (like the Banach-Tarski paradox) that their calculation-based methods could not explain. This forced an "introspection" where mathematics turned its methods on itself, moving away from formulaic manipulation toward logical deduction from abstract concepts. This period, pioneered by figures like Dirichlet, Dedekind, and Riemann, birthed modern mathematical thought but remained unknown outside academia. Devlin writes against the 2013 backdrop of a world utterly dependent on this modern mathematics (via computers, cryptography, modeling) while being educated in its ancient ancestor.

## Key Contributions
This background reading, part of an introductory course, introduces several foundational ideas for a modern understanding of mathematics:
1.  **Mathematics as the Science of Patterns:** This is the paper's central, defining contribution. It provides a unified, contemporary framework that absorbs arithmetic, geometry, calculus, logic, probability, topology, and fractals under a single, compelling idea: the identification and analysis of abstract patterns (numerical, spatial, dynamic, logical, etc.).
2.  **The Critical Distinction Between Notation and Essence:** Devlin carefully disentangles mathematical notation from mathematics itself, using the analogy of sheet music versus a performance. This is a crucial pedagogical move to demystify the intimidating symbolic language and re-center understanding on the abstract relationships it represents.
3.  **The 19th-Century Conceptual Revolution:** He explicitly defines a historical pivot point—a shift from "doing" to "understanding," from calculation to conceptual deduction. This provides a clear narrative for why college mathematics feels so different from high school math, framing it not as an arbitrary jump in difficulty but as an entry into a different *kind* of intellectual activity.
4.  **The Pervasiveness and Invisibility of Modern Math:** By citing the NSA as the largest employer of math PhDs and invoking Galileo and Newton, Devlin argues that modern mathematics makes the "invisible visible"—the fundamental forces, data patterns, and logical structures of the universe that are imperceptible without its lens.

## Methodology
The argument is structured as a pedagogical narrative and a historical corrective. Devlin employs:
*   **Analogy:** Soccer for the big picture vs. maneuvers; maps for geographic description vs. text; sheet music for notation vs. music. These serve to bridge the abstract concepts to intuitive understanding.
*   **Historical Scaffolding:** The paper moves chronologically from ancient origins to the modern pattern-based definition, highlighting key breaks and developments (Greek proof, 19th-century crisis). This grounds the abstract definition in a tangible story of human intellectual progress.
*   **Polemical Contrast:** The core rhetorical device is contrasting the outdated, public/educational view with the contemporary, professional view. This is done through specific examples (the NSA, the Banach-Tarski paradox) and a direct "blaming" of historical figures for the conceptual shift that bewilders modern students.
*   **Conceptual Framing:** Rather than proving a theorem, the paper's goal is to re-frame perception. It is an exercise in philosophical positioning, defining the scope and nature of a discipline.

## Influence
As a foundational reading for an online course (and likely a MOOC), its influence is primarily pedagogical and aimed at a broad, non-specialist audience. Its impact is in the lineage of efforts to redefine mathematical literacy.
*   It directly enables a shift in teaching from "how to calculate" to "how to think abstractly," which is the core mission of Devlin's own course on mathematical thinking.
*   It popularizes the "science of patterns" meme, which has become a standard alternative definition in many educational contexts, influencing how teachers and communicators introduce the subject.
*   It contributes to the broader "mathematical reform" conversation, aligning with arguments that seek to emphasize reasoning, proof, and abstraction over rote procedure. In this, it is a key modern voice in a lineage that includes earlier reform efforts.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
Devlin's work resonates deeply with several other thinkers in the WorryDream collection, all of whom grapple with abstraction, representation, and the human mind.
*   **Lockhart (2002, *A Mathematician's Lament*):** The most direct parallel. Lockhart's savage critique of math education as a "soul-crushing" focus on procedures without beauty or meaning is the emotional, polemical counterpart to Devlin's more historical and explanatory argument. Both demand a return to mathematics as a creative, humanistic endeavor.
*   **Thurston (1994, *Proof and Progress*):** Provides a deeper, first-person account of the "conceptual understanding" Devlin describes. Thurston details how mathematicians perceive and think about geometric structures, moving far beyond symbolic manipulation, and argues that mathematics is the *science* of understanding patterns, where "proof is the primary tool for achieving understanding."
*   **Bush (1945, *As We May Think*) & Engelbart (1962, *Augmenting Human Intellect*):** While focused on information machinery, Bush and Engelbart are concerned with amplifying human thought. Devlin's "modern mathematics" can be seen as one of the ultimate tools for augmenting intellect—a formal language for manipulating the abstract patterns of reality. The "invisible" forces of Newton that mathematics makes visible are exactly the kind of conceptual objects one would "augment" their intellect to reason about.
*   **Kay (1972, *Personal Computer*):** Kay's vision of the computer as a "medium for thought" aligns with Devlin's view of mathematical notation as a language for thought. Both see these symbolic systems not as ends in themselves but as means to explore and model complex ideas, with the ultimate "performance" happening in the mind.
*   **Papert (1980, *Mindstorms*):** Papert's constructionist learning philosophy, where children learn deep concepts by building and debugging computational models, is a practical embodiment of Devlin's ideal. Programming (like Logo) lets learners engage directly with the logical and dynamic patterns (control flow, recursion) that constitute a core part of modern mathematics.

## Modern Relevance
Devlin's analysis is acutely relevant to the contemporary landscape of AI, computing, and knowledge work.
*   **AI and Machine Learning:** The core of modern AI is precisely the identification of abstract patterns from data (Devlin's definition of mathematics) and the formulation of them as computable models. Devlin's historical shift from calculation to understanding mirrors the field's shift from hand-coded rules to learned representations.
*   **Computing:** All computer science is applied mathematics in the pattern-based sense. Algorithms are patterns of logical steps; data structures are patterns of organization; type theory is a formal system for patterns of computation. Devlin's paper helps explain why theoretical computer science is a branch of mathematics and why a "computational thinking" education is essentially a mathematical one.
*   **Knowledge Management & Hyperflash's Work:** The challenge of modern knowledge work is not information scarcity but pattern recognition and abstraction in vast datasets. Devlin's framework provides a lens for understanding the goal of tools that assist in this: to help humans "see" the invisible patterns (in business data, scientific literature, system logs) that constitute meaningful knowledge. The tools themselves are built upon the very mathematical patterns Devlin describes.
*   **Education:** The paper remains a manifesto for updating STEM education. In an era where computational tools handle all calculation, the human role must shift to the conceptual work Devlin champions: formulating problems in abstract terms, identifying relevant patterns, and understanding the logical implications.

## Key Quotes
1.  **"For all the time schools devote to the teaching of mathematics, very little (if any) is spent trying to convey just what the subject is about. Instead, the focus is on learning and applying various procedures to solve math problems. That’s a bit like explaining soccer by saying it is executing a series of maneuvers to get the ball into the goal."**
    *   *Analysis: This opening establishes the core problem. It uses a powerful analogy to indict the procedural focus of education, framing it as a failure to communicate essence, not just a failure to teach advanced topics.*

2.  **"Today, between sixty and seventy distinct categories [of mathematics] would be a reasonable figure. Some subjects, like algebra or topology, have split into various subfields; others, such as complexity theory or dynamical systems theory, are completely new areas of study."**
    *   *Analysis: This quantifies the "explosion" of modern mathematics, providing concrete evidence that the field studied in schools is a tiny, static fragment of a vast, evolving landscape.*

3.  **"Mathematics is essential to our understanding the invisible patterns of the universe... mathematics makes the invisible visible."**
    *   *Analysis: This is Devlin's most potent and quotable claim. It elevates mathematics from a tool for calculation to a fundamental lens for perception, akin to a telescope or microscope, but for abstract structure.*

4.  **"The introduction of symbolic mathematics... is generally credited to the French mathematician Françoise Viète... Mathematical notation no more is mathematics than musical notation is music."**
    *   *Analysis: This cleanly separates the representational system from the discipline itself, a critical step in demystifying math for learners and combating the symbol-shock that alienates many.*

5.  **"It became clear, then, that mathematics can lead to realms where the only understanding is through the mathematics itself... mathematicians turned the methods of mathematics inwards, and used them to examine the subject itself."**
    *   *Analysis: This succinctly describes the 19th-century crisis and the birth of modern, foundational mathematics. It explains the origin of the "new math" that is the conceptual basis for all advanced technical fields today.*

6.  **"This was a shift in emphasis from doing to understanding. Mathematical objects were no longer thought of as given primarily by formulas, but rather as carriers of conceptual properties. Proving something was no longer a matter of transforming terms in accordance with rules, but a process of logical deduction from concepts."**
    *   *Analysis: This defines the revolution at the heart of Devlin's thesis. It draws a sharp, pedagogical line between the ancient and modern conceptions of mathematics, providing the key to understanding the "abstraction cliff" in education.*
