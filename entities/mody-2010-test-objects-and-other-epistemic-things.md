# Mody 2010 - Test objects and other epistemic things

## Core Thesis
The paper argues that a specific class of research objects, exemplified by the silicon (111) 7x7 surface reconstruction, plays a foundational yet under-examined role in scientific practice. These "test objects" possess a dual, fluid identity: they are simultaneously **epistemic things** (objects of unknown structure that motivate discovery) and **technical objects** (calibration standards and tools for testing instruments and training observers). The authors contend that this duality is not a flaw but the essential mechanism through which fields like surface science mature, form communities, and develop their distinctive material culture. The Si(111) 7x7's history demonstrates that the boundary between an instrument's "conditions of observation" and its "objects of reference" is unstable and productive. The thesis challenges purely representationalist accounts of scientific imaging and observation, proposing instead that objects like the 7x7 act as a "matrix" that shapes and is shaped by the entire experimental system.

## Historical Context
The paper is situated within the history of surface science and the broader nanotechnology movement of the late 20th and early 21st centuries. Before the development of the scanning tunneling microscope (STM) in the early 1980s, surface scientists had indirect, diffraction-based techniques (like LEED) to infer surface structures. The 7x7 reconstruction on silicon was a known puzzle; its atomic arrangement was contested among several models. The problem was one of **visualization and consensus**. The STM promised direct, real-space imaging of surfaces, but it needed a compelling, high-stakes subject to prove its worth and establish a user community. The 7x7, as a complex, controversial, and widely studied object, was the perfect candidate. Its successful imaging by Binnig and Rohrer in 1985 provided the "killer app" that launched the STM and, by extension, the modern field of scanning probe microscopy. The paper also engages with the historical sociology of science, responding to the tendency in Science and Technology Studies (STS) to either collapse science and technology into "technoscience" or treat them as separate domains. Mody and Lynch use the 7x7's biography to argue for a more nuanced, practice-based understanding of how scientific objects stabilize.

## Key Contributions
1.  **The Concept of the "Test Object"**: The paper formally introduces and defines a new category of epistemic artifact—the test object—that bridges Rheinberger's distinction between "epistemic things" and "technical objects."
2.  **A Biography of a Non-Biological Standard**: It provides a detailed historical account of a non-living material standard, tracing its shifting roles from research puzzle to calibration tool to community icon across decades.
3.  **A Critique of Representationalism in Imaging**: It challenges the notion that probe microscope images are mere "representations" of pre-existing atomic configurations. Instead, the image of the 7x7 is co-produced by the instrument and the interpretive framework, which the object itself helps to establish.
4.  **Model for Material Culture Studies in Science**: The paper exemplifies how tracking the life of a single, seemingly mundane object can map the formation of research communities, the negotiation of disciplinary boundaries, and the material practices that constitute a field.
5.  **Integration of STS Theory and Empirical History**: It successfully applies Rheinberger's sophisticated philosophy of experimental systems to a concrete historical case, revealing its strengths and necessary complications.

## Methodology
The methodology is **historical and sociological**, grounded in the field of Science and Technology Studies (STS). It is primarily **empirical**, based on:
*   **Historical Analysis**: Archival research and analysis of scientific publications tracing the 7x7's role from the 1950s through the 2000s.
*   **Interviews**: The authors conducted interviews with key figures in surface science and scanning probe microscopy to understand the *emic* (insider) perspectives on the object's utility and significance.
*   **Theoretical Framing**: The analysis is structured around a dialogue with Hans-Jörg Rheinberger's theory of experimental systems, using the case study to test and refine his concepts.
*   **Ethnographic Sensibility**: The paper pays close attention to the "material culture of the laboratory," examining how the object was made, used, discussed, and taught as part of everyday practice.
The argument is not polemical or design fiction; it is a careful, interpretive reconstruction intended to illuminate the material epistemology of a specific scientific community.

## Influence
This paper has become a key text in several intersecting literatures:
1.  **History and Philosophy of Instrumentation**: It is frequently cited in discussions about how instruments and their calibration standards co-evolve, moving beyond a simplistic tool-user model.
2.  **Sociology of Scientific Knowledge (SSK)**: It provides a rich case study for understanding how scientific facts and objects are stabilized through community practice and the use of standards.
3.  **Nanoethics and Societal Reflection on Nanotechnology**: By detailing the long, contingent history of a foundational nano-object, it counters hype-driven narratives that present nanotechnology as a sudden revolution, offering a more nuanced "history of the near future."
4.  **General STS and Historical Method**: It is often cited as an exemplary "biography of an object" study, demonstrating the heuristic power of following a thing to understand a field. It has influenced subsequent work on other test objects, from biological samples (like HeLa cells) to computational benchmarks.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
The paper's themes resonate deeply with several other works in the collection, particularly regarding the relationship between understanding, tools, and material artifacts.

*   **Thurston 1994 (Proof and Progress)**: This is the most direct and important connection. Thurston discusses how mathematical understanding is not transmitted through formal proofs alone but through a complex material and social culture of explanations, images, and intuitions. The Si(111) 7x7 serves a similar function in surface science: it is a **materialized intuition**. It is the shared, tangible reference point that allows diverse techniques (STM, LEED, modeling) to be harmonized and that trains new practitioners in "seeing" surfaces correctly, much as Thurston's geometric explanations train mathematicians in mathematical perception.
*   **Papert 1980 (Mindstorms)**: Papert's constructionism emphasizes learning through the creation of tangible, manipulable artifacts. The test object is a professional analogue: surface scientists "learn" the field and demonstrate competence by mastering the production and interpretation of the 7x7. It is a core construction for entering the community.
*   **Feynman 1974 (Cargo Cult Science)**: Feynman warns against the superficial imitation of scientific form without the substance of intellectual honesty. The 7x7 test object guards against this. Using a well-characterized standard to calibrate instruments and train users is a bulwark against "cargo cult" imaging—producing pictures that look like atomic resolution but lack rigorous connection to physical reality.
*   **Lockhart 2002 (Mathematician's Lament)**: Lockhart laments the divorce of mathematics from its creative, problem-solving heart in favor of rote procedure. The duality of the 7x7 illustrates this tension. It is simultaneously a **routine procedure** (making the sample, calibrating the scope) and a **site of mystery and discovery**. A healthy field, the paper implies, needs both aspects to be woven together, not separated.
*   **Bush 1945 (As We May Think) & Engelbart 1962 (Augmenting Human Intellect)**: Both envision tools that augment human cognitive capabilities. The scanning tunneling microscope, with the 7x7 as its first key subject, is a profound augmentation—it extends human perception to the atomic scale. However, Mody and Lynch's analysis shows this augmentation is not just perceptual but **epistemic**: the tool, calibrated by the test object, structures what can be known and questioned.

## Modern Relevance
The concept of the "test object" is acutely relevant to contemporary technology and AI:
1.  **AI Benchmarks as Epistemic Objects**: In machine learning, benchmarks like ImageNet or GLUE datasets function exactly as test objects. They are "known" objects used to test and calibrate new models (technical objects), but they also become problematic "epistemic things" that drive research forward (e.g., when models start to "overfit" the benchmark, it reveals new questions about generalization and bias). The lifecycle and community-forming role of the 7x7 is mirrored in the creation and evolution of AI benchmarks.
2.  **Reproducibility Crisis**: The paper underscores that rigorous science relies on a shared material culture of well-characterized standards. The modern reproducibility crisis in many fields can be analyzed as a breakdown in the stable, shared "test objects" and procedures that allow results to be harmonized and trusted.
3.  **Education and Skill Transmission**: The 7x7 as a training tool highlights that expertise is often tacit and material. This connects to debates in HCI and education about how to effectively teach the use of complex interfaces and interpretive displays, from CAD software to data visualization tools.
4.  **The Nature of "Seeing" in Digital Interfaces**: The analysis of how STM images are *made* through a combination of physical interaction and interpretive frameworks is directly relevant to understanding user interfaces. What does a user "see" in a complex dashboard or a VR environment? It is a co-production of the system's data model and the user's trained perception, mediated by a shared, often implicit, "test object" (a canonical scenario or dataset).

## Key Quotes
1.  > "Depending upon the case, it can have variable shadings of practical, mathematical and epistemic significance. Clear cases of test objects have highly regular and reproducible visible properties that can be used for testing instruments and training novices."
    *   **Analysis**: This defines the test object's core pragmatic function. It emphasizes utility and community-building over pure discovery, framing science as a craft with shared standards.

2. > "The 7x7 was valued because, in Rumsfeldian terms, it was sometimes a ‘known known’ (a material standard...) and sometimes a ‘known unknown’ (an unsolved problem...)."
    *   **Analysis**: This pithy formulation captures the essential duality. The object's value lies in this oscillation between being a reliable tool and a mysterious problem, ensuring sustained community engagement.

3. > "Our ‘test objects’ complement, but also complicate, Hans-Jörg Rheinberger’s analysis of ‘experimental systems’... We see the 7x7 as shifting back and forth over time and in different research contexts between being a problematic object of investigation (an epistemic thing) and a standard matrix with which investigation is performed, calibrated or tested (a technical object)."
    *   **Analysis**: This is the theoretical heart of the paper. It respectfully challenges a leading framework in philosophy of science, arguing for more fluidity between its core categories based on historical practice.

4. > "The question whether visualization with the STM and related instruments is a matter of ‘seeing’ is a vexed one, not only for philosophers of science but also in popular accounts."
    *   **Analysis**: This opens a critical line of analysis. The paper later argues that seeing with a probe microscope is not passive observation but an active, haptic exploration guided by theoretical expectations that the test object helps to establish. It undermines naive realism about scientific imaging.

5. > "The Si(111) 7x7 reconstruction thus became a ‘focus object’... around which a research community precipitated, and through which the community's instruments, techniques, and standards were stabilized."
    *   **Analysis**: This states the macro-historical conclusion. The object is not just a passive focus but an active agent in community formation and disciplinary stabilization, a "focus object" in both optical and sociological senses.
