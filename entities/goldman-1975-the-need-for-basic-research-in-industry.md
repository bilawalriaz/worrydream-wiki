# Goldman 1975 - The need for basic research in industry

## Core Thesis
Goldman's central argument is a prescriptive warning and a strategic blueprint: U.S. industrial corporations must drastically increase their own investment in basic research to compensate for a severe, ongoing withdrawal of federal funding, and to navigate a fundamental shift in national R&D priorities. The paper argues this is not merely beneficial but existentially necessary. The nuance lies in his layered justification: basic research is not an abstract good, but a concrete corporate asset for **strategic intelligence** (knowing what rivals are doing), **innovation advantage**, **talent acquisition**, and **diversification**. He contends that the new national focus on "relevance" (energy, environment, health) instead of clearly defined missions (defense, space) has created an "identity problem" that only mission-oriented industry, with its full research-to-product pipeline, is structurally equipped to solve. The paper is both a defense of pure science and a cold-eyed business case for it.

## Historical Context
The paper emerges from a specific inflection point in American science policy. The post-WWII "Golden Age" of science, characterized by lavish and relatively unconstrained federal funding via military agencies (ONR, AFOSR) and NASA, was ending. The Vietnam War, the winding down of the Apollo program, and the 1973 oil crisis precipitated a retrenchment. The **Mansfield Amendment** (1970), requiring Department of Defense research to have a "direct and apparent relationship to a specific military function," was a concrete symbol of this shift toward applied, mission-driven research. The new dominant national crises—energy independence, environmental protection, urban decay, healthcare—were complex, diffuse, and lacked a single, technologically focused "customer" agency like NASA. Goldman situates his argument in this vacuum of leadership and support, where traditional supporters (military, NASA) were receding and new agencies (like ERDA) lacked the integrated structure to couple basic science to ultimate application.

## Key Contributions
1.  **The Four Pillars of Corporate Research:** Goldman crystallized four enduring justifications for industrial basic research: (1) **Defensive Intelligence:** Being part of the scientific community to avoid technological surprise. (2) **Innovation Engine:** Creating the probability of leading innovation, not just following it. (3) **Talent Pipeline:** A premier recruiting tool for attracting top technical and managerial minds. (4) **Diversification Seedbed:** Research into new fields creates options for business diversification that are hard to justify on short-term, relevance-based terms.
2.  **Diagnosis of the "Identity Problem":** He diagnosed a core mismatch between the nature of post-industrial societal problems and the structure of research support. Unlike the clear, bounded missions of defense and space, issues like energy conservation or urban systems lacked a single agency that was both sponsor and end-user. This decoupling of research from application made justifying basic research difficult for government agencies now preaching "relevance."
3.  **The Quantitative Alarm:** He provided stark, constant-dollar statistics to frame the crisis: a 36% decrease in federal support for basic research in industry over the prior decade. This quantified the "shortfall" he argued industry must fill.
4.  **Industry as the Necessary "Coupling Agent":** He argued that in the new landscape, industry itself is the only actor capable of effectively coupling basic research to complex, applied outcomes because it uniquely integrates the entire spectrum from discovery to product in a mission-oriented way.

## Methodology
Goldman's methodology is **polemical and analytical**, not empirical. He builds his case through:
*   **Repetition with Development:** He revisits and expands upon a 1964 speech, using the continuity of his core argument to highlight the dramatic environmental shift.
*   **Strategic Analogy:** He contrasts the well-defined, customer-integrated R&D models of NASA or Bell Labs (for solid-state physics) with the fragmented, "too loose" structure of agencies like ERDA, to illustrate the systemic "identity problem."
*   **Data as Weapon:** He deploys specific, devastating statistics (the 36% drop, the shrinkage of industry's own real R&D spend) to move from philosophical argument to urgent business reality.
*   **Historical Anecdote as Proof:** He quotes his own 1956 speech predicting the auto industry's need for a "radical" energy breakthrough to establish his foresight and underscore the long-term consequences of neglecting basic research.
*   **Appeal to Self-Interest:** He frames the entire argument in terms of corporate survival, competitive advantage, and management needs, speaking the language of his audience (industrial managers and scientists).

## Influence
While a trade-magazine article (*Physics Today*), this paper is an influential artifact of its time, capturing the anxieties of the industrial research establishment. Its direct influence is less in citation and more in **articulating and legitimizing a corporate R&D strategy** that became pervasive. The arguments Goldman makes—research as talent magnet, as defensive intelligence, as diversification engine—became foundational for corporate labs at Xerox, IBM, Bell Labs, and later, tech giants. It provides a historical blueprint for the corporate research arm that seeks to do "basic" work while justifying its budget to skeptical finance departments. It anticipates the later "DARPA for industry" models and the rise of corporate venture capital with an R&D ethos. In the history of science policy, it is a key document of the "post-golden age" malaise and the pivot toward industry-funded innovation.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
*   **Vannevar Bush 1945 (As We May Think):** Bush outlined the government's role as funder of the "endless frontier." Goldman chronicles the breakdown of that model and the consequent burden falling on industry. Bush's optimism about government-science partnership is met with Goldman's pragmatism about its limits and shifts.
*   **Douglas Engelbart 1962 (Augmenting Human Intellect):** Engelbart focused on augmenting individual capability. Goldman is concerned with augmenting *institutional* capability—the corporation's ability to sense, innovate, and adapt. Both see research as a tool for fundamental human/organizational enhancement.
*   **J.C.R. Licklider 1962 (Man-Computer Symbiosis):** Licklider foresaw a new symbiosis. Goldman's argument for why communications and computing industries are "most advanced" because they "do their own research" speaks directly to the kind of long-term, vision-driven R&D that enabled the digital symbiosis.
*   **Richard Feynman 1974 (Cargo Cult Science):** Feynman attacks research that mimics the form of science without its integrity. Goldman's defense of basic research is, in part, a defense of the authentic, curiosity-driven work that cargo cultism parodies. Both are concerned with the vitality of the scientific enterprise itself.
*   **Phillip Anderson 1972 (More is Different):** Anderson argues for the importance of basic, disciplinary science at all levels. Goldman provides the economic and strategic rationale for funding that very "basic" science within an industrial context, complementing Anderson's philosophical argument.
*   **Modern Connections:** The paper directly anticipates debates about corporate AI labs (e.g., Google Brain, DeepMind) doing fundamental research versus applied product work. Goldman's "admission ticket" metaphor is exactly the logic used to justify such labs. It also prefigures the "innovation ecosystem" models where universities, government, and industry play defined, interconnected roles.

## Modern Relevance
Goldman's analysis is startlingly relevant to the 2020s.
1.  **The "Relevance" Doctrine Today:** The current push for "responsible AI," climate-tech, and healthcare innovation creates the same "identity problem" he described. Basic research in machine learning or material science is often pressured to demonstrate near-term, specific applications, mirroring the 1970s energy/health push.
2.  **Corporate R&D as Strategic Asset:** The paper is a perfect historical lens for understanding today's corporate AI labs. Companies fund fundamental research (e.g., on transformer architectures, reinforcement learning) for precisely Goldman's reasons: to attract top talent (the "admission ticket"), to avoid being surprised by a competitor (like OpenAI vs. Google Brain), and to seed future, as-yet-unknown product lines (diversification).
3.  **The Public-Private R&D Balance:** The 36% drop in federal support he cites has, in many fields (AI, biotech, quantum computing), been countered or even surpassed by private capital. Goldman argued industry *must* make up the shortfall; today, it has arguably *dominated* the funding landscape in certain frontiers, raising new questions about research direction and public good that he did not foresee.
4.  **The Talent War:** His point about research as the best source of top management is visibly true in Silicon Valley, where PhDs routinely move into high-level product and executive roles. The research lab remains the premier training ground for technical leadership.

## Key Quotes

1.  **"If you are to tap the world's science and technology, you have to create some science. Your admission ticket to the club is to have something of your own to talk about."**
    *   *Analysis:* This is Goldman's most pithy and enduring metaphor. It frames basic research not as charity, but as the price of admission to the global network of knowledge that drives innovation. It reduces a complex strategy to a simple, compelling imperative for engagement.

2.  **"The oil prospects for the world are so very dim that this largest of all American industries must have an important, original, inspired breakthrough sometime within the next 25 years... for by then, we shall have to kiss goodbye to any means of [economic] locomotion which requires for its use the internal combustion of fossil fuels."**
    *   *Analysis:* This 1956 quote, repeated for emphasis, demonstrates the predictive power of long-term basic research thinking. It highlights the auto industry's failure to heed this kind of foresight, which Goldman uses as a cautionary tale versus the success of the "research-active" communications industry.

3.  **"Federally supported basic research in industry has decreased by 36% in constant dollars."**
    *   *Analysis:* This is the paper's quantitative fulcrum. The statistic is not just data; it's an indictment and a call to arms. It transforms the argument from a philosophical preference into a response to a measurable, ongoing crisis.

4.  **"How do you turn loose a scientific community that is inherently discipline-oriented to worry about the infusion of science into a technology for transportation, for urban waste disposal, for energy conservation and so on, but for the application of which the sponsor agency has no responsibility?"**
    *   *Analysis:* This question perfectly encapsulates the "identity problem." It identifies the structural disconnect between how scientists work (discipline-focused) and how complex societal problems are managed (multi-agency, diffuse). It argues that only industry, with its end-to-end mission orientation, can bridge this gap.

5.  **"I suggest that basic research in the industrial scheme of things has a specific mission to perform: that of keeping the company honest—in the poker sense."**
    *   *Analysis:* This quote reveals the deeply strategic, almost adversarial, value of research. "Keeping the company honest" means preventing strategic complacency and technological blindness. It frames research as a guardian against corporate hubris and a vital source of market and technological intelligence.
