# Cohn 2012 - Comics, Linguistics, and Visual Language

## Core Thesis
Neil Cohn’s central argument is a decisive reframing: **comics are not a language, but a medium written in a *visual language*.** This distinction is crucial. Prior scholarship and practitioner intuition often conflated the two, leading to superficial analogies (e.g., panels = words). Cohn posits that the sequential images in comics constitute a *language* in a cognitive-linguistic sense—a structured, rule-governed system for encoding and communicating meaning. Comics, like novels or films, are a *social artifact* that employs this language. The paper argues that only by isolating the visual language system itself from its cultural use in "comics" can linguistics perform a rigorous, cognitive analysis of its structure (phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics), and connect it to verbal and sign languages. The thesis is thus a methodological and ontological shift: stop studying the artifact and start studying the underlying expressive system.

## Historical Context
The field Cohn surveys was fragmented and pre-theoretical. Two streams existed:
1.  **European Structuralism/Semiology (1970s onward):** Researchers like Koch, Gubern, and Groensteen applied semiological frameworks to dissect comics into minimal units (logemes, morphemes, syntactemes). This work was taxonomic and code-focused but lacked a unifying cognitive theory of language processing.
2.  **Post-McCloud Cognitivism (1993 onward):** Scott McCloud’s *Understanding Comics* became a dominant paradigm, introducing "closure" and panel transitions. While hugely influential, McCloud’s analogies were often loose and his cognitive claims were more speculative than rigorously linguistic. Subsequent studies (Saraceni, Bridgeman, Lim) largely operated within McCloud's framework, testing his categories against discourse theory or multimodal analysis.

The problem Cohn identifies is a lack of synthesis. These works used linguistic *tools* but were not grounded in a comprehensive theory of *language as a cognitive system*. They treated comics as a monolithic entity rather than as a medium expressing a distinct modality of language. This left the field without a clear path forward or a way to formally connect visual grammar to verbal grammar.

## Key Contributions
Cohn’s paper provides a critical survey and a blueprint for a new research program. Its key contributions are:
1.  **Conceptual Clarification:** The pivotal distinction between the *object* (comics) and the *system* (visual language). This liberates analysis from the cultural constraints of "comics" to study any sequential image system.
2.  **A Unified Cognitive-Linguistic Framework:** He proposes that the fundamental questions of linguistics (how form is organized, how meaning is conveyed, how sequences are processed, how systems are learned) are fully applicable to the visual language of comics. This elevates the study from analogy to a parallel modality of human language capacity.
3.  **A Critical Taxonomy of Past Work:** Cohn provides the first comprehensive survey of linguistic approaches to comics, categorizing them by paradigm (structuralist, cognitive, generative). He critically notes their shared failing: a lack of unifying theory. He correctly identifies most as using comics as *support* for verbal linguistic theories, not developing a theory *of the visual system itself*.
4.  **A Path Toward a "Unified Theory":** While the truncated paper doesn't fully detail it, the introduction points toward a theory that connects graphic expression to verbal and manual (sign) modalities. This is his most ambitious claim: that visual language is not a metaphor but a core, comparable component of human linguistics, requiring the same rigor applied to spoken or signed languages.

## Methodology
The argument is primarily **theoretical and polemical**, not empirical. Cohn’s method is:
1.  **Historicist and Critical Review:** He surveys the literature to diagnose its state, showing what has been tried and identifying its conceptual shortcomings.
2.  **Ontological Argument:** He makes a forceful distinction between object and system, using analogies from established linguistics (e.g., studying "English" vs. studying "novels").
3.  **Framework Proposing:** He outlines a set of research questions drawn from core linguistic subfields and argues they apply equally to visual expression. This is a methodological prescription for a new field.
4.  **Integration with Cognitive Science:** He grounds his call in cognitive linguistics, seeking the cognitive mechanisms for comprehension rather than just cultural or textual analysis.

The paper is not a report on new data but a manifesto for reorienting an existing field. Its strength lies in its clear, systematic critique and the clarity of its proposed alternative framework.

## Influence
This paper is a foundational text for the emerging field of **Visual Language Theory (VLT)**. It is widely cited by researchers applying rigorous linguistic methods to sequential images, including those in comics, animation, and pictorial narratives. It directly enabled:
-   **A Research Program:** Cohn's own subsequent empirical work (e.g., on the "Visual Narrative ACE" model) which tests hypotheses about the cognitive grammar of visual narratives.
-   **Interdisciplinary Dialogue:** It provided a credible linguistic bridge between comic studies, cognitive science, and HCI, moving the conversation beyond superficial metaphor.
-   **Methodological Rigor:** It set a standard for what a linguistic claim about comics should entail, moving the field past McCloud-era intuition and toward testable, formal models. The paper is a gateway to later work on narrative schemas, visual syntax, and developmental studies of visual language acquisition.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
-   **Douglas Engelbart (1962) & "Augmenting Human Intellect":** Both papers are fundamentally about augmenting human expressiveness and cognition. Engelbart focused on the computer as a tool for thought; Cohn identifies a new *modality* of structured thought (visual language). The connection is in understanding the deep structure of symbolic systems that enable complex communication, whether coded in software or ink.
-   **Seymour Papert (1980) & *Mindstorms*:** Papert argued that programming (LOGO) was a powerful medium for thinking, a "language" for exploring ideas. Cohn argues visual narrative is a linguistic system for meaning-making. Both are concerned with how structured symbolic systems (code, visual language) shape cognition and learning.
-   **Jaron Lanier (2006?) or other VR/UI papers (hypothetical):** Cohn's work is deeply relevant to HCI and interface design. Understanding visual language grammar is essential for designing intuitive symbolic interfaces, icon systems, and spatial narratives in VR/AR. It provides a cognitive basis for why certain visual metaphors work.
-   **Alan Kay (1972) & "A Personal Computer for Children of All Ages":** Kay envisioned the computer as a "medium" for expressive, personal computing. Cohn formalizes a medium (comics/sequential art) as the carrier of a language. Both recognize that powerful cognitive work happens through mastery of a symbolic system, not just a tool.
-   **Richard Feynman (1974) & "Cargo Cult Science":** Cohn could be read as a warning against "cargo cult linguistics" in comics scholarship—using the terminology and analogies of linguistics without the deep, methodological rigor that defines the field. True understanding requires engaging with the core questions of structure, processing, and cognition.

## Modern Relevance
Cohn's framework is profoundly relevant to modern AI and knowledge work:
-   **AI and Multimodal Learning:** Large multimodal models (like GPT-4V) are attempting to "understand" images and sequences. Cohn's theory provides a cognitive-linguistic roadmap for what "understanding" a visual narrative entails: parsing a visual grammar, recognizing narrative schemas, inferring meaning from sequential closure. It argues against purely statistical approaches by positing a structured cognitive system that models might need to emulate.
-   **Knowledge Management and Visual Thinking:** In fields from software engineering (UML, flowcharts) to business strategy (storyboards), visual sequences are used to encode complex processes and narratives. Cohn’s work provides a theoretical basis for analyzing and improving these visual languages, making them more expressive and learnable.
-   **Education:** Understanding visual language acquisition (as distinct from literacy) has implications for teaching visual communication, storytelling, and even mathematics/ science where diagrams are sequential and rule-governed.
-   **Hyperflash and Digital Storytelling:** For any project involving interactive narratives, dynamic visual content, or new forms of digital expression, Cohn's work offers a foundation. It moves beyond the idea of "visuals as illustration" to "visuals as a grammatical, narrative system," enabling more sophisticated design of how stories and information unfold spatially and temporally.

## Key Quotes
1.  > "Comics are a social object that is the result of two human behaviors: writing and drawing. Believing 'comics' are an object of inquiry would be akin to linguists focusing on 'novels' as opposed to studying English, the language that novels are written in."
    *   **Analysis:** This is the core ontological argument. It cleanly separates the artifact from the cognitive system, defining the proper scope of linguistic inquiry.

2.  > "Direct analogies between the verbal and graphic modalities (i.e., like 'words = panels') are less important than the fact that the same questions are being addressed in both the verbal and graphic forms."
    *   **Analysis:** This is a call for deeper analysis. It rejects simplistic one-to-one mappings in favor of a parallel, cognitive-level approach, elevating visual language to a peer of verbal language.

3.  > "Comics themselves are not a language, but comics are written in visual languages the same way that novels or magazines are written in English."
    *   **Analysis:** The definitive restatement of the core thesis, using a perfect analogy that clarifies the object/system relationship for any reader.

4.  > "Few of these disparate works are motivated by a central theory of language, graphic expression, or comics... At their best, these studies use comics as further support for particular linguistic theories... and at their worst, they make equations that 'comics are a language' without grounding such claims with adequate contemporary linguistics knowledge."
    *   **Analysis:** This is the critical diagnosis. It points out the field's lack of synthesis and its failure to develop a native theory of the visual system, relying instead on applying verbal tools superficially.

5.  > "The guiding questions of linguistic inquiry can thus be applied to the study of the visual language that comics are written in: 1. How is the form of the expressive system organized? 2. How is meaning conveyed by a form?..."
    *   **Analysis:** This is the practical blueprint. It translates abstract linguistic theory into a concrete research agenda for visual language, demanding answers to fundamental questions about structure, meaning, and processing.
