# Papert 1990 - Computer Criticism vs. Technocentric Thinking

## Core Thesis
Papert argues that public and academic discourse about computers in education is trapped in a stage he defines as **technocentrism**: the cognitive and cultural bias of viewing technology (like computers or LOGO) as the primary, active agent of change, while people and their cultures are reduced to a secondary, facilitating role. His core proposition is that this bias blocks meaningful understanding and effective integration of computers into learning. To overcome it, he proposes a new genre of discourse: **computer criticism**, modeled on literary or social criticism. This practice would not aim to condemn but to understand, explicate, and place computers within their rich human and cultural contexts. The paper is not a defense of LOGO's efficacy but a meta-critique of how we talk about such efficacy, arguing that questions like "Does LOGO work?" are fundamentally misframed because they ignore the essential role of the human "carpenter" using the tool within a specific cultural workshop.

## Historical Context
The paper emerges from a specific moment in the history of educational technology. By 1990, Seymour Papert’s LOGO programming language had been a centerpiece of the "Logo revolution" in education for over a decade, promising to foster computational thinking, problem-solving skills, and a new kind of constructionist learning. However, a growing body of empirical research, often using randomized controlled trials (the "treatment model"), had produced disappointing or null results regarding LOGO's impact on cognitive skills. A pivotal study by Roy Pea and others, reported in popular press like *Psychology Today* (1984), concluded that "LOGO promises more than it has delivered," framing LOGO as a discrete "treatment" that failed to produce measurable outcomes.

This context placed Papert and the LOGO community on the defensive. The dominant model for educational validation—the controlled experiment—was treating LOGO like a pharmaceutical drug to be tested for a specific biological effect. Papert saw this as a category error. He was responding not just to negative findings, but to the entire epistemological framework that produced them, which he felt was inadequate for evaluating transformative cultural technologies. He was influenced by Piaget's stages of cognitive development, extending the concept of egocentrism to a collective, cultural bias toward technology.

## Key Contributions
1.  **The Concept of Technocentrism:** Papert’s most lasting contribution is this precise term for a pervasive cognitive bias. It diagnoses a failure to distinguish between properties of a tool and properties of the culture that uses it. Technocentrism leads to simplistic, deterministic questions ("What is the effect of the computer?") and misinterprets research results by ignoring the necessary conditions of skill, context, and cultural practice.

2.  **A Call for Computer Criticism:** He proposes a new intellectual discipline aimed at achieving a "textured and knowledgeable discourse" about computers. Analogous to literary criticism, its goal is hermeneutic: to interpret, evaluate, and understand computers as "a medium of human expression." This shifts the object of study from isolated technical effects to the complex interplay of tools, people, and meaning-making.

3.  **A Fundamental Critique of Educational Research Methodology:** While not rejecting controlled experiments outright, Papert rigorously critiques their application to transformative educational technologies. He argues the "treatment model" is only valid for contexts where "change is small, slow, and superficial." For systemic change, it is radically incompatible, as it attempts to hold culture constant while introducing a disruptive cultural element.

4.  **Reframing the "Failure" of LOGO:** Papert reinterprets negative research findings not as evidence against LOGO, but as evidence for the inadequacy of the evaluation framework. A null result in a decontextualized test says more about the experimenters and their limited model of learning than about LOGO's potential. This is a powerful defense of constructionist learning environments against reductionist metrics.

5.  **Analogy to Social Prejudice:** By comparing technocentric stereotypes to sexist or racist ones, Papert elevates the issue from a technical misunderstanding to a deep-seated cultural construct requiring fundamental re-examination of pre-existing assumptions about education itself.

## Methodology
Papert’s argument is primarily **theoretical and polemical**, constructed through analogy, critique, and philosophical reframing. His methodology is that of the critical theorist and intellectual provocateur:

*   **Analogy as Primary Tool:** He builds his case by analogy—computers to wood and hammers, technocentrism to egocentrism, computer criticism to literary criticism. This transfers understood principles from one domain to illuminate the other.
*   **Critique of an Extant Paradigm:** He targets a specific, dominant mode of inquiry (the educational psychology experiment) and demonstrates its philosophical limitations when applied to a certain class of problems.
*   **Reinterpretation of Evidence:** He takes existing, widely-cited "negative" studies (Pea, Hassett) and re-reads them through his proposed lens, showing that their conclusions are artifacts of their technocentric design.
*   **Conceptual Definition and Prescription:** The paper functions by first diagnosing a problem (technocentrism) with a new label, then prescribing a solution (computer criticism) as a new practice.
The structure is discursive, moving from a general theory of criticism, to a specific diagnosis, to a case study of LOGO research, and finally to implications for practice and culture-building.

## Influence
Papert's 1990 paper crystallized and popularized a critique that has had enduring influence in philosophy of technology, educational technology, and media studies.

*   **In Educational Technology:** The concept of "technocentrism" became a standard term of critique, cited to argue against naive solutionism and for more holistic, context-aware technology integration. It bolstered the "learning sciences" approach that studies cognition in context, not in isolation.
*   **In Philosophy of Technology:** The paper aligns with and strengthens social constructivist and cultural shaping of technology (SCOT) theories, which argue that technology's meaning and effects are produced through social processes, not inherent in the artifact.
*   **Direct Influence on Discourse:** It shaped how a generation of educators, researchers, and advocates (like those in the Maker Education movement) framed their work, emphasizing culture, community, and creative expression over skills training or test scores.
*   **Critique of "Tech Fix" Mentality:** Papert's attack on the "technological fix" and the image of "LOGO driving a delivery truck loaded with crates of promises" provided a powerful rhetorical tool for critiquing techno-utopianism in education and beyond. This lineage is visible in later critiques of MOOCs, gamification, and AI tutors that similarly promise to "disrupt" education with a single tool.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
*   **Papert 1980 (Mindstorms):** This is the essential prequel. *Mindstorms* lays out the constructionist philosophy and LOGO's design rationale. "Computer Criticism" is Papert's reflective, defensive response to the cultural reception and mis-evaluation of the ideas from *Mindstorms* a decade later.
*   **[[feynman-1974-cargo-cult-science|Feynman]] 1974 (Cargo Cult Science):** A deep methodological kinship exists here. [[feynman-1974-cargo-cult-science|Feynman]] critiques scientific practice that has the *form* of rigor (peer review, experiments) but lacks its *substance* (intellectual honesty, genuine testing of hypotheses). Papert levels a similar charge at educational research: it has the form of a controlled experiment but lacks the substance of a methodology suited to the problem.
*   **Kay 1972 (Personal Computer):** Kay's vision of computers as "a medium of personal and interpersonal expression" directly anticipates Papert's call for computer criticism. Both see the computer not as a tool for calculation but as a canvas for human creativity and communication, which requires a humanistic mode of analysis.
*   **[[engelbart-1960-games-that-teach-the-fundamentals-of-computer-operation|Engelbart]] 1962 (Augmenting Human Intellect):** Engelbart's "system perspective" (augmenting human capabilities within a system of tools, methods, and training) is the practical antithesis of technocentrism. Papert provides the theoretical critique of why looking at a tool in isolation (the technocentric view) is a failure.
*   **[[bush-1931-the-differential-analyzer|Bush]] 1945 (As We May Think):** Bush's memex is a powerful tool, but his essay is fundamentally about the human *associations* it enables, not its technical specs. This is a non-technocentric vision. Papert would argue that evaluating the memex by its CPU speed would be a technocentric error.
*   **[[lockhart-2002-a-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]] 2002 (Mathematician's Lament):** [[lockhart-2002-a-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]] laments the death of mathematics in education by reducing it to a technical procedure. Papert's lament is parallel: the death of the transformative potential of computers in education by reducing them to a technical "treatment" measured by narrow skills. Both advocate for a return to the creative, aesthetic, and cultural heart of their disciplines.

## Modern Relevance
Papert's analysis is startlingly prescient and directly applicable to current technological discourse:

*   **AI in Education and Work:** The debate around AI (LLMs, tutoring systems) is rife with technocentrism. Questions like "Can AI replace teachers?" or "What is AI's effect on creativity?" treat AI as a monolithic agent. Papert would insist we ask instead: "How are particular AI tools being integrated into specific learning cultures?" and "What new forms of human expression and criticism are made possible when people collaborate with these media?"
*   **The "AI Effect" and Hype Cycles:** Papert's critique explains the pattern where a technology's initial hype (the "promise") is followed by disillusionment when it fails to produce revolutionary results in decontextualized trials. The problem is not the technology's inherent power, but our culturally-bound, simplistic model of how it should "deliver."
*   **Hyperflash & Knowledge Work:** For an organization focused on knowledge tools and "learning in the flow of work," Papert's framework is a vital guardrail. It warns against building tools with a technocentric focus on features and metrics ("our AI boosts productivity by X%") and instead demands a focus on the cultural shifts in how people think, collaborate, and create meaning. True augmentation requires understanding and shaping the culture of use, not just delivering a technical artifact.
*   **Ethical and Social Discourse on Tech:** The call for "computer criticism" is a call for a mature, humanistic field of study for all powerful technologies. It directly prefigures today's interdisciplinary fields of AI ethics, algorithmic fairness, and digital sociology, which aim to "understand, explicate, and place in perspective" technology's social role.

2.  **"The context for human development is always a culture, never an isolated technology. In the presence of computers, cultures might change and with them people's ways of learning and thinking.     *   *Analysis:* This is the prescriptive core. It relocates the unit of analysis from the technology to the human system, arguing that influence and understanding are only possible through this shift in perspective.
