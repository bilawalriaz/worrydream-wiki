# Latour 1986 - Visualisation and Cognition

## Core Thesis
Latour's central argument is that the unique, overwhelming power of modern science and technology cannot be explained by positing a radical difference in human minds, consciousness, or culture (a "Great Divide" between [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] and non-[[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] thought). Instead, he proposes a more mundane, material explanation: the strategic use of novel techniques for **inscription**. The power of science lies not in superior brains, but in the craft of transforming the world into manipulable, combinable, and portable signs on paper (diagrams, lists, equations, images). These inscriptions are not mere illustrations of thought; they are the essential tools that reconfigure cognition itself, enabling new forms of argument, proof, and persuasion. The "cognition" in the title is not about the individual mind, but about the collective, practical activity of "thinking with eyes and hands" via these material supports.

## Historical Context
Latour was writing within the field of Science and Technology Studies (STS), actively challenging two dominant, opposing explanatory frameworks:

1.  **Mentalist/Idealist Explanations:** These attributed the rise of science to shifts in human rationality, consciousness, or intellectual method (e.g., Bachelard's *coupures épistémologiques*, Garfinkel's modes of thought). These required an inexplicable "new man" to emerge.
2.  **Materialist/Structuralist Explanations:** These sought economic or social infrastructure causes (e.g., capitalist modes of production). While "material," Latour found these overly abstract, empirically unverifiable, and ironically dependent on the authority of another science (economics).

The paper emerges from Latour's own ethnographic work in a biology laboratory (*Laboratory Life*, 1979), where he observed the daily practices of scientists. The problem he sought to solve was how to account for the immense gap between the cognitive outcomes of particle physics and folk botany without resorting to mystical or tautological "great divides." He builds on and synthesizes earlier scholarship emphasizing material practice, such as [[goody-1963-the-consequences-of-literacy|Goody]]'s work on lists and graphics, Cole & Scribner's studies on schooling, and Eisenstein's analysis of print culture.

## Key Contributions
1.  **The Concept of "Inscriptions":** Latour formalizes the idea that the core activity of science is the production of *inscriptions*—mobile, reproducible, and combinable graphic entities (papers, figures, graphs). The laboratory's primary output is not "knowledge" in the abstract, but a steady stream of these paper artifacts.
2.  **Cognition as Collective Craftsmanship:** He relocates cognitive power from the individual brain to a distributed process of manipulating external symbols. When scientists have access to their inscriptions, they reason brilliantly; without them, they "stutter, hesitate, and talk nonsense." Thought is offloaded onto and structured by these tools.
3.  **The Agonistic Criterion for Explanation:** He proposes a pragmatic test for which innovations in inscription matter: those that help an author "muster on the spot the largest number of well aligned and faithful allies" in an argument. Innovations in visualization or notation are revolutionary only if they make actors more persuasive and their facts "harder" in public debate.
4.  **The "Circulating Reference":** He traces how a fact (e.g., about rainforests) becomes mobile and accumulates strength as it passes through a chain of transformations (field sketches, maps, formulas, papers), each making it more manipulable but also further from its original context.
5.  **A Deflationary Strategy for Science Studies:** The paper provides a methodological program: to explain complex cognitive or cultural achievements, start by examining the most mundane material practices of recording, visualizing, and argumentation.

## Methodology
The argument is **polemical, theoretical, and synthetic**, grounded in Latour's prior ethnographic observation. Its structure is primarily one of elimination and redefinition:
1.  **Eliminative:** He systematically dismantles "Great Divide" hypotheses (mental, social, economic) as either empirically false or explanatorily bankrupt.
2.  **Synthetic:** He gathers evidence from diverse fields (history, anthropology, education, engineering) to demonstrate the recurring importance of material practices (lists, drawings, diagrams) in shaping thought.
3.  **Theoretical Construction:** He builds his alternative model of "inscription"[[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966| and "agon]]istic" persuasion, using the metaphor of [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] debate as a battle for allies.
4.  **Empirical Anchor:** He grounds the theory in his own detailed ethnography of laboratory work, where the transformation of rats and chemicals into paper traces was the central activity.

The methodology is a classic STS move: shifting analysis from the content of ideas to the *practices* and *materials* that make those ideas possible, arguable, and persuasive.

## Influence
This paper was foundational in consolidating the "material turn" in Science Studies and Anthropology of Knowledge. Its influence is vast:

*   **Within STS:** It became a central text, cited by scholars studying the practices of science, engineering, and medicine. It provided a vocabulary ("inscriptions," "circulating reference") that shaped a generation of research.
*   **On History of Science:** It challenged intellectual history approaches by insisting on the material infrastructure of knowledge-making (e.g., the importance of specific types of diagrams, printing presses, or mathematical notations).
*   **On HCI & Cognitive Science:** It provided a theoretical underpinning for distributed cognition and "extended mind" hypotheses, arguing that human intelligence is inherently coupled with its material tools. It directly informs the study of how interfaces and visualizations shape thought.
[[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|*   **On P]]hilosophy of Science:** It offered a pragmatic, practice-based alternative to debates about [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] realism or social constructivism by focusing on the production of "factish" objects through inscription.
*   **Lineage:** It is a key precursor to Latour's later actor-network theory (ANT), where inscriptions are crucial "immutable mobiles" that allow networks to scale and stabilize.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
*   **[[bush-1945-as-we-may-think-with-engelbart-notes|Bush 1945]] (As We May Think):** [[vannevar-bush-symposium-1995-closing-panel|Bush]]'s Memex is the quintessential imagined machine for manipulating inscriptions. Latour's paper provides the anthropological and cognitive theory for *why* such a machine would augment thought: it scales up the human capacity for associative indexing and visual combination that was already revolutionary in paper-based science.
*   **[[engelbart-1962-augmenting-human-intellect-typeset|Engelbart 1962]] ([[engelbart-1962-augmenting-human-intellect|Augmenting Human]] Intellect):** [[engelbart-2003-improving-our-ability-to-improve|Engelbart]]'s framework is a direct technological implementation of Latour's thesis. His "H-LAM/T" (Human using Language, Artifacts, Methodology, in which he is Trained) system is an explicit attempt to build the material infrastructure for better "augmenting the intellect"—i.e., for more effective inscription and argumentation.
*   **[[thurston-1994-on-proof-and-progress-in-mathematics|Thurston 1994]] (Proof and Progress):** [[thurston-1994-on-proof-and-progress-in-mathematics|Thurston]]'s paper on mathematical understanding is a deep case study in Latour's theory. He describes the [[egan-2003-some-cognitive-tools-of-literacy|cognitive tools]] of the geometer: visual intuition, notation, and the need to communicate ideas in a form (diagrams, prose) that can persuade others. The "communication of understanding" is precisely the agonistic process of mustering allies.
*   **[[papert-1980-mindstorms-1st-ed|Papert 1980]] (Mindstorms):** [[papert-2000-whats-the-big-idea-toward-a-pedagogy-of-idea-power|Papert]]'s LOGO environment is a microworld for generating and manipulating dynamic, computational inscriptions. It operationalizes the idea that children (and others) learn by constructing external representations that they can then reflect upon and debug—a direct pedagogical application of Latour's "thinking with eyes and hands."
*   **[[backus-1978-can-programming-be-liberated-from-the-von-neumann-style|Backus 1978]] (FP):** [[backus-1978-can-programming-be-liberated-from-the-von-neumann-style|Backus]]'s argument for [[hughes-1990-why-functional-programming-matters|functional programming]] can be read as a reform in *notation* designed to make certain kinds of program transformations (inscriptions) more visible, composable, and provably correct, aligning with Latour's criterion for a valuable innovation.
*   **[[hofstadter-2001-analogy-as-the-core-of-cognition|Hofstadter 2001]] (Analogy):** [[hofstadter-2001-analogy-as-the-core-of-cognition|Hofstadter]]'s focus on analogy as the core of cognition complements Latour. Analogies are often constructed and tested through visual or notational transformations (mapping diagram A onto situation B). Latour explains why the externalization of such mappings is powerful.
*   **[[lockhart-2002-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart 2002]] (Mathematician's Lament):** [[lockhart-2002-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]] decries the teaching of mathematics as dead formalism rather than a creative, exploratory activity. Latour would agree: the lament is for a loss of the living craft of building and manipulating visual/mathematical inscriptions to pursue a problem.

## Modern Relevance
Latour's framework is profoundly relevant today:

1.  **AI & Machine Learning:** Modern AI, especially deep learning, is a vast inscriptive engine. Models are "thought" encoded in numerical parameters; training data and outputs are transformed into plots, graphs, and embedding spaces. Interpretability research (e.g., attention maps, feature visualization) is an attempt to make the AI's "cognitive" process legible as manipulable inscriptions. The "agonistic" aspect appears in debates over model benchmarks and leaderboard rankings.
2.  **Digital Knowledge Work & Collaboration:** Tools like Figma, Notion, Git, or collaborative spreadsheets are modern "factories for inscriptions." Latour explains their power: they don't just store information, they enable the combination, versioning, and rhetorical presentation of ideas in ways that make collective argumentation possible.
3.  **Data Visualization & Infographics:** The field of data viz is explicitly about crafting persuasive inscriptions. Latour's insight that the power lies not in realism but in the capacity to be combined, argued with, and stabilized is crucial for designing effective visualizations.
4.  **Education:** It challenges purely mentalist pedagogies. Learning complex subjects requires engaging in the material practices of the field: drawing diagrams, writing proofs, building models. The tool shapes the thought.
5.  **Hyperflash's Work:** Latour provides a theoretical backbone for projects focused on "tools for thought." He shifts the goal from merely displaying information to creating environments where ideas can be *inscribed, transformed, and debated* in novel, powerful ways. The aim is to enhance the user's ability to "muster allies" in their own thinking.

## Key Quotes

1.  **"The most powerful explanations... are the ones that take writing and imaging craftsmanship into account."**
    *   *Analysis:* This encapsulates his core methodological and explanatory principle. Power derives from material practice, not abstract mentality.

2.  **"Who will win in an agonistic encounter between two authors...? Answer: the one able to muster on the sp[[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|ot the lar]]gest number of well aligned and faithful allies."**
    *   *Analysis:* This defines his pragmatic criterion for what constitutes a successful [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] fact. It replaces philosophical definitions of truth with a sociological one grounded in persuasion and alliance-building.

3.  **"Instruments... were of various types, ages, and degrees of sophisti[[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|cation... ]]But their end result, no matter the field, was always a small window through which one could read a very few signs from a rather poor repertoire."**
    *   *Analysis:* A brilliant deflation of [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] machinery. The expensive, complex lab gear exists solely to produce simple, readable inscriptions. This focuses analysis on the *output* of the inscription process.

4.  **"When these resources were lacking, the self same scientists stuttered, hesitated, and talked nonsense... However, inscriptions or the practice of inscribing could [keep their minds in place]."**
    *   *Analysis:* This empirically grounds the theory of distributed cognition. Intelligence is not a stable property of the brain, but a perfor[[egan-2003-some-cognitive-tools-of-literacy|mance that is c]]ontingent on access to external cognitive tools.

5.  **"The Great Divide can be broken down into many small, unexpected and practical sets of skills to produce images, and to read and write about them."**
    *   *Analysis:* The paper's central move: replacing a grand, [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|mystical d]]ichotomy with a series of mundane, analyzable technical practices. This opens up the field for empirical study.

6.  **"No 'new man' suddenly emerged sometime in t[[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|he sixteen]]th century... The idea that a more rational mind or a more constraining [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] method emerged from darkness and chaos is too complicated a hypothesis."**
    *   *Analysis:* His opening salvo against mentalist explanations, using Occam's razor to demand simpler, more material causes for the [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|scientific]] revolution.
