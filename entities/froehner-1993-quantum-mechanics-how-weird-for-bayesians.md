# Froehner 1993 - Quantum Mechanics, How Weird For Bayesians

## Core Thesis
Froehner's central thesis is that the mathematical formalism of quantum mechanics, often presented as ontologically mysterious and fundamentally distinct from classical reasoning, can be largely "demystified" and reinterpreted as a natural, non-weird extension of classical probability theory and mechanics. The paper argues that if one adopts a Bayesian viewpoint—where probability represents a rational scale of incomplete knowledge—and combines it with the classical Hamiltonian equations for particles with *uncertain initial conditions*, the core apparatus of quantum mechanics (superposition, wave functions, operators, uncertainty relations, the Schrödinger equation) emerges as a mathematical consequence. The apparent weirdness stems from historical contingency and a failure to recognize that the superposition principle is a theorem in probability theory, not a unique quantum axiom. The paper contends that quantum probabilities are not "operator-valued" in a way that makes them fundamentally different from classical ones; this distinction contradicts the foundational work of Cox and Schrödinger on probability theory as a logical calculus.

## Historical Context
The paper was written 65 years into the quantum revolution, at a time when the interpretation of quantum mechanics remained hotly contested. The standard "Copenhagen" interpretation, with its emphasis on wave function collapse, complementarity, and the observer's role, held sway in academia but was philosophically unsatisfying to many. Alternative interpretations, such as Bohmian mechanics and various hidden-variable theories, existed but were often marginalized. Crucially, the paper explicitly pushes back against the prevailing discourse that "operator-valued" quantum probabilities represent a radical break from "classical" probability. Froehner situates his work within a lineage of probabilistic reasoning, citing Cox (1946), Schrödinger (1947), and Renyi (1954) to argue that any consistent system of inference must be equivalent to standard probability theory. The problem he addresses is the persistent metaphysical baggage attached to quantum formalism, which obscures its operational core and fuels debates about "spooky action" and instantaneous collapse. He seeks to provide a pragmatic, inference-based foundation that strips away this baggage.

## Key Contributions
1.  **Demystifying Superposition via Fourier Analysis:** The paper's primary intellectual contribution is framing the quantum mechanical superposition principle not as a puzzling postulate but as a direct consequence of the Riesz-Fejér theorem (1915) from Fourier analysis. This theorem states that any non-negative Fourier polynomial can be expressed as the absolute square of another polynomial. Froehner reinterprets this: probability densities (which are inherently non-negative) can be constructed from the modulus squared of "wave functions" (which can have complex phases). This makes the wave function a mathematical tool for ensuring non-negativity, not an ontological entity.
2.  **Deriving QM from Classical Mechanics with Uncertainty:** Froehner demonstrates that by taking classical point particles obeying Hamilton's equations but assigning them *uncertain initial phase-space coordinates* (described by a probability density), one can derive the entire formal framework of quantum mechanics. This includes:
    *   The emergence of wave functions via Fourier transforms.
    *   The identification of momentum (k) and energy (w) as Fourier-conjugate variables to position and time, leading to de Broglie's relations (H=ħω, p=ħk).
    *   The appearance of operators (like x̂ = i∂/∂k, p̂ = -i∂/∂x) as tools for calculating expectation values.
    *   Heisenberg's uncertainty relation as a direct analog of the classical Liouville theorem for phase-space probability conservation, not a fundamental limit on nature.
    *   The Schrödinger equation as a statement of consistency for expectation values.
3.  **Classical Derivation of EPR Correlations:** The paper challenges "EPR lore" by showing that the correlations measured in the spin version of the Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen experiment can be derived using simple classical probability theory (uniform distribution over spin orientations), without invoking quantum non-locality. This argues that the correlations alone do not necessitate the abandonment of an ontological (realist) viewpoint. However, he carefully notes that this does *not* salvage all hidden-variable models, as Bell's inequalities rule out a specific, more restrictive class of them—a point he attributes to [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]] (1989).
4.  **Bayesian Interpretation of Collapse:** The paper explicitly reframes the "collapse of the wave function" as a logical update of probabilities (Bayesian inference), not a physical process. This dissolves questions about its timing or speed, framing it as an update in the knowledge of the observer, not a change in reality.

## Methodology
The argument is purely **theoretical and mathematical**, grounded in probability theory, Fourier analysis, and classical mechanics. It is structured as a proof-of-concept or a reconceptualization. The methodology is:
*   **Axiomatic Starting Point:** Begin with the axioms of probability theory (Cox's theorem) and classical Hamiltonian mechanics for particles.
*   **Incorporation of Uncertainty:** Introduce uncertainty in initial conditions, described by probability densities.
*   **Mathematical Bridge:** Use the Riesz-Fejér theorem as the critical link to connect non-negative probability densities to the complex wave functions of quantum mechanics.
*   **Derivation by Analogy:** Show that the operational formalism of QM (operators, commutation relations, the Schrödinger equation) emerges from calculating expectation values for uncertain classical systems. The methodology is thus **polemical**—it directly challenges the standard historical narrative by providing an alternative, less mystical derivation of the same mathematical tools.

## Influence
The paper's direct influence is likely niche, residing within the subfield of quantum foundations and Bayesian approaches to probability. It serves as a concise statement of the "epistemic" or "Bayesian" interpretation of quantum mechanics, akin to the work of E.T. [[jaynes-1984-the-evolution-of-carnots-principle|Jaynes]], whom Froehner cites. Its contribution is not in proposing new experiments or predictions, but in providing a clear, pedagogical argument that quantum weirdness is, in large part, a failure of interpretation rather than of physics.
*   **Who cited it:** It appears in the collection *Maximum Entropy and Bayesian Methods* (1993), placing it squarely in the Jaynesian tradition. Its influence would be on researchers interested in the foundations of probability, the interpretation of quantum mechanics, and the links between information theory and physics. It likely has minimal citation in mainstream physics journals focused on quantum computing or high-energy theory, where the operational formalism is used as a tool.
*   **What it enabled:** It contributes to a body of work that makes quantum mechanics more accessible and less ontologically burdensome, potentially aiding in pedagogy. It reinforces the idea that quantum theory is a powerful framework for *inference under uncertainty*, not necessarily a description of a bizarre underlying reality. This perspective aligns with and supports pragmatic applications of quantum formalism.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
*   **[[feynman-1974-cargo-cult-science|Feynman]] 1974 (Cargo Cult Science):** Froehner's paper can be seen as an antidote to "cargo cult" interpretations of quantum mechanics. [[feynman-1974-cargo-cult-science|Feynman]] warned against merely mimicking the formalism (the rituals of the cargo cult) without a deep understanding of its foundations. Froehner's work attempts to provide that deeper understanding by deriving the formalism from more familiar, intuitive principles (probability and classical mechanics), thus moving from mimicry to comprehension.
*   **[[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] 1994 (Proof and Progress):** [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] discusses how mathematical understanding is not just about formal proof but about human comprehension and explanation. Froehner's project is similar: he is not proving a new theorem within QM, but providing a new *explanation* for why its formalism works, aiming to advance human understanding and demystify the subject. Both papers critique opaque formalism in favor of deeper insight.
*   **[[lockhart-2002-a-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]] 2002 (A Mathematician's Lament):** [[lockhart-2002-a-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]] decries the teaching of mathematics as a set of meaningless rules disconnected from sense-making. The traditional presentation of quantum mechanics often suffers from the same ailment—students learn to compute with operators without understanding their origin. Froehner's derivation is a direct assault on this "lamentable" pedagogy in physics, attempting to reconnect the formalism to its meaningful roots.
*   **[[anderson-1972-more-is-different|Anderson]] 1972 (More is Different):** Anderson's "More is Different" explores how complex systems exhibit emergent properties not easily deducible from their microscopic laws. Froehner's argument subtly pushes back against the *necessity* of a mysterious quantum realm for emergence. He shows that by starting with classical laws and adding uncertainty (a "more is different" ingredient), you get the complex-looking phenomena of QM. It provides a bridge between the microscopic (deterministic classical mechanics) and the apparently different quantum world.
*   **[[bush-1931-the-differential-analyzer|Bush]] 1945 (As We May Think):** [[bush-1931-the-differential-analyzer|Bush]] envisioned a system for managing humanity's exploding knowledge using mechanical aids. Froehner's paper is a metaphorical exercise in knowledge management: it seeks to reorganize our understanding of quantum mechanics to reduce its "cognitive load" (the weirdness) by linking it to well-managed, prior knowledge (probability theory and classical mechanics).

## Modern Relevance
The paper's relevance to modern technology, particularly AI and quantum computing, is indirect but profound.
*   **AI and Probabilistic Reasoning:** The Bayesian viewpoint Froehner champions is the foundation of modern probabilistic machine learning and AI. His argument that quantum mechanics is, at its core, a theory of inference under uncertainty aligns with the view that quantum computing is a form of probabilistic computation. This perspective is crucial for developing quantum algorithms and understanding their potential advantage, framing it as an advantage in probabilistic representation and sampling, not as "magic."
*   **Quantum Computing Education:** For educators and newcomers to quantum computing, Froehner's demystification is valuable. Understanding that quantum gates are essentially unitary transformations on probability amplitudes, and that measurement is Bayesian update, makes the field more accessible. It shifts the focus from exotic ontology to the practical manipulation of information states.
*   **Knowledge Management and Design:** For designers of complex systems (like HCI practitioners), the paper exemplifies a powerful technique: reducing complexity by finding deep connections to familiar frameworks. The "weirdness" of an unfamiliar system (quantum mechanics, a novel interface) often evaporates when its underlying structure is mapped onto known principles. This is a core tenet of design thinking and cognitive systems engineering.
*   **Philosophy of AI:** As AI systems grow more complex and their decision-making processes become less intuitive, we risk encountering a new "weirdness." Froehner's approach offers a model: rather than treating AI outputs as oracular, we should rigorously derive their behavior from their fundamental rules (e.g., loss functions, optimization dynamics) and the uncertainty in their inputs and training data.

## Key Quotes
1.  **"Quantum-mechanical probabilities are often said to be 'operator-valued' and therefore fundamentally different from 'classical' probabilities, in disregard of the work of Cox (1946) - and of Schrödinger (1947) - on the foundations of probability theory."** (Abstract)
    *   *Analysis:* This quote encapsulates the paper's core critique. It identifies the specific point of philosophical divergence in the standard narrative and grounds the author's counter-argument in established foundational work.
2.  **"If, on the other hand, one starts with probabilities, the superposition principle, far from puzzling, appears as a theorem, valid not only in quantum mechanics but throughout probability theory..."** (Section 1)
    *   *Analysis:* This is the keystone of the demystification. It reverses the historical logic, showing that a seemingly unique quantum postulate is actually a general mathematical truth, making the formalism less special and more logically grounded.
3.  **"With this understanding there is nothing strange about the 'collapse of the wave function' when new data become available. Their utilisation by means of Bayes' theorem inevitably changes all prior probabilities to posterior ones. As this is not a physical but a logical change, questions about its sudden (superluminal) occurrence throughout physical space... do not arise for Bayesians."** (Section 1)
    *   *Analysis:* This quote directly dissolves one of the most famous philosophical puzzles of quantum mechanics by re-categorizing it. It shifts the problem from physics to epistemology, rendering debates about its timing moot.
4.  **"Thus we obtain, for a classical particle obeying Hamilton's canonical equations, the basic formal apparatus of quantum mechanics - complete with wave-particle duality, operator calculus including commutation rules, uncertainty relations and Schrödinger equation. All we had to do was to admit finite uncertainties of the phase space coordinates."** (Section 2, end)
    *   *Analysis:* This is the paper's triumphant conclusion. It states the central result with shocking simplicity, asserting that the entire edifice of quantum formalism is not new physics, but classical physics viewed through the lens of probability theory.
5.  **"It is often stated that the correlation (22) cannot be obtained classically. Its confirmation by experiment is then taken as evidence that the spin coordinates cannot exist simultaneously... If, on the other hand, one treats the quantum-mechanical spin eigenvalues... as determined by hidden variables, one gets the inequalities derived by Bell (1964) that are, in fact, contradicted by experiment."** (Section 3)
    *   *Analysis:* This quote demonstrates the paper's nuanced stance. It carefully distinguishes between the sufficiency of a classical calculation for the correlations (supporting a realist ontology in principle) and the failure of specific, simplistic hidden-variable models (per Bell's theorem). It avoids oversimplification.
