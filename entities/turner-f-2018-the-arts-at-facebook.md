# Turner F 2018 - The arts at Facebook

## Core Thesis
Turner’s central argument is that Facebook’s internal arts programs—the Analog Research Laboratory (ARL) and the Artist in Residence (AIR) program—are not merely decorative or morale-boosting perks. They constitute an **“aesthetic infrastructure”** that serves a specific political-economic function: legitimating and naturalizing the company’s core business model of **surveillance capitalism**. The art blurs the boundary between the public, progressive social sphere and the private, profit-driven corporate space, transforming the office into a managed environment that mirrors Facebook’s external platform. It encourages employees to see themselves not as cogs in a global data-harvesting apparatus, but as creative, community-building artists in a new form of expressive democracy. This is a radical departure from 20th-century corporate art patronage, which aimed to cultivate a liberal public *outside* the firm. Facebook’s art works *inside* to cultivate a specific subject: the engineer-as-user who willingly participates in a system of mutual, yet asymmetric, self-disclosure.

## Historical Context
Turner situates Facebook’s practice within a century-long evolution of corporate arts engagement, highlighting a fundamental shift in purpose:
*   **Industrial Era (pre-1980s):** Corporations like Chase Manhattan used art to project civic virtue, status, and a forward-looking image to the *external* public. Art was a tool for corporate social responsibility and prestige, meant to “illumine and reinforce our individuality” (David Rockefeller) in the broader society. Collaborations with artists (e.g., at Bell Labs, NASA) aimed to bridge the “Two Cultures” and stimulate innovation.
*   **Post-Fordist, Start-Up Culture (1990s-2000s):** The rise of “liberation management theory” in tech blurred work and life, valorizing creativity, autonomy, and flat hierarchies. Arts programs became part of a bohemian, risk-embracing workplace culture designed to attract talent in a competitive market. This is the direct ancestor of Facebook’s programs.
*   **The Problem Solved:** Turner argues Facebook solves a new, specific problem for surveillance capitalism: how to make the constant, underlying work of extracting and analyzing personal data feel ethically positive, creatively fulfilling, and socially responsible *for the workers building the system*. The arts provide the ideological framework to achieve this internal legitimation.

## Key Contributions
1.  **Concept of “Aesthetic Infrastructure”:** This is the paper’s core analytical framework. Turner moves beyond seeing corporate art as symbolism or decoration to theorizing it as a functional component of the political-economic system. Like the platform’s code, the art is an infrastructure that structures behavior and perception, encouraging bottom-up collaboration (mirroring user-generated content) and a particular worldview.
2.  **Mapping the Internal Ecology:** The paper provides a detailed, empirical map of Facebook’s two primary arts programs (ARL and AIR), their history, operations, and the specific aesthetic they promote—a DIY, activist, countercultural aesthetic that repurposes symbols of public resistance for a private corporate context.
3.  **Linking Art to Surveillance Capitalism:** This is the crucial theoretical contribution. Turner explicitly connects the cultural practices inside a tech giant to Shoshana Zuboff’s theory of surveillance capitalism. He demonstrates how the internal culture (the “aesthetic infrastructure”) is designed to mirror and legitimate the external business model (behavioral surplus extraction). The art helps manage the moral contradiction at the heart of the company’s work.
4.  **Re-framing the “Community” Narrative:** Turner shows how the art helps redefine “community.” It shifts the concept from a geographic or politically organized public to a managed network of individual expressions and connections, which is precisely what Facebook’s platform offers and monetizes. The office becomes a prototype for the kind of social world Facebook seeks to build and govern online.

## Methodology
Turner employs a **mixed-methods, ethnographic-critical approach**:
*   **Empirical Base:** His analysis is grounded in three extensive tours of Facebook’s Menlo Park campus, interviews with program directors (current and founding), participating artists, and a comprehensive review of the programs’ internal archives. This gives him a detailed, insider-level view of the practices.
*   **Theoretical Lens:** The argument is structured using critical sociology and political economy, primarily the framework of **surveillance capitalism** from Shoshana Zuboff. He also draws on historical sociology of corporate culture and the “counterculture-to-cyberculture” lineage he has explored in previous work.
*   **Argumentative Structure:** The paper is a **polemical case study**. It begins with a vivid, contradictory scene (activist posters above an NDA) to hook the reader into the puzzle. It then builds a historical contrast (old vs. new corporate art), provides the empirical details of the two programs, and finally delivers the core theoretical interpretation linking it all to surveillance capitalism. The methodology is about building an interpretive case, not testing a hypothesis.

## Influence
*   **Academic Citation:** The paper has become a key reference point in **critical data studies**, **platform labor studies**, and the **sociology of technology**. It is frequently cited alongside works by Zuboff, Sadowski, and Pasquale to analyze how tech companies cultivate internal cultures that align with their external extractive logics.
*   **Applied Influence:** It has informed discussions among **tech ethics advocates**, **labor organizers**, and **critical designers** who seek to understand and critique the material and symbolic tools used to normalize invasive technologies. The concept of “aesthetic infrastructure” provides a useful term for analyzing how culture is weaponized in platform capitalism.
*   **Enabling Further Critique:** The paper helped shift analysis of tech company “perks” (like art, free food, etc.) from being seen as benign benefits to being scrutinized as components of a sophisticated management and ideology apparatus. It provided a template for studying other tech firms’ internal cultural productions.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
Turner’s work stands in a rich dialogue with several core texts:
*   **Engelbart (1962) & Kay (1972):** It offers a devastating critique of the implementation of their visions. While Engelbart and Kay imagined augmenting human intellect and personal computing to empower individuals and communities, Turner shows how one dominant implementation (Facebook) has used the tools of connection to build a “surveillance apparatus.” The arts program is part of the “infrastructure” that makes this feel liberating, not oppressive, for its builders.
*   **Bush (1945):** It connects to Bush’s idea of a “memex” as an augmented, associative trail of information. Facebook’s platform can be seen as a commercialized, behavioral version of this, and Turner’s analysis reveals the internal cultural work required to get engineers to build and maintain this trail-mining system.
*   **Feynman (1974):** The paper is a masterclass in what Feynman called “Cargo Cult Science.” Facebook’s arts programs use the *forms* of community, activism, and creativity (the surfboard, the posters, the residency) but divorce them from their traditional political and social contexts (unions, protest movements, public squares). This creates a “cargo cult” of social change, where the aesthetic trappings of progress are used to reinforce the underlying, unchanged economic engine of extraction.
*   **Lockhart (2002):** Both Turner and Lockhart lament a deep disconnection between a subject’s stated values and its practiced reality. Lockhart describes math education as a soul-crushing set of meaningless rituals, hiding the creative essence of mathematics. Turner describes Facebook’s art as a ritualistic display of countercultural values that hides the alienating essence of surveillance labor. Both see a form of institutionalized falsehood.

## Modern Relevance
This analysis is acutely relevant to the current technological moment:
*   **AI Ethics & Labor:** As AI companies (like OpenAI, Google) promote their work as advancing human knowledge and creativity, Turner’s framework helps scrutinize their internal cultures. Do their perks and stated missions serve as an “aesthetic infrastructure” to legitimize data scraping, exploitative content moderation, or environmental costs?
*   **Platform Governance:** It explains why platforms’ self-descriptions as “neutral public squares” or “connectors” are so persistent and appealing, even amid scandals. The internal culture, reinforced by symbols and art, makes this narrative feel authentic to builders and users alike.
*   **Knowledge Work & Management:** The paper provides a lens for understanding modern corporate culture-building in all tech sectors. It warns that the valorization of “creativity,” “authenticity,” and “community” within a company can be a tool for deepening commitment to potentially harmful goals.
*   **Critical Design & HCI:** It challenges designers and human-computer interaction researchers to consider how the aesthetic and cultural dimensions of the systems they build might serve to normalize surveillance and asymmetric power relations, even while promising connection and empowerment.

## Key Quotes
1.  **“So what are these posters doing here?”** (p. 2) - *This opening question encapsulates the central puzzle. The juxtaposition of radical posters with a non-disclosure agreement terminal immediately highlights the contradiction between the art’s message of openness/activism and the corporate reality of secrecy and privatized data.*
2.  **“They transform political and aesthetic movements into management tools.”** (p. 2) - *This is the thesis in miniature. It names the core function: the co-optation and repurposing of public, oppositional culture into a private, managerial technique for shaping employee subjectivity.*
3.  **“The arts at Facebook blur the line between the public, social sphere and private corporate space, and they encourage workers to imagine the company as a community—a community centered on the celebration of individual creativity.”** (p. 2) - *This explicitly states the mechanism of the aesthetic infrastructure: boundary dissolution and the redefinition of “community” as a network of individual expressions within the firm’s walls, mirroring the platform’s logic.*
4.  **“The new media of surveillance capitalism solicit social behaviors, monitor those behaviors, map social interactions, and resell what they learn to advertisers and others.”** (p. 2) - *This clear definition of the business model, quoted from Zuboff, is the essential baseline against which the art’s legitimating function is measured.*
5.  **“In this new society, Facebook’s engineers will use the company’s technologies to become citizens and shapers of a public they see represented on the walls around them.”** (p. 3) - *This is a powerful description of the idealized subject the art aims to produce: an engineer who experiences their work on surveillance systems as a form of civic participation and world-building, not alienated labor.*
6.  **“The posters and murals these units produce encourage Facebook’s workers to imagine themselves not as architects of a global surveillance apparatus, but as creative technical artists and perhaps even builders of a new, individual-centered expressive democracy.”** (p. 2) - *This quote starkly contrasts the two possible identities (surveillance architect vs. creative artist) that the aesthetic infrastructure seeks to resolve in favor of the latter.*
7.  **“Because I have not been able to interview current employees of Facebook outside the arts programs, I have leaned on published journalistic reports and online materials to ﬂesh out my account.”** (p. 4, footnote) - *This honest admission of methodological limitation is crucial. It underscores the opacity of tech firms and makes the resulting analysis, built from inside-out, all the more valuable as a piece of critical research.*
