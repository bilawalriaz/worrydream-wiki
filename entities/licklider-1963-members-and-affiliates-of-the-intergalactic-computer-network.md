# [[licklider-1960-man-computer-symbiosis|Licklider]] 1963 - Members and Affiliates of the Intergalactic Computer Network

## Core Thesis
This is not a formal paper but an internal ARPA memorandum, a logistical update and intellectual brainstorming session for a small group of researchers. Its core thesis is implicitly stated: **the future of computing lies not in isolated, monolithic machines but in a collaborative network of people and machines sharing resources, standards, and code.** [[licklider-1960-man-computer-symbiosis|Licklider]] argues that for this vision to materialize, the disparate research efforts funded by ARPA must consciously evolve toward interoperability, shared conventions, and a "network-wide" perspective, even while preserving individual innovation. The memo's nuance lies in its [[smith-r-1987-experiences-with-the-alternate-reality-kit-an-example-of-the-tension-between-literalism-and-magic|tension between]] top-down standardization ("network-wide conventions") and bottom-up organic growth ("proliferation" of languages). [[licklider-1960-man-computer-symbiosis|Licklider]] is not mandating uniformity but advocating for *conscious design choices* that facilitate future connection.

## Historical Context
In April 1963, the world of computing was defined by isolation. Mainframes were behemoths, each at a single site, programmed in machine code or early, incompatible high-level languages (FORTRAN, ALGOL, LISP). Time-sharing was a nascent concept, with only a few systems like the CTSS at MIT operational. The idea of connecting these machines over distances was science fiction.

However, ARPA had just been tasked with command-and-control research, a mission requiring robust, [[armstrong-2003-making-reliable-distributed-systems-in-the-presence-of-software-errors|distributed systems]]. [[licklider-1960-man-computer-symbiosis|Licklider]], heading this office, had already published his seminal "[[licklider-1960-man-computer-symbiosis|Man-Computer Symbiosis]]" (1960), which framed computers as partners in human thought. The problem he now faced was practical: he was funding multiple, brilliant but siloed research groups (at MIT, BBN, SDC, Stanford, etc.) all building tools for this future. Without coordination, they would build separate, incompatible futures. The "Intergalactic Computer Network" was a whimsical name for a very real coordination problem. This memo is the first blueprint for the collaborative networked world we now inhabit, written at the moment its constituent parts were being forged.

## Key Contributions
The memo's contributions are visionary, not technical. It pioneered concepts that would define the next 50 years of computing:

1.  **The Network as Primary Unit of Analysis:** [[licklider-1960-man-computer-symbiosis|Licklider]] shifts focus from optimizing a single computer to optimizing a *network of computers and researchers*. The "group advantage" becomes a central design principle.
2.  **The Vision of Ubiquitous Resource Sharing:** He concretely describes a future user effortlessly retrieving data and programs ("curve-fitting routines") from remote machines ("a disc file in Berkeley"), regardless of local [[borning-1981-the-programming-language-aspects-of-thinglab|programming language]]. This is the foundational concept of cloud computing, open-source software, and package managers.
3.  **The Problem of Heterogeneous System Interoperability:** He identifies the core challenge: how do radically different systems ("each center being highly individualistic") communicate? He frames it as a fundamental problem of "asking such questions as 'What language do you speak?'"
4.  **The Concept of a Network Control Language:** He speculates on the need for a meta-language or set of conventions to manage connections [[smith-r-1987-experiences-with-the-alternate-reality-kit-an-example-of-the-tension-between-literalism-and-magic|and resource di]]scovery, an idea that would manifest in protocols like TCP/IP and the Domain Name System.
5.  **The Tension Between Standardization and Innovation:** He argues for "homogeneity" in sub-languages to facilitate "transfer of training" (e.g., consistent symbols for "contents of"), while accepting that a single "preferred" language per facility is more realistic than universal uniformity. This is the eternal engineering and community management struggle.
6.  **Collaborative [[meyer-1989-the-new-culture-of-software-development|Software Development]] as Infrastructure:** He proposes that the group's shared work should be on the tools themselves—compilers, debuggers, documentation systems—not just on isolated applications. This is the precursor to the open-source ecosystem and developer toolchains.

## Methodology
[[licklider-1960-man-computer-symbiosis|Licklider]]'s methodology is **design fiction and scenario-based storytelling**. He does not present data or a logical proof. Instead, he constructs a detailed, first-person narrative ("Let me suppose that I am sitting at a console...") of a hypothetical user performing a complex, multi-step research task across a network. This scenario does the following:
*   **Makes the abstract concrete:** It translates vague ideas like "resource sharing" into a tangible workflow involving data retrieval, program borrowing, and collaborative coding.
*   **Surfaces latent requirements:** The story reveals unspoken needs: information retrieval systems, program linkage mechanisms, file naming conventions, and the need for user-guidance systems.
*   **Engages the reader's imagination:** It invites his colleagues to see themselves as this user and to recognize the pieces they are individually building within this larger, coherent vision.
*   **Is inherently interdisciplinary:** The scenario blends [[hamming-1968-one-mans-view-of-computer-science|computer science]], statistics, psycholinguistics, and HCI, mirroring [[licklider-1960-man-computer-symbiosis|Licklider]]'s own "symbiotic" vision.

The memo's structure is also telling. It begins with a logistical apology, then moves to "intuitive" feelings, attempts to structure those feelings, dives into technical specifics (languages, network control), and culminates in the user story. This mirrors the creative process itself: from intuition to structure to concrete manifestation.

## Influence
The influence of this memo is monumental and direct.
*   **It catalyzed the ARPANET:** This memo and the meetings it organized (the "Intergalactic" meetings) directly preceded and motivated the ARPANET project. The need to share resources and facilitate collaboration among ARPA-funded sites was its primary driving [[air-force-1960-air-force-office-of-scientific-research|force]]. The first ARPANET link was established in 1969.
*   **It established the intellectual framework for the Internet:** The concepts of resource sharing, interoperability, and user-centric network design laid out here became the dogma of the research community that built the Internet.
*   **It defined ARPA's (later DARPA's) research style:** The model of funding visionary, collaborative teams to build shared infrastructure became a hallmark of ARPA/DARPA, leading to everything from Gopher to the Web (via Tim Berners-Lee's work at CERN, which was influenced by the existing Internet culture).
*   **It influenced a generation of computing pioneers:** The memos circulated among figures like Robert [[taylor-2008-oral-history-mcjones|Taylor]], Larry Roberts (who would lead the ARPANET project), Wes [[clark-w-1986-the-linc-was-early-and-small|Clark]], and others who would directly implement these ideas.

## Modern Relevance
The memo reads as a prophetic specification for the modern world.
*   **Cloud Computing & DevOps:** The vision of retrieving data and programs from remote locations is now a daily reality via AWS, Azure, and software repositories like GitHub. The "linkage" between programs is handled by APIs, containerization (Docker), and dependency managers (npm, pip).
*   **Open Source & Collaborative Development:** The call for sharing "compilers, debugging systems and documentation schemes" is the ethos of the open-source movement. Linux, Python, and countless projects embody the "group advantage" [[licklider-1960-man-computer-symbiosis|Licklider]] described.
*   **Interoperability & Standards Wars:** The debate over "network-wide conventions" versus "proliferation" of languages perfectly captures modern conflicts over file formats, communication protocols, and programming paradigms.
*   **AI Development:** Today's AI research labs (like those at Google, OpenAI, Meta) function as "Intergalactic Networks," sharing massive datasets (ImageNet), pre-trained models (on Hugging Face), and tooling (PyTorch, TensorFlow). The need for common interfaces and resource-sharing to accelerate progress is identical.
*   **Knowledge Management:** The entire field of personal knowledge management (Obsidian, Roam) and its networked evolution struggles with the same core problem [[licklider-1960-man-computer-symbiosis|Licklider]] identified: how to structure, share, and build upon interconnected ideas across a "network" of minds and digital tools.

## Key Quotes
1.  **"We have among us a collection of individual (personal and/or organizational) aspirations, efforts, activities, and projects. These have in common, I think, the characteristics that they are in some way connected with advancement of the art or technology of [[licklider-1968-interactive-information-processing-retrieval-and-transfer|information processing]], the advancement of intellectual capability (man, man-machine, or machine), and the approach to a theory of science."**
    *   **Analysis:** This defines the collaborative community not by its tools but by its shared *purpose*: the advancement of intelligence itself. It’s a mission statement for ARPA and, implicitly, for the entire field of human-computer interaction.
2.  **"To make progress, each of the active research needs a software base and a hardware facility more complex and more extensive than he, himself, can create in reasonable time."**
    *   **Analysis:** This is the foundational economic and practical argument for networking and collaboration. It’s the reason for open source, shared libraries, and cloud services. Individual brilliance is insufficient; collective infrastructure is necessary.
3.  **"It seems to me that it is much more likely to be advantageous than disadvantageous for each to see the others’ tentative plans before the plans are entirely crystallized."**
    *   **Analysis:** A powerful argument for transparency and early collaboration over proprietary, secret development. It champions pre-competitive research and the sharing of unfinished work to optimize the entire ecosystem's growth.
4.  **"It will possibly turn out, I realize, that only on rare occasions do most or all of the computers in the overall system operate together in an integrated network. It seems to me to be interesting and important, nevertheless, to develop a capability for integrated network operation."**
    *   **Analysis:** This reveals a key visionary strategy: design for the ambitious, general case even if the common case is simpler. Building the *capability* for deep integration changes what is possible in the future, even if it's not always used.
5.  **"I want to retrieve a set of experimental data... Is there a suitable grid-plotting program anywhere in the system? Using prevailing network doctrine, I interrogate first the local facility, and then other centers... My programs were written in JOVIAL. The programs I have located... were written in FORTRAN. I would like to bring them in as relocatable binary programs and, using them as subroutines..."**
    *   **Analysis:** The heart of the design fiction. It’s a remarkably precise specification for a [[perkins-1997-inventing-the-lisa-user-interface|user interface]], network query protocol, and cross-language interoperability, all wrapped in a naturalistic user story. This is the blueprint for modern package managers and microservices.
