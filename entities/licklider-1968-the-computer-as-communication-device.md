# Licklider 1968 - The Computer as Communication Device

## Core Thesis

Licklider and Taylor's central argument is a radical re-conception of the computer's fundamental purpose. They contend that within a few years, the computer will enable human communication to be more effective than face-to-face interaction, not by optimizing the transmission of information, but by serving as a medium for **cooperative modeling**.

The nuance lies in their definition of "communication." They explicitly reject the engineering definition (transferring signals from point A to point B) and the passive model (two tape recorders playing to each other). True communication is an active, creative, and constructive process where minds interact to produce new ideas. The computer's unique value is not as a faster telephone or a better filing cabinet, but as a **plastic, moldable, and dynamic medium** that allows groups to externalize, examine, manipulate, and converge upon shared conceptual models of problems. This transforms communication from a mere exchange of data into the collaborative construction of understanding.

## Historical Context

The paper was written in 1968, at a pivotal moment. Mainframe computing was dominant, used primarily for "informational housekeeping": payroll, accounting, and ballistic calculations. Computers were seen as powerful calculators and data processors, not as tools for thought or interpersonal connection.

The intellectual context, however, was already primed for a shift. Earlier that decade, Douglas Engelbart had begun developing the "Augmented Human Intellect" system at SRI, demonstrating that computers could be used interactively to enhance human capability. Licklider himself, in his 1960 paper "Man-Computer Symbiosis," had outlined a future of close human-machine partnership. The ARPANET, which Licklider helped inspire as head of ARPA's Information Processing Techniques Office, was in its infancy, with the first nodes connected in late 1969.

Licklider and Taylor were writing to challenge the prevailing paradigm and to define a new one. They were not merely predicting better technology; they were arguing for a fundamental change in the *relationship* between people and computers, and by extension, between people themselves. They were solving the problem of group decision-making and complex problem-solving in an increasingly complex world, where existing communication technologies (telephones, memos) failed to support the shared modeling necessary for deep understanding.

## Key Contributions

1.  **The "Cooperative Modeling" Definition of Communication:** This is the paper's most profound conceptual contribution. By defining communication as "cooperation in the construction, maintenance, and use of a model," they provided a theoretical foundation for what computers should facilitate. This shifts the metric of success from bits-per-second to the quality of shared understanding.

2.  **The Distinction Between Informational Housekeeping and Creative Activity:** They carved a clear divide between the computer's established role in transactional processing and its potential role in "creative informational activity." This created a mandate for a new class of software and systems aimed at thinking and collaboration, not just bookkeeping.

3.  **The Computer as a "Dynamic Medium":** They positioned the computer not as a tool or an appliance, but as a medium with properties superior to paper and voice: moldable, persistent, and capable of linking premises to consequences (i.e., executing simulations or models). This foreshadowed concepts like interactive documents, simulations, and shared workspaces.

4.  **The Primacy of Externalized Models:** They identified that the key weakness of telephone communication is its failure to provide a shared, externalized object for joint attention and manipulation. The computer's screens and shared interfaces could provide this focal point, enabling a level of collaborative editing and analysis impossible over voice alone.

5.  **A Vision for Networked Collaboration:** The paper is one of the earliest clear articulations of what would become groupware and collaborative computing. The description of Engelbart's NLS system at a project meeting is a direct, functional prototype of tools like Google Docs, Figma, or Microsoft Teams, complete with shared cursors ("mice") and drill-down capabilities.

## Methodology

The argument is **theoretical, polemical, and visionary**. It is not based on controlled experiments or empirical data, but on a synthesis of observed human behavior (meetings), a critique of existing technology, and a powerful extrapolation from early interactive computing demonstrations (like Engelbart's).

The structure is a persuasive essay:
1.  **Bold Hook:** Makes the startling prediction of super-face-to-face communication.
2.  **Conceptual Reframing:** Defines communication as cooperative modeling to establish their framework.
3.  **Diagnostic Critique:** Analyzes why current (1968) telecom fails, pinpointing the lack of externalized models.
4.  **Proof-of-Concept Demonstration:** Uses the Engelbart meeting as a concrete, existing example of their vision.
5.  **Explication of Mechanism:** Details how the computer enables the key actions: thumbing through files, maintaining a common model, linking data to context.
6.  **Qualification and Call to Action:** Prudently notes that present computers and software are primitive, but argues the direction is correct and crucial for high-stakes decision-making. The tone is that of a manifesto, urging a community of "on-line" experimenters forward.

## Influence

The influence of this paper is vast and direct. It served as a **foundational text for the field of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI)** and the vision of interactive computing.

*   **Immediate Influence:** It directly inspired and provided intellectual scaffolding for **Doug Engelbart's "Mother of All Demos"** in December 1968, which showcased the very ideas Licklider described (collaborative editing, shared screens, hypertext). It also shaped the development of the **ARPANET** from a communications research network into a platform for resource sharing and collaboration.
*   **Conceptual Lineage:** The ideas permeated **Alan Kay's** work at Xerox PARC, leading to the Dynabook and the Smalltalk environment, which emphasized interactive, personal, and collaborative computing. It is a cornerstone for the entire field of **Computer-Supported Cooperative Work (CSCW)**.
*   **Enabled Technologies:** The vision laid out here is the direct ancestor of: **hypertext systems** (Ted Nelson, Tim Berners-Lee), **groupware** (Lotus Notes), **real-time collaboration platforms** (Google Docs, Miro, Figma), **virtual workspaces**, and the foundational ethos of the **World Wide Web** as a space for both publishing and interaction.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection

*   **Bush 1945 (As We May Think):** Bush imagined a personal, associative "memex" for storing and linking information. Licklider and Taylor extend this personal tool into a **shared, interactive medium**. They take Bush's "trails" through information and make them a collective, editable, and live modeling process.
*   **Engelbart 1962 (Augmenting Human Intellect):** This is the most direct connection. Engelbart's framework of "doing better what we already do" and augmenting capability through language, artifacts, and methodology is the engineering implementation of Licklider and Taylor's theoretical vision. Engelbart's system was the first proof-of-concept for the "computer as a communication device." Their work is symbiotic.
*   **Kay 1972 (A Personal Computer for Children of All Ages):** Kay's Dynabook concept takes the Licklider/Taylor vision of a dynamic medium and makes it **personal, portable, and ubiquitous**. It democratizes the "common medium" they described, imagining it in the hands of every child, further enabling the creative, model-building communication they advocated.
*   **Backus 1978 (Programming Languages as Formal Systems):** While focused on programming, Backus's critique of the von Neumann bottleneck and his advocacy for functional programming can be seen as a quest for more "plastic" and composable formal systems. A better programming language is a more powerful tool for building the very models Licklider and Taylor envisioned.
*   **Hofstadter 2001 (Analogy as the Core of Cognition):** Hofstadter's focus on how minds build and manipulate mental models through analogy aligns perfectly with Licklider and Taylor's core thesis. Their "computer as communication device" is, in essence, a tool to externalize and make interoperable the analogical, model-building processes of the mind.

## Modern Relevance

This paper reads less like a prediction and more like a **specification for systems that now exist**. Its relevance is not historical but operational.

*   **Knowledge Management & Collaboration:** Every collaborative platform (Notion, Confluence, Teams) is an attempt to build the "common medium" for cooperative modeling. They provide shared spaces for documents (externalized models), discussions (the communicative process), and linked data (connecting premises to consequences). The paper's critique of simplistic "information transfer" is a direct challenge to email-centric work cultures.
*   **AI as a Communication Partner:** The paper's framework offers a powerful lens for evaluating modern AI, especially large language models (LLMs). When we use an LLM to brainstorm, summarize conflicting documents, or simulate outcomes, we are using it as a tool for **model-building**. A poor interaction is one where the AI just gives answers (information transfer). A powerful interaction is one where it helps us externalize, critique, and refine our own mental models—the "plastic medium" Licklider described. The ideal AI assistant, from this perspective, is not an oracle but a **facilitator of cooperative modeling** between humans, or between a human and their own evolving ideas.
*   **Hyperflash's Work:** This paper is the philosophical bedrock for any work on augmenting human thought or collaborative sense-making. Projects that build tools for visual thinking, structured argumentation, or dynamic knowledge bases are directly working to realize the "dynamic medium in which premises will flow into consequences." It argues against static content and for environments that respond to interaction, a core tenet for next-generation knowledge interfaces.
*   **The Problem of Scale:** The paper's insight about the necessity of simplifying complex models for decision-making is more relevant than ever in our age of information overload. The challenge remains: how to use digital tools to model complexity without being overwhelmed by it, preserving the "deep modeling" necessary for true understanding.

## Key Quotes

1.  **"In a few years, men will be able to communicate more effectively through a machine than face to face."**
    *   *Analytical:* The paper's bombastic opening thesis. It immediately reframes the computer's potential from calculation to communication, setting a new, ambitious target for the field.

2.  **"To communicate is more than to send and to receive. ... We believe that communicators have to do something nontrivial with the information they send and receive."**
    *   *Analytical:* The core rejection of the Shannon-Weaver model of communication for human purposes. It establishes the paper's humanistic, action-oriented definition, where communication is defined by its *consequences* (new ideas, changed models), not its mechanism.

3.  **"Creative, interactive communication requires a plastic or moldable medium that can be modeled, a dynamic medium in which premises will flow into consequences..."**
    *   *Analytical:* A prescient description of the required properties of collaborative software. "Plastic" implies editability and persistence; "dynamic" implies simulation and reactivity. This is a specification for tools like Figma, simulation environments, or live-coding notebooks.

4.  **"...communication, which we now define concisely as 'cooperative modeling'—cooperation in the construction, maintenance, and use of a model."**
    *   *Analytical:* The paper's most important conceptual contribution. This elegant definition provides a powerful, enduring framework for analyzing any collaborative technology or process.

5.  **"Perhaps the reason present-day two-way telecommunication falls so far short of face-to-face communication is simply that it fails to provide facilities for externalizing models."**
    *   *Analytical:* A brilliant diagnostic insight. It shifts the problem from audio fidelity to cognitive ecology. The solution to better meetings isn't just better microphones; it's a shared whiteboard or a shared digital document.

6.  **"At a project meeting held through a computer, you can thumb through the speaker’s primary data without interrupting him to substantiate or explain."**
    *   *Analytical:* A concrete, mundane, yet revolutionary feature of collaborative software. It describes asynchronous verification within a synchronous session, a capability that fundamentally changes the dynamics of meetings and reduces the "overhead" of explanation.

7.  **"We do not say: 'Buy a computer and your communication problems will be solved.' ... We are confident ... that we are entering a technological age in which we will be able to interact with the richness of living information."**
    *   *Analytical:* A crucial qualification that separates visionary thinking from hype. They acknowledge the gap between the grand vision and the "primitiveness" of current tools, but frame it as a trajectory toward "living information"—systems that are dynamic, interconnected, and responsive.
