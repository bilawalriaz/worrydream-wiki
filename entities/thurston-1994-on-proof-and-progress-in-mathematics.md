# [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] 1994 - On Proof and Progress in Mathematics

## Core Thesis

William [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s central argument is both simple and radical: **mathematics is fundamentally about advancing human understanding, not about producing formal proofs**. He opens by reframing the question from "How do mathematicians [[mueller-prove-2002-vision-and-reality-of-hypertext|prove]] theorems?" to "How do mathematicians advance human understanding of mathematics?" — a shift that sounds subtle but has profound consequences. The first question smuggles in hidden assumptions: that proof has a uniform, established theory, and that mathematical progress consists of proving theorems. [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] rejects both assumptions.

His contention is that what mathematicians actually accomplish is finding ways for people to understand and think about mathematics. The 4-color map theorem controversy illustrates this: the objection to Appel and Haken's computer-assisted proof was not about veracity but about the desire for *human understanding* beyond mere knowledge of truth. A computer that prints out the first 10,000 primes has not given you what you wanted — what you wanted was understanding. This distinction between knowledge and understanding is the engine of the entire paper.

[[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] proposes a recursive definition of mathematics itself: mathematics is the smallest subject including natural numbers and geometry, that mathematicians study, where mathematicians are those humans who advance human understanding of mathematics. As understanding deepens, the subject itself changes to reflect how we think.

## Historical Context

[[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] wrote this essay as a response to Arthur Jaffe and Frank Quinn's 1993 article "Theoretical Mathematics: Toward a Cultural Synthesis of Mathematics and Theoretical Physics," which proposed formalizing the division between rigorous proof and speculative exploration. Jaffe and Quinn had characterized mathematics as "extremely slow and arduous" and "arguably the most disciplined of all human activities" — a characterization [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] found absurd given his experience programming computers, where the standard of formal correctness required is orders of magnitude higher than what mathematicians actually demand.

The paper sits within a long tradition of mathematicians reflecting on the nature of their discipline. The Bourbaki program, which attempted to rebuild all of mathematics on rigorous set-theoretic foundations, cast a long shadow over 20th-century mathematics. [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s essay implicitly challenges the Bourbaki vision by arguing that the formalist approach misunderstands what makes mathematics work. He notes that "most mathematicians adhere to foundational principles that are known to be polite fictions" — for instance, there exists no way to actually construct a well-ordering of the real numbers — and that Gödel's incompleteness theorem guarantees no formal system can serve as a complete basis for mathematics.

[[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] was uniquely positioned to write this paper. As a Fields Medal winner (1982) and one of the most influential geometric topologists of the 20th century, he had direct experience with two radically different modes of mathematical work. His early work on foliations produced dramatic theorems but left the field with a high entry barrier because the proofs were written in "conventional, formidable mathematician's style." His later work on 3-manifolds and hyperbolic geometry took a different approach: he spent years building infrastructure, writing expository notes, and developing the mathematical community's capacity to think in his way before publishing complete proofs.

## Key Contributions

**Understanding versus proof.** [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] argues that understanding is the actual currency of mathematical progress, not formal proofs. He identifies seven different ways of thinking about the derivative (infinitesimal, symbolic, logical, geometric, rate, approximation, microscopic) and notes that this list has no end — entry 37 involves the Lagrangian section of a cotangent bundle. These are not merely different formal definitions but genuinely different cognitive modes. "One person's clear mental image is another person's intimidation."

**Multiple cognitive faculties.** The paper catalogs the diverse mental systems mathematicians use: human language (including mathematical symbol notation), vision and spatial/kinesthetic sense, logic and deduction, intuition and metaphor, stimulus-response patterns, and process/temporal reasoning. Crucially, [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] argues that human mathematical thinking does not work on a single track like a computer's CPU — the brain's facilities "work together loosely, talking to each other at high levels rather than at low levels of organization."

**Communication as the bottleneck.** [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] observes that within a subfield, mathematical ideas can be communicated in minutes through informal channels (gestures, pictures, diagrams, body language), but the same content requires an hour-long talk or a 20-page paper when the channel narrows. The formal written language of mathematics "efficiently conveys some, but not all, modes of mathematical thinking." He draws a vivid analogy: reading a well-understood proof in your subfield is like receiving a toaster with a 16-page manual — if you already understand toasters, you just plug it in.

**Proof as social practice.** [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] describes how mathematical validity actually works: through social networks, shared intuition, and community standards rather than through formal verification. He recalls his own graduate experience of being "highly suspicious" of the social process but finding it reliable. He cites Andrew Wiles's proof of Fermat's Last Theorem, where experts believed the proof was correct based on high-level ideas long before details could be checked. "People are usually not very good in checking formal correctness of proofs, but they are quite good at detecting potential weaknesses or flaws in proofs."

**The theorem-credit problem.** [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] critiques the "common currency" of theorem-credits that drives the mathematical profession. He argues that this narrow metric devalues the many essential activities — expository writing, mentoring, community building, developing shared infrastructure — that make mathematical progress possible. His soccer metaphor is telling: "We do not judge players on a soccer team only by whether they personally make a goal; we judge the team by its function as a team."

**The foliation cautionary tale.** [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s personal account of working in foliation theory illustrates the pathology of the theorem-first approach. His dramatic theorems were documented in formidable style, creating a high entry barrier. Graduate students were advised not to enter the field because "[[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] was cleaning it out." The field evacuated — not because intellectual resources were exhausted, but because the results weren't communicated in a way that sustained a community of practitioners. "More than the knowledge, people want personal understanding."

**The 3-manifolds success story.** By contrast, when [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] turned to 3-manifold topology and hyperbolic geometry, he deliberately concentrated on building infrastructure rather than claiming all the theorems. He distributed expository notes to 1,200 people worldwide, gave talks emphasizing shared understanding over proof, and left room for others to find alternative proofs and claim credit. The result: "quite a number of mathematicians have what was dramatically lacking in the beginning: a working understanding of the concepts." The field thrived.

## Methodology

The paper is structured as a philosophical and autobiographical essay, not a formal argument. [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] poses six open questions (What do mathematicians accomplish? How do people understand mathematics? How is understanding communicated? What is a proof? What motivates people to do mathematics?) and uses his own career as primary evidence. This is deliberate — by using his personal experience as data, [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] demonstrates that the phenomena he describes are not abstract theorizing but lived reality for working mathematicians.

The essay's form embodies its argument. It uses vivid analogies (the soccer team, the toaster manual, the prime-number printout) rather than formal propositions. It draws on cognitive science and psychology as readily as on mathematics. It treats the reader's understanding as the goal, not their agreement with a chain of deductions — precisely the value it argues mathematics itself should pursue.

## Influence

The paper has been widely cited in mathematics education reform, particularly in arguments for visual and spatial approaches to mathematics. [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s catalog of cognitive faculties maps directly onto the multiple-representations framework now common in math pedagogy. His insistence that different people understand the same mathematical object through radically different mental models has influenced efforts to diversify mathematical instruction beyond the definition-theorem-proof paradigm.

In the philosophy of mathematics, the paper helped crystallize the "social practice" view of mathematical knowledge, alongside work by sociologists of science. [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s argument that mathematical validity rests on community standards and social networks — not primarily on formal verification — resonates with philosophical pragmatism and anticipates later debates about computer-assisted proofs.

The paper also influenced the "expository mathematics" movement and the culture of mathematical blogs and informal communication that has flourished since the early 2000s.

## Connections to Other WorryDream Papers

**[[lockhart-2002-a-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]]'s Lament** is perhaps [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s most direct descendant. [[lockhart-2002-a-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]]'s complaint that mathematics education strips away beauty and understanding in favor of rote procedure is essentially [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s argument applied to K-12 schooling. Both authors insist that mathematics is an art and that understanding — not proof — is the real product. [[lockhart-2002-a-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]]'s vivid image of music education reduced to notation drills without ever hearing music mirrors [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s image of mathematicians who produce theorems without communicating understanding.

**[[papert-1980-mindstorms|Papert]]'s Mindstorms** shares [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s cognitive pluralism — the insistence that mathematical thinking is not monolithic but draws on diverse mental faculties (spatial, kinesthetic, linguistic, intuitive). [[papert-1980-mindstorms|Papert]]'s Logo programming language was designed to give children multiple entry points into mathematical ideas, directly reflecting [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s view that "one person's clear mental image is another person's intimidation."

**[[hofstadter-2001-analogy-as-core-of-cognition|Hofstadter]]'s GEB** overlaps in its concern with the gap between formal systems and human understanding. Both [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] and [[hofstadter-2001-analogy-as-core-of-cognition|Hofstadter]] see Gödel's incompleteness theorem not as a technical curiosity but as evidence of something fundamental about the relationship between formal systems and minds.

**[[hamming-1968-one-mans-view-of-computer-science|Hamming]]'s "You and Your Research"** shares the sociological perspective — the recognition that mathematics and science are human activities embedded in social structures, and that understanding these structures is essential to doing good work. Both authors are refreshingly honest about the role of status, motivation, and community in shaping research directions.

## Modern Relevance

**AI and mathematical understanding.** [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s distinction between knowledge and understanding has become urgently relevant in the age of large language models. An AI system that can produce formally correct proofs or state theorems about 3-manifolds may still lack the kind of understanding [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] describes — the rich web of spatial intuitions, metaphorical connections, and cognitive models that human mathematicians use. His paper poses the question: if an AI can produce proofs but cannot communicate understanding in the way [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] describes, is it doing mathematics?

**Computer-assisted proofs.** [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] predicted in 1994 that "in not too many years, we will have interactive computer programs that can help people compile significant chunks of formally complete and correct mathematics." Projects like Lean, Coq, and the Liquid Tensor Experiment have fulfilled this prediction. Yet [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] also warned that formal proofs are "quite different" from "humanly understandable and humanly checkable proofs" — a tension that persists in the formalization community, where much effort goes into making formal proofs intelligible to humans.

**The communication crisis.** [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s observation that mathematical knowledge lives "in the minds and in the social fabric of the community" rather than primarily in documents has gained new urgency as mathematics becomes more specialized and interdisciplinary. The proliferation of online seminars, collaborative blogs, and informal communication channels (MathOverflow, Mathstodon) represents an attempt to maintain the "wide channels of communication" [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]] valued.

**Inclusive mathematics.** [[thurston-1990-mathematical-education|Thurston]]'s catalog of diverse cognitive faculties — spatial, linguistic, intuitive, procedural — and his insistence that different people understand mathematics differently has informed movements toward more inclusive mathematics education and research, recognizing that the "single track" model of mathematical thinking systematically excludes people whose minds work differently.

"Computers and people are very different. For instance, when Appel and Haken completed a proof of the 4-color map theorem using a massive automatic computation, it evoked much controversy. I interpret the controversy as having little to do with doubt people had as to the veracity of the theorem or the correctness of the proof. Rather, it reflected a continuing desire for human understanding of a proof, in addition to knowledge that the theorem is true."
