# Hamming 1968 - One Man's View of Computer Science

## Core Thesis
Richard Hamming's [[turing-1936-on-computable-numbers-with-an-application-to-the-entscheidungsproblem|Turing]] Lecture argues that the nascent field of "computer science" is misnamed and misdirected. His core thesis is that the discipline should be reconceived as "computer engineering," with a primary focus on **practical problem-solving** rather than theoretical possibility. He contends that the fundamental challenge is not whether something can be done in principle (the domain of science), but how to accomplish it "well and simply" under real-world constraints (the domain of engineering). This shift in emphasis, he argues, must permeate everything from academic curricula and teaching methods to the field's public image and its relationship with funding bodies. The paper is a call to prioritize utility, craftsmanship, and social responsibility over mathematical elegance and game-playing.

## Historical Context
Hamming delivered this lecture in 1968, at a critical juncture for computer science. The field was transitioning from an offshoot of mathematics, physics, and engineering departments into a recognized academic discipline. The ACM's Curriculum 68 report, which he directly engages with, was one of the first major attempts to standardize computer science education.

The context is one of explosive growth and identity crisis. Computer knowledge was doubling rapidly, the "blizzard of papers" was already underway, and the classical model of the "scholar who knows 90 percent of the relevant knowledge" was becoming obsolete. There was a prevailing anxiety about the field's direction, its funding future, and its reputation for delivering poor software. Hamming speaks from the industrial research powerhouse of Bell Labs, contrasting its applied ethos with the growing abstraction in academia. His intervention is a direct response to the "mathematization" of computer science and a push to tether it firmly to the machine and its practical applications.

## Key Contributions
1.  **The Science vs. Engineering Dichotomy:** Hamming establishes a foundational heuristic: science explores what *is possible*; engineering selects the best practical solution from many possibilities. He argues computer science is fundamentally the latter.
2.  **Critique of the Curriculum:** He proposes radical changes to CS education, most notably a mandatory, intensive laboratory course where students must design, build, debug, and document a substantial program (e.g., a simulator or compiler), judged on style, efficiency, bug-freedom, and documentation. He also demands a strong minor in a field *other* than math or CS to produce professionals, not just "technicians" or "idiot savants."
3.  **Ethics and Social Responsibility:** The paper is among the earliest to explicitly argue that computer professionals must develop ethical standards and take responsibility for the social consequences of their work. He sees this as crucial for maintaining public trust and funding.
4.  **Reframing the Goal:** He shifts the objective from mere "cleverness" (which he acknowledges was essential in the resource-scarce past) to "genuine understanding." The aim is to produce creators of practical systems, not just programmers of other people's instructions.
5.  **The "Specialization Leads to Triviality" Dictum:** He warns forcefully against excessive, narrow specialization driven by the need to publish, arguing it will yield diminishing returns and leave graduates unprepared for the interconnected challenges of the future.

## Methodology
Hamming's methodology is primarily **polemical and prescriptive**, rooted in personal experience and engineering pragmatism. He builds his argument through:
*   **Dichotomies:** He consciously uses black-and-white distinctions (science/engineering, theory/practice, cleverness/understanding, technician/professional) as rhetorical tools to clarify his position, while admitting their simplification of a "world of shades of grey."
*   **Appeal to Authority and Reality:** He grounds his claims in the observed outcomes of industry (Bell Labs), the practical needs of society, and the historical successes of engineers over pure mathematicians in building key systems.
*   **Logical Deduction from First Principles:** From the definition of engineering and the stated goal of the field, he deduces the necessary changes in education and research priorities.
*   **Future-Oriented Pragmatism:** His arguments are frequently framed around preparing students for the year 2000 and securing the long-term funding and health of the discipline.

## Influence
Hamming's lecture became a touchstone in the ongoing debate about the nature of computer science and its pedagogy.
*   **Curriculum Reform:** His emphasis on "true-to-life programming" and lab work directly influenced subsequent revisions to CS curricula, bolstering the case for software engineering as a core discipline.
*   **Professionalization:** His early call for ethical standards and social responsibility foreshadowed the development of professional codes of ethics by organizations like the ACM and IEEE.
*   **Cultural Critique:** He gave authoritative voice to a sentiment shared by many practitioners: that academia was becoming too detached from the realities of building and maintaining complex software systems. This critique resonated with the burgeoning software crisis of the 1970s.
*   **Citation Legacy:** The paper is frequently cited in discussions of CS history, education, and philosophy, establishing Hamming not just as a contributor to coding theory, but as a foundational thinker about the discipline itself.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
*   **[[bush-1931-the-differential-analyzer|Bush]] 1945 (As We May Think):** Both papers focus on the **practical augmentation of human capability** through technology. [[bush-1931-the-differential-analyzer|Bush]] dreams of tools for the scientist; Hamming wants the creators of those tools to be rigorously trained in their practical application.
*   **[[engelbart-1960-games-that-teach-the-fundamentals-of-computer-operation|Engelbart]] 1962 (Augmenting Human Intellect):** A direct intellectual predecessor in advocating for a **system-level, engineering approach** to enhancing human capability. Hamming's focus on "how" complements Engelbart's focus on "augmentation."
*   **Kay 1972 (Personal Computer):** Kay's vision of the computer as a "metamedium" for personal expression requires exactly the kind of **practical, engineering-focused professional** Hamming advocates for. Kay's future needs Hamming's "professionals," not his "technicians."
*   **[[backus-1978-can-programming-be-liberated-from-the-von-neumann-style|Backus]] 1978 (FP):** A fascinating contrast. [[backus-1978-can-programming-be-liberated-from-the-von-neumann-style|Backus]] advocates for a radical theoretical shift in programming language design (away from the state-mutation of imperative programming). Hamming, while not anti-theory, would likely see such innovations as valuable only insofar as they lead to more **practical, reliable, and understandable** systems.
*   **[[papert-1980-mindstorms-1st-ed|Papert]] 1980 (Mindstorms):** Strong resonance on **education and the role of hands-on construction**. Papert's "learning by making" with Logo mirrors Hamming's call for a lab-intensive curriculum. Both reject rote, abstract instruction.
*   **[[feynman-1974-cargo-cult-science|Feynman]] 1974 (Cargo Cult Science):** Both are devastating critiques of **superficial imitation of form without substance**. [[feynman-1974-cargo-cult-science|Feynman]] attacks bad scientific practice; Hamming attacks "mathematical game-playing" and "superficial cleverness" in CS that lack genuine understanding and practical utility.
*   **[[lockhart-2002-a-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]] 2002 (Mathematician's Lament):** [[lockhart-2002-a-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]] laments the killing of mathematical beauty in school. Hamming, conversely, is wary of *over-emphasizing* abstract mathematical beauty in CS at the expense of practical skill. They represent two sides of the same pedagogical coin.

## Modern Relevance
Hamming's arguments are profoundly, almost eerily, relevant to contemporary computing:
1.  **The Theory vs. Practice Chasm:** The debate persists. Hamming's call for engineering rigor is echoed in the push for DevOps, MLOps, and robust software engineering practices amidst the hype of theoretical AI breakthroughs. The "how" of deploying and maintaining AI systems is now a central challenge.
2.  **Education and Skills Gaps:** His critique of producing "mere technicians" who can code but not solve broader problems speaks directly to concerns about coding bootcamps versus comprehensive CS education. The need for interdisciplinary knowledge (a "strong minor") is more critical than ever as computing pervades every field.
3.  **Ethics and Social Responsibility:** In an era of AI bias, surveillance technology, and platform power, Hamming's early insistence that "the social responsibility of the programmer" is a core professional concern is not just relevant, but urgent.
4.  **Specialization vs. Breadth:** His warning against "specialization leading to triviality" is amplified by today's extreme fragmentation of the field (e.g., "AI" encompassing ML, NLP, CV, robotics, etc.). The need for a "broad training" to see systemic connections is paramount.
5.  **Quality over Quantity:** His preference for "careful, critical, considered survey articles" over a flood of non-essential papers is a direct challenge to modern publish-or-perish academic incentives.

## Key Quotes

1.  > "We call the field 'computer science' but I believe that it would be more accurately labeled 'computer engineering' were not the term likely to be misunderstood."
    *   **Analysis:** This is the thesis in miniature. Hamming wants to reorient the field's identity from passive inquiry ("what is possible?") to active construction ("what is the best practical solution?").

2.  > "Specialization leads to triviality."
    *   **Analysis:** A powerful, memorable aphorism against the tunnel vision of hyper-specialization. It argues that deep, narrow work loses sight of the meaningful, interconnected whole, a critical warning in any rapidly expanding field.

3.  > "Mathematics is what mathematicians do... What is true about defining mathematics is also true about many other fields: there is often no clear, sharp definition of the field."
    *   **Analysis:** This deflationary definition cleverly shifts the argument from abstract identity to concrete practice. If a field is defined by the work its practitioners do, then the nature of that work (engineering vs. science) is what matters.

4.  > "Whether or not a pure mathematician is right in claiming that what is utterly useless today will be useful tomorrow... it is simply poor propaganda for raising the large amounts of money we need."
    *   **Analysis:** A brutally pragmatic argument about survival. Hamming links the field's theoretical purity directly to its funding viability, forcing a reckoning between academic ideals and operational reality.

5.  > "We need to avoid making computer science look like pure mathematics: our primary standard for acceptance should be experience in the real world, not aesthetics."
    *   **Analysis:** This strikes at the heart of the academic culture war Hamming is waging. He explicitly rejects aesthetic judgment (the mathematician's "elegance") as the primary criterion, replacing it with a standard of practical, real-world efficacy.

6.  > "What we need are professionals!"
    *   **Analysis:** The capstone of his educational argument. "Professionals" here means individuals with deep technical skill, broad interdisciplinary understanding, and a sense of ethical responsibility to society—the antithesis of the narrow "technician" or the irresponsible "clever" programmer.
