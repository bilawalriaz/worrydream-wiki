# Ong 1985 - Writing is a Technology that Restructures Thought

## Core Thesis
Walter Ong’s central argument is that writing is not a mere tool or transparent medium for recording thought, but a fundamental **technology that restructures human consciousness itself**. Its adoption creates a new kind of mind—the "chirographic" or literate mind—whose very patterns of thinking, analysis, and abstract conceptualization are shaped by the visual, spatial, and fixative properties of written text. This restructuring is so profound that literate individuals often mistake their technologically-mediated thought for a natural, universal baseline. Ong contrasts this with "primary orality," the cognitive mode of cultures without any knowledge of writing, which operates on entirely different principles (formulaic, aggregative, conservative, and agonistic). The paper’s nuance lies in showing that literacy isn’t just an addition to human capability; it’s a transformative layer that alters the "noetic economy"—the entire mental ecosystem for managing knowledge.

## Historical Context
Ong writes within the field of **media studies and the history of consciousness**, a space shaped by several key predecessors. The foundational work of Milman Parry and Albert Lord on oral-formulaic composition (studying Homeric epics) demonstrated that oral poetry wasn’t a degraded version of written literature, but a sophisticated, self-sustaining system. Marshall McLuhan’s "the medium is the message" (1964) popularized the idea that communication technologies shape societies and minds. Ong also engages with the linguistic anthropology of Jack Goody and the cognitive linguistics of Wallace Chafe.

The problem Ong addresses is the deep **"chirographic bias"** of modern, high-technology societies. Educated, literate people in the 20th century (including scholars) implicitly assumed that abstract, analytical, linear thought was simply "how humans think," and that writing merely preserved it. They viewed illiteracy as a simple lack of skill, like shoe-tying, failing to grasp that they themselves thought differently because of their immersion in a text-based culture. Ong’s work was part of a corrective wave in the 1980s, synthesizing research to dismantle this assumption and recover an understanding of pre-literate cognitive modes.

## Key Contributions
1.  **The Primary/Secondary Orality Distinction:** Ong formalizes two types of orality. **Primary orality** is the mindset of people with no contact with writing. **Secondary orality** is the orality of radio and television, which is dependent on and generated by a prior literate culture, sharing some features of primary orality but also fundamentally altered by it.
2.  **Writing as a "Technology" and a "Neural" Extension:** He powerfully frames writing as a technological intervention comparable to modern computers. It is an externalization of thought that, once internalized ("interiorized"), becomes part of the cognitive apparatus. He suggests it acts as a kind of neural prosthesis, changing how the mind performs its core operations.
3.  **Formulaic vs. Analytic Thought:** He details how primary orality manages knowledge through **formulaic structures** (proverbs, epithets, rhythmic patterns, narrative) to combat the evanescence of sound. Literacy, by fixing thought visually, enables analysis—literally "taking apart"—of concepts, leading to the categories, definitions, and abstract logic that define modern science and philosophy.
4.  **The "Interiorization" of Writing:** A key mechanism he identifies is that literate people **cannot imagine a word without its visual, written form** (e.g., the letters of "nevertheless"). This demonstrates how deeply the technology has been internalized, shaping even our private, inner speech and making the orality of our ancestors seem alien and "deviant."
5.  **Plato's Critique as a Proto-Critique of Technology:** Ong astutely observes that Plato’s four objections to writing in the *Phaedrus* (it’s artificial, unresponsive, destructive to memory, and cannot defend itself) are identical to modern critiques of computers and calculators. This frames literacy itself as the original, disruptive "new technology" of the Western mind.

## Methodology
Ong’s methodology is **theoretical synthesis and polemical argument**, grounded in interdisciplinary scholarship. It is not empirical in a lab-experiment sense, but draws on:
*   **Historical Evidence:** Data on the age of scripts vs. the age of *Homo sapiens*, statistics on the number of written vs. unwritten languages.
*   **Oral-Formulaic Research:** Citing John Miles Foley’s massive compilation of oral tradition studies to demonstrate the universality of formulaic thought.
*   **Cognitive Phenomenology:** Using thought experiments (like the "nevertheless" test) to make the invisible effects of literacy tangible to the reader.
*   **Philosophical Framing:** Employing a technology-studies lens (comparing writing to computers) and phenomenological concepts (consciousness, noetic economy, life-world) to elevate the discussion from mere history to an analysis of human being.

The structure is **diagnostic and corrective**. He first diagnoses the modern bias that naturalizes literacy, then presents the evidence for primary orality as a contrast, and finally analyzes the transformative consequences of writing’s invention.

## Influence
Ong’s work, particularly this paper and his book *Orality and Literacy* (1982), became a **foundational text in media ecology, composition studies, and the history of the book.** It influenced:
*   **Human-Computer Interaction (HCI):** By framing writing as a technology, he provided a historicized framework for thinking about computers as the next major cognitive restructuring tool. His ideas about externalization and interiorization directly inform discussions of "extended cognition" and "augmented intellect."
*   **Digital Literacy Studies:** The debate over whether texting and internet use constitute a "new literacy" with its own cognitive restructuring often draws on Ong’s paradigm.
*   **Post-Structuralism and Deconstruction:** While not a deconstructionist, Ong’s emphasis on the material, visual technology shaping meaning resonated with theorists like Jacques Derrida who critiqued "logocentrism" (the privileging of spoken presence).
*   **Education and Pedagogy:** His work gave profound theoretical weight to arguments about the importance of oral discussion, storytelling, and the dangers of assuming a purely text-based model of learning.
His ideas enabled a generation of scholars to stop seeing technology as external to the human mind and to start investigating how **each major technology (writing, print, electricity, computation) reconfigures what it means to think, know, and be human.**

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
Ong’s paper is a crucial hub in a network of ideas about technology and the human mind:
*   **Bush 1945 (As We May Think):** Bush’s Memex is a **technology for augmenting the literate mind**. He is not questioning the literate model of thought; he is designing a better machine to support its associative, memory-intensive work. Ong would see Bush as operating entirely within the chirographic/typographic paradigm, seeking to extend it electronically.
*   **Engelbart 1962 (Augmenting Human Intellect):** Engelbart’s "Augment" system is a direct attempt to build the kind of neural extension Ong describes. Engelbart seeks to *restructure thought* by coupling it with a dynamic, non-linear technological framework. Ong provides the historical and cognitive theory that explains *why* such augmentation is both possible and transformative.
*   **Kay 1972 (Personal Computer):** Kay’s vision of a "personal computer" as a **"bicycle for the mind"** is the logical next step in Ong’s sequence. After the restructuring by writing and print, the computer offers a new medium that combines fixity (like text) with dynamism (like speech) and restructures thought in new, still-unfolding ways.
*   **Backus 1978 (FP):** Backus’s argument that the **von Neumann bottleneck** stems from a language (imperative programming) that mimics how we think *after* literacy (step-by-step, linear, command-driven) is a deep example of Ong’s thesis. Functional Programming (FP), with its focus on expressions and transformations, might represent a different cognitive model, hinting at a possible restructuring of thought enabled by a new formal language.
*   **Lockhart 2002 (Mathematician’s Lament):** Lockhart’s critique that math education reduces a creative, narrative, artistic practice to the rote manipulation of symbols is profoundly **Orngean**. He is lamenting the deadening of a more dynamic, oral-like mode of mathematical thought (proof, exploration, argument) by a brittle, chirographic pedagogy that mistakes the notation for the mathematics itself.

## Modern Relevance
Ong’s framework is **essential for understanding the cognitive impact of the digital age**.
1.  **AI and Large Language Models (LLMs):** LLMs are the ultimate **"chirographic" entities**. They operate solely on the fixated, visual residue of human thought (text). They have no access to primary orality, no embodied life-world, and no understanding of sound, evanescence, or interpersonal context. This explains their power at mimicking literate patterns and their fundamental lack of understanding. They are the logical endpoint of the "text as paradigm" bias Ong critiques.
2.  **Knowledge Management & Hypertext:** Modern tools like wikis, note-taking apps (Roam, Obsidian), and the "second brain" concept are attempts to create a new **"secondary orality" for literate knowledge**. They use networked, non-linear, "living" text to mimic some associative and mnemonic properties of oral cultures, but on a technological foundation of absolute fixity and external storage. Ong would analyze this as a new cognitive restructuring.
3.  **Education:** The debate between lectures (oral) and textbooks (literate) is clarified by Ong. More so, the push for video, podcasting, and interactive digital media represents a move toward a **"secondary orality" pedagogy** that must be understood on its own terms, not as a poor substitute for text.
4.  **"Flash" Information & Cognitive Bandwidth:** The feeling of being overwhelmed by information streams can be understood as the clash between our **literately-restructured minds** (which crave linear analysis and fixity) and the **orally-structured, evanescent, and aggregative nature of social media feeds and real-time data**. Ong’s work helps diagnose this mismatch.

## Key Quotes

> **"Literacy is imperious. It tends to arrogate to itself supreme power by taking itself as normative for human expression and thought."**
> *Analysis:* The paper’s opening salvo. It defines the problem: we who are literate cannot see past literacy to consider it as one possible state among others, not the natural state.

> **"Without a deep understanding of the normal oral or oral-aural consciousness and noetic economy of humankind before writing came along, it is impossible to grasp what writing accomplished."**
> *Analysis:* States the paper’s methodological premise. To understand the tool, you must first understand the world it transformed. This is a direct challenge to ahistorical views of mind and technology.

> **"The mind trained in an oral culture does not feel the literate's distress: it can operate with exquisite skill in the world of sounds, events, evanescences."**
> *Analysis:* A crucial corrective that avoids a hierarchical view of oral vs. literate. Orality is not primitive; it is a different, sophisticated cognitive system adapted to a different medium.

> **"Writing was an intrusion, though an invaluable intrusion, into the early human lifeworld, much as computers are today."**
> *Analysis:* The paper’s most powerful analogy, making the ancient transformation relatable. It frames every major technology as a disruptive "intrusion" that restructures consciousness.

> **"For the real word 'nevertheless', the sounded word, cannot ever be present all at once, as written words deceptively seem to be... To the extent that it makes all of a word appear present at once, writing falsifies."**
> *Analysis:* A brilliant phenomenological observation. It shows how writing creates the illusion of timeless, static entities (words, concepts), whereas reality is processual and temporal.

> **"Hard-core textualism is snobbery, often hardly disguised."**
> *Analysis:* A polemical punch line that underscores the political and ethical implications. Assuming the primacy of text marginalizes the vast majority of human cultural and linguistic experience, past and present.
