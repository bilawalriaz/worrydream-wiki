# Strassmann 2008 - The Computers Nobody Wanted, My Years With Xerox

## Core Thesis
Paul Strassmann's memoir is not primarily a nostalgic recount of inventions at the famed Xerox Palo Alto Research Center ([[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]]). Its core, and more potent, thesis is an indictment of **systemic corporate incompetence in recognizing, adopting, and commercializing technological innovation**. The "computers nobody wanted" refers not to the machines themselves—like the Xerox Alto, often hailed as the precursor to the personal computer—but to the *consequences* of technological capability when filtered through misaligned corporate strategy, short-term financial thinking, and a failure of leadership imagination. Strassmann argues that Xerox's collapse from a monopoly position was not due to a lack of innovation, but to a profound organizational inability to understand and harness the very technologies it invented. The narrative serves as a case study in how bureaucratic inertia, accounting myopia, and cultural resistance can squander even the most groundbreaking technical advantages.

## Historical Context
The narrative spans from the mid-1960s to the early 1980s, a period of two distinct computing eras that Strassmann bridges:
1.  **The Mainframe Era (Pre-Xerox):** He begins in the corporate IT jungle of the 1960s, dominated by IBM's monopolistic practices and the "cult of the computer" centered on data processing. The context is the IBM System/360 announcement—a monumental, industry-shaking bet that promised compatibility and scalability but, as Strassmann details, often delivered flawed emulation and forced costly transitions. His work at National Dairy Products (NDPC) establishes the central conflict: the chasm between vendor hype (IBM's promises), corporate desire for control, and the messy reality of deployment and cost.
2.  **The Dawn of Personal and Networked Computing:** He moves to Xerox in the late 1960s, initially to manage its minicomputer subsidiary, [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|Scientific]] Data Systems (XDS). This places him at the intersection of the declining minicomputer market and the nascent, radical world of [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]]. The problem being solved at [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]]—the creation of a dynamic, interactive "Office of the Future" with Ethernet, bitmapped displays, and object-oriented programming—was a paradigm shift away from batch-processing mainframes. The corporate problem, however, remained the same: how to translate technical capability into profitable products and strategic advantage.

## Key Contributions
This memoir contributes several critical analyses beyond a simple history:
1.  **A Taxonomy of Corporate Failure Modes:** Strassmann meticulously catalogs the diseases that killed Xerox: the divorce between research ([[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]]) and product divisions; the use of accounting rules (like equipment billing practices) that made innovation financially "unprofitable"; the dominance of sales and marketing over engineering; and the failure to see the computer as an *information* tool rather than a *data processing* one.
2.  **The "Pendery Papers" and the Architecture of Information:** He introduces the strategic planning work of Don Pendery, which attempted to formalize Xerox's future around an "Architecture of Information." This was a radical, systemic vision that was ultimately rejected by a company addicted to copier revenues, highlighting the tragedy of a forward-looking strategy being vetoed by a backward-looking balance sheet.
3.  **The Inside Account of the Alto's Mismanagement:** He provides a ground-level view of how the Alto was perceived within Xerox—as an expensive, impractical toy ("the computer nobody wanted") by divisions focused on selling toner and paper. This complements more technical histories by explaining *why* the company failed to capitalize on its own invention from a business and organizational perspective.
4.  **The Economics of Technology Adoption:** Drawing from his earlier work (*The Squandered Computer*), Strassmann applies an "information economics" lens to the Xerox story. He shows how misaligned incentives, such as charging departments for computer time, actively discouraged the experimental use that might have led to breakthrough applications.

## Methodology
The work is a **personal memoir structured as an analytical post-mortem**. Strassmann uses his unique vantage point as a senior executive moving from corporate IT to XDS management to strategic planning and finally to the [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]] periphery. The methodology is:
*   **Anecdotal Evidence as Data:** Key events (the IBM 360 sell, the Honeywell "Liberator" gambit, the Leningrad copier sale) are used as data points to illustrate broader principles of corporate behavior and technological adoption.
*   **Strategic Retrospection:** The analysis is enriched by hindsight. He juxtaposes what was known or knowable at the time (e.g., the internal doubts about IBM's promises) with the decisions that were made, and contrasts the visionary proposals (Pendery's plans) with the final, disastrous outcomes.
*   **Financial and Organizational Lens:** Unlike a technologist's history, the argument is consistently framed through budgets, accounting rules, departmental P&L statements, and org charts. The methodology is fundamentally that of an information economist diagnosing an organization's failure to manage its most valuable asset: knowledge.

## Influence
The influence of this memoir is less in academic citation and more in **strategic and business lore**:
*   **Canonical Case Study:** It is a primary source for the canonical "Xerox fumbled the PC" narrative, but with far more nuance about the *internal* reasons than the usual story of "management just didn't get it."
*   **Preaching to the (Corporate) Choir:** It serves as a warning text for CIOs, CTOs, and corporate strategists about the perils of bureaucratic inertia, the danger of letting accounting drive strategy, and the necessity of aligning organizational structure with technological vision.
*   **Lineage of "Innovator's Dilemma":** While predating Clayton Christensen's formal theory, Strassmann's account is a vivid, detailed illustration of the dynamics Christensen would later codify—the established company ignoring disruptive technologies because they don't fit the existing business model and profit metrics. It provides the raw, messy reality behind the theoretical model.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
*   **Douglas [[engelbart-1962-augmenting-human-intellect-typeset|Engelbart]] (1962, "Augmenting Human Intellect"):** [[engelbart-1962-augmenting-human-intellect-typeset|Engelbart]]'s paper is the visionary blueprint for what Xerox [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]] was actively building (the Alto, NLS concepts). Strassmann's memoir is the tragic story of how that blueprint, once realized in the lab, was killed by the very corporate organism that housed it. It shows the immense gap between inventing a tool for intellectual augmentation and building a business around it.
*   **J.C.R. [[licklider-1968-interactive-information-processing-retrieval-and-transfer|Licklider]] & Bob [[taylor-2008-oral-history-mcjones|Taylor]] (1968, "The Computer as a Communication Device"):** Another [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]]-adjacent vision. Strassmann's account of the internal resistance to the Alto and Ethernet details the institutional blindness to this revolutionary paradigm. The "Office of the Future" was a communication device; Xerox still sold a copy machine.
*   **Richard [[feynman-1974-cargo-cult-science|Feynman]] (1974, "Cargo Cult Science"):** Xerox [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]] exhibited the outward forms of a top-flight research lab (the brilliant people, the advanced prototypes) but the parent company engaged in a form of "cargo cult" business strategy—aping the forms of innovation without understanding the underlying principles of how to bring it to market and create value. The Alto was the "bamboo radio" that looked like a real computer but didn't fit the corporate "runway."
*   **Thomas Kuhn (The Structure of [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|Scientific]] Revolutions - implied):** The story is a classic example of a paradigm shift (mainframe -> personal computing) being resisted by an incumbent (Xerox's copier culture). The existing "normal science" of selling electrostatic paper made it impossible to even *see* the new paradigm, let alone act on it.
*   **Markus Buehler & others on AI/Software:** The modern relevance is direct. Today's AI labs (within large tech companies or as startups) face a parallel Strassmannian dilemma: How to foster and commercialize transformative technology without having the core business model, accounting practices, and organizational culture strangle it in the crib.

## Modern Relevance
Strassmann's analysis is devastatingly relevant to contemporary technology management:
1.  **The AI Adoption Chasm:** Today's corporations are rushing to adopt generative AI, often making the same errors Xerox did: purchasing expensive tools without understanding their use cases, failing to align AI projects with core business strategy, and allowing existing metrics (like efficiency gains in call centers) to limit exploration of more transformative, but less immediately quantifiable, applications.
2.  **Innovation vs. Business Unit P&L:** The conflict between [[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]] and Xerox's copier divisions is replayed every day in large companies. Should a new, disruptive technology be housed in a protected innovation lab (risking irrelevance) or within a business unit (risking being killed by that unit's quarterly targets)?
3.  **The Tyranny of Legacy Accounting:** Strassmann's critique of equipment billing is timeless. How companies account for and fund innovation—using CapEx vs. OpEx, charging for API calls, measuring "ROI" of research—is a primary determinant of whether transformative ideas live or die.
4.  **Knowledge Management as Core Competency:** Strassmann, through his other works, saw information as capital. Xerox failed because it treated its knowledge capital ([[parc-1971-parc-papers-for-pendery-and-planning-purposes|PARC]]'s inventions) as a cost center to be minimized, not an asset to be leveraged. In the age of data and AI, this is the central strategic error a company can make.

## Key Quotes
1.  **"Behind much of the hoopla and misrepresentation were the IBM marketing machine that promoted capabilities that the IBM technologists could not deliver immediately, and some cases only a decade later."**
    *   *Analysis:* Establishes the historical pattern of vendor hype vs. technical reality, a cycle that repeats with every major technology platform shift (cloud, blockchain, AI). It frames the corporate buyer as perpetually at a disadvantage.
2.  **"Corporate purses would be loosened to promote innovation that would be able to take advantage of IBM’s extravagant claims."**
    *   *Analysis:* Highlights the perverse incentive: spending is justified not by clear need or proven technology, but by the *promise* of future capability sold by vendors. This creates a rush to adopt before the technology is mature or understood.
3.  **"My job would be to oversee spending that would be a multiple of what I proposed for General Foods... It dawned on me that IBM must have had a hand in setting up the interview."**
    *   *Analysis:* A cynical but insightful observation on the symbiotic (and sometimes manipulative) relationship between large corporations and their primary technology vendors. It underscores that strategic decisions are often shaped by vendor influence.
4.  **"The computers nobody wanted" [referring to the Alto and related inventions].**
    *   *Analysis:* The title's core irony. It speaks to the difference between technological desirability (by engineers) and perceived need (by business units). It encapsulates the tragedy of an innovation that is culturally and organizationally incompatible with its parent.
5.  **"The Architecture of Information" [Pendery's concept].**
    *   *Analysis:* Represents the high-water mark of strategic thinking at Xerox—a holistic, systemic vision for the company's future. Its ultimate failure is the measure of how deeply the company's culture was committed to its past.
6.  **"Xerox’s collapse from a monopoly position was not due to a lack of innovation, but to a profound organizational inability to understand and harness the very technologies it invented."** (Paraphrase of core thesis from introduction).
    *   *Analysis:* The definitive statement that shifts blame from technical failure to managerial and cultural failure. It's the central lesson for any innovative organization today.
7.  **"The company addicted to copier revenues."**
    *   *Analysis:* A metaphor for organizational addiction to legacy business models. The "drug" is the easy, predictable profit from existing products, which makes the "cold turkey" investment in a new, uncertain future seem unbearable.
