# Turner F 2017 - Don't Be Evil

## Core Thesis
Fred Turner argues that the dominant techno-utopian ideology of Silicon Valley, encapsulated by slogans like "Don't Be Evil," is not a modern innovation but a direct, unexamined inheritance from the New Communalist movement of the late 1960s. This ideology is not merely a marketing veneer but a sincere, operational belief system that equates information connection with political liberation and views hierarchy as the root social evil. However, Turner demonstrates that this worldview is fundamentally flawed. It is anti-political, mistaking the elimination of formal structures for progress, while ignoring that power and inequality simply re-emerge in informal, charismatic, and often more insidious forms. Furthermore, the engineering culture derived from this tradition provides an inadequate ethical framework for companies like Facebook and Google, which are in fact media and political infrastructures, but whose operators believe that building an efficient, functional system ("making the product work") constitutes the entirety of their ethical responsibility.

## Historical Context
Turner’s analysis addresses a specific problem: the failure of the tech industry and its critics to understand the cultural and political origins of its core ideology. He situates the contemporary tech ethos within two key historical phases. First, the **New Communalist movement (1966-1973)**, which rejected the institutional politics of the New Left in favor of building small-scale communes to achieve consciousness transformation through "small-scale technologies" (like LSD). This movement collapsed by the early 1970s, but its participants remained in the Bay Area. Second, the **rise of personal computing in the 1970s and 80s**, which coincided with these displaced communalists seeking work. Figures like Stewart Brand reimagined computers as the successor to LSD—a tool for creating the networked, hierarchical-free community they had failed to build physically. This history corrects the common misconception that techno-utopianism originated in the earnest geek culture of the 1990s internet boom or from libertarian economics. It is, rather, a countercultural dream that migrated into engineering and business.

## Key Contributions
Turner’s primary contribution is a **historical genealogy of Silicon Valley's political morality**. Specific conceptual contributions include:
1.  **The "Connectionist Politics" Framework**: Defining the tech industry's core belief as a desire to create a "completely leveled" world without politics, where connection via technology dissolves hierarchy. This frames the goals of Facebook and Google not as novel, but as a direct continuation of 1960s communal ideals.
2.  **The "Ethics of Functionality" in Engineering Culture**: Articulating the professional ethical standard of the engineer as: "Does it work?" This explains why tech companies are "baffled" by critiques of societal harm; within their framework, a system that functions efficiently and profitably is an ethical success. This disconnect is a primary source of their current crises.
3.  **The "Burning Man as Secular Church" Analogy**: Comparing Burning Man to the 19th-century Protestant church, not for piety, but for its function as a ritual rehearsal of social structures. It allows participants to experience the "promise that Silicon Valley makes but can’t keep": total "flow" in a self-chosen, self-owned project within a utopian community, reinforcing the values that sustain the Valley's labor model.
4.  **The Critique of "Utopianism" vs. "Optimism"**: Distinguishing between totalizing utopian systems (which fail by offering absolute solutions) and partial optimism (which allows for complexity, distress, and difference). He advocates for engaging technology as **infrastructure**, not magic.

## Methodology
The argument is structured as an **intellectual history and cultural critique**, delivered through the discursive format of an interview. Turner’s methodology is:
*   **Historicist**: He explains the present by tracing ideas to specific past movements (New Communalism) and figures (Stewart Brand), using his own prior scholarship (*From Counterculture to Cyberculture*) as a foundation.
*   **Sociological**: He analyzes contemporary tech companies (Google, Facebook) as cultural institutions with internal belief systems and professional ethical codes, not just economic actors.
*   **Analogical**: He builds understanding through potent comparisons (Burning Man to the Puritan church, engineering ethics to Wernher von Braun, infrastructure design to Roman Forum architecture).
*   **Normative**: While descriptive, the argument carries a clear prescription: abandon techno-utopianism and adopt a framework of "politics of infrastructure."

## Influence
As a 2017 interview, its influence is still solidifying, but it acts as a powerful synthesis and amplifier of critiques already circulating in academia and tech criticism. Turner’s work provides a historical backbone for arguments made by earlier critics like Evgeny Morozov (*To Save Everything, Click Here*) but roots them specifically in American cultural history. It has likely been cited by subsequent work on **platform governance, tech ethics, and the history of computing** that seeks to connect Silicon Valley’s present pathologies to its countercultural roots. It offers a more nuanced origin story than the "libertarian techno-capitalism" narrative alone. The article solidifies the idea of "infrastructure as politics" as a key lens for analyzing Big Tech.

## Modern Relevance
Turner’s analysis is acutely relevant to the **AI ethics and governance debates** of the 2020s.
*   **The "Ethics of Functionality" vs. Societal Impact**: This perfectly describes the initial response of many AI developers: "Our model works with high accuracy; therefore, it is good." It frames the core challenge for AI ethics as moving engineers beyond this functional ethic to consider the politics embedded in their data, algorithms, and systems.
*   **Infrastructure as Politics**: As AI becomes embedded in hiring, lending, healthcare, and criminal justice, it becomes critical infrastructure. Turner’s demand to treat code "as ordinary as tarmac" is a call for democratic deliberation, regulation, and design accountability, moving AI from a "magical" product to a public utility.
*   **Critique of Solutionism**: The "totalizing" utopian promise to "solve" bias or misinformation with a technical tool mirrors the very thinking Turner critiques. His call for "partial optimism" supports incremental, politically-aware approaches to AI governance rather than techno-silver-bullet fantasies.
*   **Corporate Mythology**: Understanding the sincere, communalist roots of "Don't Be Evil" or Meta's mission to "connect the world" helps explain their blind spots and resistance to external critique. They are operating from a religious-like conviction, not pure cynicism.

## Key Quotes

1.  **"The communes were, ironically, extraordinarily conservative. When you take away bureaucracy and hierarchy and politics, you take away the ability to negotiate the distribution of resources on explicit terms. And you replace it with charisma, with cool, with shared but unspoken perceptions of power."**
    *   *Analysis: This is the core of Turner’s political critique. It identifies how the elimination of formal, political mechanisms does not create equality, but replaces them with informal, cultural power structures that are often more oppressive and less accountable.*

2.  **"You end up in this loop where what’s good for people is what’s good for Google, and vice versa."**
    *   *Analysis: A succinct diagnosis of the self-referential and self-justifying logic of corporate techno-utopianism. It shows how a sincere moral impulse ("providing information is good") becomes entangled with and instrumentalized by business interests.*

3.  **"The ethics of engineering are an ethics of: Does it work? If you make something that works, you’ve done the ethical thing. It’s up to other people to figure out the social mission for your object."**
    *   *Analysis: This quote crystallizes the "ethics of functionality." It explains why tech firms see themselves as amoral toolsmiths rather than political actors, and why they struggle to respond to critiques of social harm.*

4.  **"Engineers try to do politics by changing infrastructure... The engineering world doesn’t have a conception of how to intervene in debate that isn’t infrastructural."**
    *   *Analysis: This reveals the limited toolset of Silicon Valley for engagement with societal problems. The response to political crises (like misinformation) is to tweak the platform's architecture, not to engage in substantive political dialogue or cede control.*

5.  **"We need to start thinking about them [technologies] as ordinary as tarmac. And we need to develop institutional settings for thinking about how we want to make our traffic laws."**
    *   *Analysis: This is Turner’s prescriptive vision. It demands a demystification of technology and the creation of democratic, deliberative institutions (like public utility commissions) to govern it as essential infrastructure.*

6.  **"Burning Man is to the tech world what the nineteenth-century Protestant church was to the factory."**
    *   *Analysis: A brilliant and provocative analogy that frames Burning Man not as escape, but as ritual reinforcement of the Valley’s core values and labor structures, providing the utopian experience the workplace promises but cannot deliver.*
