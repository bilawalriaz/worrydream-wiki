# Costikyan 2002 - I Have No Words and I Must Design

## Core Thesis
Greg Costikyan argues that the field of game design is hampered by the lack of a precise, analytical vocabulary. The dominant term "gameplay" is a vague placeholder that obscures rather than illuminates what makes games function and engaging. The paper's central thesis is that to advance game design as a discipline and to communicate intelligently about games, we must dissect the medium into fundamental, observable components. Costikyan posits that the essence of a game, across all its wildly varying forms, is not its technology or presentation but its structure as an **interactive system of purposeful decision-making, defined by goals and conflict**. The nuance lies in his rejection of "interaction" alone (as a light switch is interactive but not a game) and his expansion of "goals" beyond explicit win states to include player-defined objectives in "software toys."

## Historical Context
The paper emerges from the early 2000s, a period of rapid growth and commercialization for the video game industry, but a time when game studies as an academic discipline was in its infancy. The field was dominated by practitioner knowledge and anecdote. The primary reference point Costikyan cites is Chris Crawford's 1982 *The Art of Computer Game Design*, which, while foundational, offered limited theoretical tools. The context was one where games were beginning to be taken seriously as a cultural form, but lacked a critical language to analyze them beyond journalism or marketing hype. Costikyan is responding to a problem articulated by the title itself—a paraphrase of a line from the sci-fi short story "Vintage Season" by Kuttner and [[moore-1966-autotelic-responsive-environments-and-exceptional-children|Moore]] (1947), suggesting a deep, inexpressible intuition about an experience for which one lacks the proper terminology. This was the state of game design: practitioners knew *what* felt good, but couldn't articulate *why*.

## Key Contributions
1.  **A Foundational Taxonomy:** The paper introduces a clear, hierarchical framework for analyzing games: **Interaction → Goals → Conflict → Resources**. This moves the conversation from a monolithic "gameplay" to discrete, examinable pillars.
2.  **The Critical Distinction between Puzzle and Game:** Reviving Crawford's distinction but refining it, Costikyan defines the core dividing line as **purposeful interaction**. A puzzle is static; a game is an interactive system where the state changes in response to player action aimed at a goal.
3.  **The Goal Spectrum:** He expands the concept of goals beyond explicit "victory conditions." He analyzes the spectrum from the clear, built-in goals of *Chess*, to the player-defined goals of "software toys" like *SimCity*, to the implicit, social, and emergent goals of role-playing games (RPGs) and MUDs. This was a crucial framework for understanding sandbox and emergent gameplay.
4.  **Conflict as the Engine of Engagement:** Costikyan identifies **conflict** as the essential element that gives goals meaning and makes interaction engaging. Conflict can be oppositional (other players, AI), environmental, or even a struggle with the player's own limitations (as in puzzle-platformers). Without conflict, goals feel hollow.
5.  **Resource Management as a Universal Element:** He argues that most meaningful decision-making in games revolves around managing **resources** (time, information, health, currency, units, etc.), making it a key lens for analysis.

## Methodology
The methodology is **theoretical and polemical**, constructed through **conceptual analysis** and **critical observation**. Costikyan does not present empirical data or run experiments. Instead, he builds an argument by:
*   **Deconstruction:** Breaking down the vague, industry-standard term "gameplay."
*   **Taxonomy:** Proposing a structured set of categories (Interaction, Goals, Conflict, Resources).
*   **Comparative Analysis:** Using contrasting examples (*SimCity* vs. *SimEarth*, adventure games as puzzles vs. games, RPGs vs. MUDs) to test and refine his categories.
*   **Reductio ad Absurdum:** Showing that the lack of this vocabulary leads to poor design outcomes (e.g., *SimEarth*'s failure due to lack of player-directed goals).
The paper reads as a manifesto, designed to persuade fellow designers and critics to adopt a new way of thinking and speaking about their craft.

## Influence
This paper became a seminal text in game studies and design education. Its direct influence is pervasive:
*   **Academic Game Studies:** It provided an early, accessible theoretical framework that was widely taught and cited in the first wave of game studies programs. It helped legitimize the analytical approach to games.
*   **Design Practice:** The vocabulary it proposed (goals, conflict, resources) became standard in design bibles and postmortems. It gave designers a shared language to dissect mechanics and diagnose design flaws. Concepts like "player agency" and "emergent gameplay" are easier to discuss within its framework.
*   **Lineage:** It built directly on Crawford and laid groundwork for later theorists like Jesper Juul (formalism, rules) and Ian Bogost (procedural rhetoric). It is a key node in the lineage from Crawford's early craft manual to the sophisticated formal analysis of the 2010s.
*   **Specific Citation:** It is frequently cited in works discussing the definition of games, the nature of interactivity, and the design of goal systems, from Raph Koster's *A Theory of Fun* to academic journals like *Game Studies*.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
*   **Seymour [[papert-1980-mindstorms-1st-ed|Papert]] (1980) *Mindstorms*:** Both Costikyan and [[papert-1980-mindstorms-1st-ed|Papert]] argue for the importance of **active, engaged, goal-directed learning through construction and interaction**. Papert's "microworlds" and "objects-to-think-with" are analogous to Costikyan's "software toys" and interactive systems. Both see the power in the user shaping the medium, not just consuming it. The difference is that Papert's focus is epistemological (how we learn), while Costikyan's is aesthetic and ludological (how we engage and are entertained).
*   **John Dewey (implied) / Alan Kay (1972) *Personal Computer*:** This paper aligns with the vision of the computer as an **interactive medium for exploration and expression**, not just calculation. Kay's "Dynabook" and the Smalltalk environment were designed for user agency and playful exploration, the very qualities Costikyan identifies in successful games. The paper can be seen as applying the humanist-computing ideals of Kay to the specific domain of games.
*   **Douglas [[engelbart-1960-games-that-teach-the-fundamentals-of-computer-operation|Engelbart]] (1962) *Augmenting Human Intellect*:** Engelbart's core problem is augmenting human capability in complex tasks through interactive tools. Costikyan's games are, in a sense, "augmented" environments for practicing decision-making under constraints. Both are deeply concerned with the nature of **purposeful human-computer interaction**, though Engelbart's is utilitarian and Costikyan's is for leisure and art.
*   **Douglas [[hofstadter-2001-analogy-as-core-of-cognition|Hofstadter]] (2001) *Analogy as the Core of Cognition*:** Costikyan's entire project is an exercise in building a **framework for analogy and comparison** across disparate games. By identifying common structural elements (goals, conflict, resources), he enables the player or designer to see that a board wargame, a MUD, and a platformer are variations on a theme. This allows for the transfer of design insights across genres, much as [[hofstadter-2001-analogy-as-core-of-cognition|Hofstadter]] argues analogy underlies all thought.
*   **[[lockhart-2002-a-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]] (2002) *A Mathematician's Lament*:** Both papers are **passionate critiques of how their respective fields are misunderstood and poorly taught**. [[lockhart-2002-a-mathematicians-lament|Lockhart]] laments the focus on mechanical procedure in math education, which kills the spirit of inquiry and beauty. Costikyan laments the vague, unanalytical language of game design, which obscures the craft and makes it harder to create or appreciate meaningful interactive experiences. Both argue for a return to first principles—artistic intent, aesthetic experience, and fundamental structure.

## Modern Relevance
Costikyan's framework remains profoundly relevant, especially as interactive systems extend beyond traditional games into AI, knowledge work, and education.
*   **AI and Procedural Generation:** His analysis of goals and conflict is essential for designing meaningful AI behavior. An NPC or an AI assistant that only reacts without purpose is a "light switch." His taxonomy helps define what makes an AI interaction engaging: does it present the user with meaningful goals, appropriate conflict, and scarce resources to manage?
*   **Knowledge Management & Productivity Software:** This lens can critique tools like note-taking apps or project management software. Are they mere "software toys" (like SimCity) that rely on the user to define goals, or do they provide structured workflows (like SimEarth) that may stifle user agency? The most powerful tools, like a blank text editor or a set of LEGO, provide a low floor, high ceiling, and wide walls—allowing users to set their own goals within a system of constraints (resources) and challenges (conflict).
*   **Education:** Gamification often fails because it applies superficial goals (points, badges) without understanding the interplay of meaningful conflict, resource scarcity, and player agency that Costikyan describes. His framework is a tool for designing educational experiences that are intrinsically engaging, not just decorated with game-like trappings.
*   **AI Alignment and Human-AI Interaction:** As we design AI partners, his emphasis on **shared goals** and the nature of **conflict** (is the AI an opponent, a tool, a co-player?) becomes critical. How do we design interactive systems where the AI's "goals" and the human's goals can productively align or conflict to create a valuable dynamic?

## Key Quotes
1.  **"Saying 'it has good gameplay' is about as useful as saying 'that's a good book.' Calling something 'good' doesn't help us understand what's good about it..."**
    *Analytical Commentary: This is the inciting problem. It highlights the gap between intuitive appreciation and analytical understanding that the paper seeks to bridge.*
2.  **"A puzzle is static. A game is interactive... But still, Crawford’s distinction is a useful one."**
    *Analytical Commentary: This establishes the foundational binary. The later refinement—that interaction must be *purposeful*—is the crucial next step in his argument.*
3.  **"What makes a thing into a game is the need to make decisions."**
    *Analytical Commentary: This is the core of his thesis. It moves the definition from the medium (computer) or the feeling (fun) to the cognitive activity: purposeful choice under constraint.*
4.  **"SimCity is a good toy – it’s susceptible to so many goal-directed behaviors that it is a good game despite the fact that it has no inherent 'win state'..."**
    *Analytical Commentary: This nuance on goals is vital. It explains the success of sandbox and emergent gameplay, decoupling "game" from "built-in victory condition."*
5.  **"Without conflict, the goals have no meaning."**
    *Analytical Commentary: This succinctly states why mere interactivity or mere goal-setting is insufficient. Conflict is the engine that converts potential into kinetic engagement.*
6.  **"We have to do this if we want to communicate with one another about games. And we have to do it if we want to design better ones."**
    *Analytical Commentary: This reveals the paper's ultimate purpose: it is a call for professionalization and discipline. Language shapes thought; a better vocabulary enables better design.*
