# Bush 1945 - As We May Think

## Core Thesis

Vannevar Bush argues that the central problem facing post-war civilization is not the production of knowledge but its retrieval and use. The rate of scientific publication has far outstripped humanity's ability to store, index, search, and synthesize that knowledge. Existing methods of information management — alphabetical filing, hierarchical classification, linear publication — are relics of a pre-industrial age, wholly inadequate to the scale of the modern record. Bush contends that emerging technologies (microfilm, dry photography, photocells, thermionic tubes, relay logic) make it possible to build machines that augment human memory and reasoning, not by replacing creative thought, but by mechanizing the repetitive, associative, and retrieval-intensive parts of intellectual work. The paper's culminating proposal is the **memex**: a mechanized private library and filing system that stores information on microfilm and, crucially, allows the user to build persistent associative links ("trails") between documents — the first published description of what would later be called hypertext.

## Historical Context

Bush wrote in the closing months of World War II. As Director of the U.S. Office of Scientific Research and Development (OSRD), he had overseen some 6,000 scientists working on radar, the proximity fuse, the atomic bomb, and countless other wartime projects. The war had demonstrated that organized, well-funded scientific collaboration could produce transformative results at unprecedented speed. But Bush saw that the same scientists who had mastered the physical world were drowning in the information world.

The immediate backdrop is the explosion of scientific publication. Bush notes that even specialists in narrow fields cannot keep up with the current literature. He invokes Mendel's forgotten paper on genetics — lost to the world for a generation because it didn't reach the right readers — as a paradigmatic case of systemic failure. The problem is not that we publish too much, but that "publication has been extended far beyond our present ability to make real use of the record."

Technologically, the war had accelerated the development of:

- **Microfilm and microphotography** (used for military intelligence, documentation)
- **Photocells and television** (electronic image transmission)
- **Thermionic tubes / vacuum tubes** (radar, communications, early computing)
- **Relay-based computing machines** (Babbage's dream finally becoming feasible)
- **Punched-card tabulation** (Hollerith machines used in census and military logistics)

Bush himself had built differential analyzers and understood computation. He had also invented the "rapid selector" — a microfilm-based retrieval device. He saw clearly that complexity and reliability were no longer synonymous, as they had been in Babbage's day: "The world has arrived at an age of cheap complex devices of great reliability; and something is bound to come of it."

Before Bush, the dominant paradigm for organizing knowledge was classification — Dewey Decimal, Library of Congress, faceted classification systems. These are hierarchical: a book goes in one place on one shelf. Bush recognized this as the fundamental limitation and proposed to transcend it.

## Key Contributions

### 1. The Problem Statement: Information Overload as Civilizational Threat

Bush was among the first to frame information overload not as an inconvenience but as a structural failure of civilization. He argued that the increasing specialization of science creates a paradox: progress requires deeper specialization, but specialization makes cross-disciplinary synthesis harder, and "the effort to bridge between disciplines is correspondingly superficial." This framing — that the tools of knowledge management shape what knowledge is possible — would become central to the entire tradition of augmented intellect.

### 2. Associative Indexing as Alternative to Hierarchical Classification

The most radical conceptual contribution. Bush recognized that "the human mind does not work" by alphabetical or numerical filing. "It operates by association. With one item in its grasp, it snaps instantly to the next that is suggested by the association of thoughts, in accordance with some intricate web of trails carried by the cells of the brain." He proposed that machines should mimic this associative structure rather than imposing rigid taxonomies. This is the conceptual seed of hypertext, hyperlinks, and the entire non-linear model of information.

### 3. The Memex

The memex is a desk-based device where an individual stores all their books, records, and communications on microfilm. Its key features:

- **Massive storage** — 5,000 pages per day input, hundreds of years to fill
- **Microfilm-based retrieval** — code-based lookup, rapid scrolling at variable speeds (1, 10, 100 pages)
- **Multiple projection screens** — compare documents side-by-side without "emerging from the system"
- **Marginal annotations** — add handwritten notes via stylus, stored with the document
- **Associative trails** — the core innovation: link any two documents together with a named connection, build chains of links that form a trail through a body of knowledge
- **Trail sharing** — copy a trail and pass it to another person's memex
- **Trail blazers** — "a new profession of trail blazers, those who find delight in the task of establishing useful trails through the enormous mass of the common record"

### 4. Vision of Wearable, Ubiquitous Capture

Bush imagined a camera "worn on the forehead, a lump a little larger than a walnut" — taking 3mm stereoscopic photographs triggered by a cord down the sleeve. This is strikingly close to Google Glass, GoPro, or lifelogging cameras. He envisioned hands-free, always-on documentation for scientists in the field.

### 5. Speech-to-Text Input

He described combining Bell Labs' Vocoder (speech recognition) with the stenotype to create "a machine which types when talked to" — voice-to-text input, decades before it became practical.

### 6. Machines for Formal Logic

Bush foresaw that "whenever thought for a time runs along an accepted groove, there is an opportunity for the machine." He described relay-circuit machines that could manipulate premises according to formal logic — an anticipation of both programming languages and, distantly, expert systems and automated reasoning.

### 7. Direct Brain-Machine Interface (Speculative)

In the final section, Bush speculated about intercepting nerve impulses directly — "Might not these currents be intercepted?" — to bypass mechanical input entirely. He noted that bone conduction already delivered sound to deaf patients and that the electroencephalograph produced traces "which bear some relation to the electrical phenomena going on in the brain itself." This is a remarkably early articulation of the brain-computer interface concept.

## Methodology

Bush's method is **speculative engineering** grounded in existing technology. He does not conduct experiments or present data. Instead, he:

1. **Establishes the problem** (information overload, failed retrieval) with concrete examples (Mendel, the scale of publication)
2. **Surveys available technologies** (microfilm, photocells, tubes, relays, dry photography) with enough technical specificity to demonstrate feasibility
3. **Projects forward** — extends known trends by a generation, identifying what is plausible versus fantastical
4. **Synthesizes into a vision** — the memex, constructed entirely from near-term technologies
5. **Distinguishes creative from repetitive thought** — explicitly preserves human agency and creativity while mechanizing the mechanical

The argument is structured as a progressive build-up: recording technology (camera, microphone), storage technology (microfilm), input technology (speech-to-text, computation), retrieval technology (associative indexing), culminating in the memex as an integrated system. Bush is careful to note that "for mature thought there is no mechanical substitute" — the machine assists but does not replace the human thinker.

## Influence

### Direct Lineage: Hypertext and the Web

Ted Nelson, who coined the terms "hypertext" and "hypermedia" in the 1960s, explicitly cited Bush's memex as his inspiration. Nelson's Project Xanadu was conceived as a realization of Bush's vision — a system of linked, transcludable documents. Doug Engelbart, who invented the mouse, collaborative editing, and the oN-Line System (NLS), read Bush's article in 1945 while stationed in the Philippines and later wrote that it "launched" his career. Engelbart's 1968 "Mother of All Demos" was a direct implementation of many memex concepts: linked documents, collaborative trail-building, multimedia information retrieval.

Tim Berners-Lee's World Wide Web implements a subset of Bush's vision — hyperlinked documents accessible from a personal workstation. But Berners-Lee's web lacks several memex features that Nelson and others have noted: trails are not persistent or shareable in the memex sense, links are unidirectional, there is no built-in annotation system, and there is no notion of a "profession of trail blazers" as a first-class activity.

### The Engelbart Connection

Engelbart's 1962 research program "Augmenting Human Intellect: A Conceptual Framework" is the most systematic intellectual descendant of Bush's article. Engelbart read Bush in 1945 and spent the next two decades building exactly the kind of knowledge-work augmentation system Bush had described — but with interactive computing, real-time collaboration, and direct manipulation rather than microfilm.

### The Kay Connection

Alan Kay's vision of the Dynabook (1972) — a personal, portable computer for children to explore and create knowledge — extends Bush's memex into interactive, graphical, and educational territory. Kay acknowledged Bush as a precursor. The Dynabook added programmability, visual simulation, and direct manipulation to Bush's essentially passive (read-and-link) model.

### The Licklider Connection

J.C.R. Licklider's 1960 paper "Man-Computer Symbiosis" directly builds on Bush's framing of the problem (human cognition overwhelmed by information volume) and proposes real-time interactive computing as the solution — where Bush had imagined microfilm and mechanical relays, Licklider envisioned timesharing and interactive terminals.

### The Nelson Connection

Ted Nelson's work is perhaps the most direct technical descendant. Xanadu's model of bidirectional, persistent links, transclusion (embedding one document within another by reference), and visible connection structure is essentially the memex trail system taken to its logical conclusion. Nelson has described Bush as the fountainhead of the entire hypertext tradition.

### The Sutherland Connection

Ivan Sutherland's Sketchpad (1963), the first interactive graphical program, implemented a different aspect of Bush's vision — not retrieval and linking, but direct manipulation of structured information. Sutherland demonstrated that computers could be not just calculators but tools for thinking, an idea implicit in Bush's distinction between creative and repetitive thought.

- **[[engelbart-1962-augmenting-human-intellect|Engelbart 1962]]** — Engelbart read "As We May Think" in 1945 and called it a launching point. - **[[kay-1972-personal-dynamic-media|Kay 1972]]** — Kay's Dynabook extends the memex from a passive retrieval device into an active creative medium. Bush imagined reading and linking; Kay imagined authoring, simulating, and programming. Both share the conviction that personal computing should augment individual thought.

- **[[nelson-1974-computer-lib|Nelson 1974]]** — Nelson's hypertext vision is the most technically faithful descendant of the memex. His insistence on bidirectional links, visible structure, and "trail blazers" as a profession maps directly onto Bush's sections 7-8. Nelson has consistently cited Bush as the origin of hypertext.

- **[[licklider-1960-man-computer-symbiosis|Licklider 1960]]** — Licklider's "Man-Computer Symbiosis" picks up exactly where Bush left off: the gap between human cognition and information volume. Licklider's key advance is replacing Bush's batch-oriented, mechanical vision with real-time interactive computing.

- **[[sutherland-1963-sketchpad|Sutherland 1963]]** — Sketchpad realized one dimension of Bush's vision that the memex did not address: direct graphical manipulation of structured objects. Bush's memex is essentially a reading and linking machine; Sutherland showed that the computer could also be a drawing and modeling machine.

## Modern Relevance

### Knowledge Management and Personal Information Management

Bush's core problem — the mismatch between information volume and human retrieval capacity — has only intensified. Modern tools like Obsidian, Roam Research, Logseq, and Notion implement memex-like associative linking (backlinks, bidirectional links, graph views) and have attracted large user communities organized around exactly the "trail blazer" ethos Bush described. The "tools for thought" movement is a direct descendant.

### AI and Augmented Cognition

Bush's distinction between creative and repetitive thought maps onto the modern debate about AI augmentation. He argued that "essentially repetitive thought" should be mechanized while "mature thought" has "no mechanical substitute." Modern LLMs challenge this boundary — they can generate novel-seeming text and synthesize across domains — but the question Bush raised (what should be mechanized, what should remain human?) remains the central question of AI ethics and design.

### Brain-Computer Interfaces

Bush's speculative closing section on direct nerve-signal interception anticipates Neuralink, BrainGate, and the entire BCI field. He was remarkably precise about the mechanism (electrical induction from nerve channels) and the philosophical framing (bypass the "cumbersomeness" of mechanical I/O).

### The Web's Unfinished Promise

The World Wide Web implements Bush's vision of globally accessible linked documents but fails to implement several key memex features: persistent named trails, trail sharing, local annotation, and first-class trail-blazing as a profession. Many modern tools (Hypothesis for annotation, Readwise for trail-building, various "second brain" methodologies) attempt to fill this gap. The Web, as built, is closer to a library without a librarian than to the memex Bush envisioned.

### Wearable Computing and Lifelogging

Bush's forehead camera — hands-free, always-on, triggered by a gesture — anticipated wearable cameras, lifelogging (Gordon Bell's MyLifeBits project explicitly cited Bush), and eventually smartphone cameras that are always at hand. The "Memex Revisited" essay Bush wrote in 1967 updated the vision for electronic computers but was still ahead of what was then buildable.

## Key Quotes

> "There is a growing mountain of research. But there is increased evidence that we are being bogged down today as specialization extends. The investigator is staggered by the findings and conclusions of thousands of other workers — conclusions which he cannot find time to grasp, much less to remember, as they appear."

> "Professionally our methods of transmitting and reviewing the results of research are generations old and by now are totally inadequate for their purpose."

> "The human mind does not work that way. It operates by association. With one item in its grasp, it snaps instantly to the next that is suggested by the association of thoughts, in accordance with some intricate web of trails carried by the cells of the brain."

> "Selection by association, rather than by indexing, may yet be mechanized."

> "Consider a future device for individual use, which is a sort of mechanized private file and library. It needs a name, and, to coin one at random, 'memex' will do."

> "The process of tying two items together is the important thing."

> "There is a new profession of trail blazers, those who find delight in the task of establishing useful trails through the enormous mass of the common record. The inheritance from the master becomes, not only his additions to the world's record, but for his disciples the entire scaffolding by which they were erected."

> "For mature thought there is no mechanical substitute. But creative thought and essentially repetitive thought are very different things. For the latter there are, and may be, powerful mechanical aids."

> "The world has arrived at an age of cheap complex devices of great reliability; and something is bound to come of it."

> "His excursions may be more enjoyable if he can reacquire the privilege of forgetting the manifold things he does not need to have immediately at hand, with some assurance that he can find them again if they prove important."
