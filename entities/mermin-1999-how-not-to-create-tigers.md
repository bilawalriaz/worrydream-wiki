# Mermin 1999 - How Not to Create Tigers

## Core Thesis
N. David Mermin's "How Not to Create Tigers" is a satirical polemic disguised as a transcribed conversation. Its core thesis is a profound rejection of top-down, goal-oriented ("strategic") funding and management of scientific research. Mermin argues that this approach is not merely inefficient but fundamentally misconceived—it is a form of "creationism" that misunderstands the nature of discovery. Scientific breakthroughs are not products to be commissioned or inevitable endpoints of a directed plan; they are emergent, unpredictable outcomes of a free, curious, and exploratory intellectual environment. The paper contends that by attempting to surgically define and extract specific "golden eggs" (practical outcomes), funders and policymakers are anesthetizing and destroying the very "geese" (the culture of fundamental inquiry) capable of laying them.

The nuances are critical: Mermin is not arguing against all purposeful work, but against the imposition of that purpose onto basic research. He distinguishes between a planned hike (legitimate) and insisting on the outcome of a complex, evolving process like natural selection (fatal). The "tiger" is the ultimate metaphor for an emergent, unplanned, and awe-inspiring result of a complex system—a result that a grant proposal for "tiger creation" could never anticipate or produce.

## Historical Context
The paper is a direct response to the political and funding climate in United States science in the mid-to-late 1990s. The immediate context is the 1993 cancellation of the Superconducting Super Collider (SSC), a monumental particle physics project Mermin's alter-ego, Professor Mozart, had invested in internationally. This event symbolized a larger shift in national priorities.

More broadly, Mermin critiques the "Gingrich revolution" ethos of the time, which emphasized "strategic research" and measurable, short-term returns on federal investment. This was part of a decades-long trend towards applying corporate or engineering management principles to the process of basic science. The ghost of the Soviet Union is explicitly invoked, not for its atheism, but for its "creationism in the economic sphere"—the centrally planned economy that assumed complex, adaptive systems (like agriculture or society) could be engineered from a desired endpoint. Mermin sees the same flawed logic now being applied to the ecosystem of scientific discovery.

## Key Contributions
1.  **The "Creationism" Analogy for Strategic Research:** Mermin’s most striking contribution is reframing the debate over science funding by drawing a parallel to creationism. This equates "intelligent design" in biology with "intelligent design" in science policy—the belief that discovery is a process of executing a plan rather than navigating a landscape of the unknown.
2.  **The "Free-Range Goose" Metaphor:** He provides a powerful, memorable image for the culture of fundamental research: the "free-range goose" that lays golden eggs (discoveries) "unmolested in the tall grass." The metaphor captures the fragility and autonomy of the creative process.
3.  **The University as Monastery:** In a profound twist, Mermin re-imagines the university's role. In an era of applied, strategic research, the university's primary mission becomes preservation—a repository for the "flame of learning" during a "dark night," akin to monasteries preserving literacy during the European Dark Ages. This is a pessimistic but potent argument for the long-term custodial value of pure academia.
4.  **A Defense of Curiosity-Driven Research as a Systemic Process:** Mermin moves beyond a simple defense of "basic research." He articulates it as a necessary system where not knowing the outcome is a feature, not a bug. The "next miracle" is intrinsically unplannable, and a healthy system must support the conditions for its occurrence.

## Methodology
The argument is structured as a **design fiction** or **satirical dialogue**. Mermin adopts the persona of a narrator recording a conversation with his brilliant, disillusioned, and hyper-articulate colleague, "Professor Mozart" (a playful nod to W.A. Mozart, evoking both genius and a certain chaotic creativity). This methodology achieves several goals:
*   **Emotional Persuasion:** It allows Mermin to express visceral frustration and melancholy through Mozart, making the argument more resonant than a dry editorial.
*   **Pedagogical Clarity:** The Socratic dialogue format (Mermin as the naive questioner, Mozart as the clarifying sage) breaks down complex ideas into digestible, dramatic exchanges.
*   **Ironic Distance:** The satirical frame allows Mermin to make extreme claims (comparing science policy to Stalinist agriculture) while maintaining a layer of artistic protection.
*   **Historical Anchoring:** The narrative is set at the time of writing (1995) but published in 1999, allowing Mermin to comment on the ongoing relevance of these ideas. The fictional disappearance and return of Mozart adds to the fable-like quality.

The argument is **polemical and philosophical**, not empirical. It is a critique of an epistemological error in policy—misunderstanding the nature of knowledge creation.

## Influence
Within the physics community, the article likely served as a sharp, popular articulation of sentiments many researchers felt privately, giving them a shared metaphor and voice. Its influence is less in citation-based academic literature and more in the discourse around science policy, education, and the philosophy of research. It is frequently referenced in essays and blog posts defending fundamental science and criticizing metrics-driven research assessment (e.g., the "impact factor" mentality).

The article's ideas resonate strongly with later works that critique the industrialization of knowledge, such as Robert P. Crease's historical reflections on physics and Benoit Mandelbrot's critiques of applied mathematics. Its central metaphor has become part of the toolkit for those arguing against overly directive funding bodies.

## Modern Relevance
The paper is strikingly prescient and relevant to contemporary debates in AI, science, and knowledge work.
*   **AI Research Funding:** The field is dominated by a frantic race to build Artificial General Intelligence (AGI), often framed as a strategic goal. This attracts massive, targeted funding. Mermin's critique warns that this "creationist" approach risks creating a brittle field focused on chasing specific, hype-driven benchmarks, while potentially neglecting the serendipitous, "free-range" exploration that could lead to more profound and unexpected understanding of intelligence.
*   **Metrics and "Strategic" Priorities:** Today's "strategic research" takes the form of grand challenges, mission-oriented institutes, and research proposals evaluated by their "societal impact." This mirrors the climate Mermin decries. The pressure to demonstrate immediate relevance can stifle high-risk, high-reward research.
*   **Education:** The push for STEM education focused on job outcomes and standardized testing echoes the desire for a direct, purposeful path from education to a pre-defined economic goal. This risks creating technicians, not innovators, and stifles the curiosity that drives discovery.
*   **Knowledge Management:** The paper is a stark warning against overly systematized or "strategic" approaches to innovation management. True innovation, like Mermin's tiger, is emergent and cannot be engineered into a corporate R&D pipeline through mandatory brainstorming sessions or prescribed processes.

## Key Quotes
1.  **"Proponents of strategic research hold the creationist belief that science works by deciding where we want to get and then going there."**
    *   *Analytical Note:* This is the thesis in one sentence. It powerfully links two seemingly disparate ideas—religious creationism and science funding policy—to expose a shared flaw: a refusal to accept the emergent, non-linear nature of complex systems.
2.  **"No immortal hand or eye could frame a strategic plan for that fearful symmetry."**
    *   *Analytical Note:* A brilliant repurposing of William Blake's "The Tyger." Mermin uses it to state that the tiger (a major scientific discovery) is fundamentally beyond intentional design, belonging instead to a realm of emergent beauty and terror that can only be stumbled upon.
3.  **"I am awaiting the day when people remember the fact that discovery does not work by deciding what you want and then discovering it."**
    *   *Analytical Note:* This cuts to the heart of the epistemological error. It separates the *process* of discovery (open exploration) from the *product* (the discovery itself), which can only be known after the fact.
4.  **"We are trapped in an epoch of stifling creationism."**
    *   *Analytical Note:* This reframes the current funding climate not as progressive or modern, but as regressive and pseudo-scientific, bound by a dogma that denies the unpredictability of true discovery.
5.  **"As the monasteries preserved literacy during the Dark Ages, so must the universities bend to the task of keeping learning alive during the dark night of strategic research."**
    *   *Analytical Note:* This is the most sobering and prophetic line. It recasts the university's mission from progress to preservation, a drastic downshift necessitated by a hostile intellectual climate.
6.  **"If I knew that," he proclaimed, as he disappeared into the freight elevator, "there'd be little point in doing it!"**
    *   *Analytical Note:* The ultimate anti-strategic statement. The value of thinking lies precisely in not knowing the outcome in advance. This principle is the antithesis of a planned grant proposal.
7.  **"To keep learning alive."**
    *   *Analytical Note:* Spoken by Mermin as he echoes Mozart's point. The phrase is simple, poignant, and reduces the mission of the university to its most essential, elemental form: sustenance of knowledge itself, stripped of all strategic goals.
