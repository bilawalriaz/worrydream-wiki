# Kennedy 1956 - Administration of Research in a Research Corporation

## Core Thesis
The paper argues that the "research corporation," a novel post-war institutional form, emerged to solve a specific crisis: the overspecialization of existing research institutions (universities, industrial labs) left them incapable of tackling complex, large-scale problems that required integrating diverse disciplines. Its core thesis is that the central administrative challenge of this new form is not managing traditional research, but enabling and rewarding a distinct, crucial activity called **"search"**—the collaborative, often chaotic process of establishing a shared conceptual framework for understanding a problem. The authors contend that administrators and researchers fundamentally misunderstand each other because they operate with different "cultures" and value different types of work. Effective administration requires a shared tool (they suggest a hierarchical planning method) to make the process of "search" visible, communicable, and manageable, thereby bridging the gap between administration and specialized research, and between research and development.

## Historical Context
The paper was written in 1956, at the height of the Cold War and the beginning of the "Big Science" era. The rapid technological growth since World War II—symbolized by projects like the atomic bomb and the emerging space race—created complex, systemic problems (e.g., command-and-control systems, nuclear strategy, large-scale logistics) that no single discipline could solve. Institutions like the RAND Corporation (where both authors worked) were founded specifically as "research corporations" to address these needs for the U.S. government.

The existing institutional landscape was seen as inadequate. Universities were characterized as producing narrow specialists. Industrial labs were driven by immediate product timelines. Government labs were bureaucratic. The research corporation was an experimental "institutional innovation" meant to be more agile and integrative. The paper's historical significance lies in its early, self-aware diagnosis of the pains of creating interdisciplinary work within a formal, funded structure. It diagnoses a problem—communication failure due to cultural and methodological chasms—that remains central to complex project management today.

## Key Contributions
1.  **Conceptual Distinction: "Search" vs. "Research" vs. "Development."** This is the paper's most enduring contribution. "Research" is problem-solving within an established framework and disciplinary ritual. "Development" is applying a research-derived framework to create practical solutions. "Search" is the meta-activity of *creating the framework itself*—identifying the right questions and the right way to think about them. The authors argue the research corporation exists primarily to perform "search" on large-scale problems.
2.  **Identification of the "Interdisciplinary Problem" as a Cultural One.** They move beyond calling for teamwork to analyze *why* it fails. The issue is not just technical but rooted in deep-seated "cultures" of disciplines—preferred methodologies, terminologies, and values. Specialists resist framework-challenging "search" because it threatens their established expertise and ritualistic comfort.
3.  **Diagnosis of Structural Incentive Misalignment.** They identify a critical flaw: academic and professional reward systems ("our research culture is based upon reward to the individual, not to the team") directly conflict with the needs of interdisciplinary "search," which requires collective, often anonymous, framework-building work. This makes interdisciplinary teams inherently unstable.
4.  **Proposal for a Communication Tool.** They advocate for a specific planning method (a hierarchical tree-structure for breaking down problems) not as a solution in itself, but as a *shared language* to make the elusive "search" process concrete and discussable between administrators, researchers, and developers. It's an early model of what we might now call a "collaborative sense-making" tool.

## Methodology
The argument is **theoretical and observational**, grounded in the authors' direct participant-observation experience as an administrator and a department head within a real research corporation (RAND). It is essentially a piece of organizational epistemology. They build their case through:
1.  **Philosophical Framing:** Starting with a broad definition of research as "investigation... to predict and, by one’s own behavior, to control the... environment," they situate the research corporation within the historical arc of science from generalist "natural philosophers" to modern specialized disciplines.
2.  **Phenomenological Description:** They describe the lived experience of interdisciplinary work, emphasizing cultural friction, the instability of teams, and the communication gap between "search" activities and the administrator's need for progress metrics.
3.  **Conceptual Model-Building:** They introduce the "Search/Research/Development" triad as a diagnostic tool to reframe the problem.
4.  **Polemical Advocacy:** They argue forcefully for the necessity of "search" despite its apparent unproductiveness and for the need to redesign reward structures and communication methods to support it.

The paper is not empirical in the social science sense (no surveys, controlled experiments). Its authority derives from the authenticity of the insider perspective on a then-novel institutional form.

## Influence
While difficult to trace precise citation lineages, the paper's influence is seminal in the fields of **science and technology studies (STS), organizational theory, and innovation management.**
*   It prefigures and likely influenced later work on **paradigm shifts** (Thomas Kuhn, 1962) by describing the ritualistic nature of established disciplines and the disruptive need for new frameworks ("search").
*   Its diagnosis of interdisciplinary problems directly speaks to the field of **team science** and **collaborative research**, which has since grown into a major area of study in research policy.
*   The "Search/Research/Development" distinction provides a foundational lens for analyzing any innovation pipeline, influencing how R&D departments, think tanks, and corporate innovation labs conceptualize their work stages.
*   The paper is an early canonical text in understanding the specific **management philosophy of institutions like RAND**, which sought to blend academic freedom with mission-oriented, actionable research. It helps explain the "Rand style" of systems analysis.

## Modern Relevance
The paper's relevance has, if anything, intensified.
1.  **AI & [[cook-2000-how-complex-systems-fail|Complex Systems]] Research:** Modern AI research, especially in areas like artificial general intelligence or climate modeling, is the quintessential interdisciplinary "search" problem. It requires integrating [[hamming-1968-one-mans-view-of-computer-science|computer science]], neuroscience, philosophy, ethics, and domain sciences. The cultural clashes Kennedy describes (e.g., between empirical ML engineers and theoretical neuroscientists) are rampant.
2.  **Knowledge Management & Collaboration Tools:** The paper's call for a "tool... to improve communication" is prophetic. Today's tools (Slack, Miro, Notion, Confluence) are attempts to build the shared workspace Kennedy envisioned. However, these tools often still privilege discrete messages (research artifacts) over the fluid, iterative process of building a shared conceptual model (search).
3.  **Agile & Design Thinking:** Modern agile methodologies and design thinking are practical, if less theoretically grounded, responses to the same problem: how to enable teams to "search" for the right framework/solution in an uncertain, fast-moving environment. Kennedy's paper provides the deep organizational rationale for why these practices are necessary.
4.  **The "Mission-Driven" Organization:** Companies like Alphabet's "Other Bets," modern research hospitals, and climate tech startups are essentially contemporary research corporations. They face the exact tensions Kennedy outlines: rewarding collaborative search while needing to demonstrate tangible research outputs and eventual development outcomes.
5.  **Academic Reform:** The paper is a critique of the academic incentive structure (rewarding individual, publishable research) that remains painfully relevant. Calls to reward team science and interdisciplinary work echo Kennedy's diagnosis.

## Key Quotes

> **"Research tends to yield a set of unintegrated answers on components of the problem. The integration demanded by the problem must then be done by research administrators and directors, using the best framework available—usually the administrator’s own private framework. Again, no one is satisfied."**
> *Analysis: This succinctly captures the core dysfunction. Specialized research solves pieces; administrators [[air-force-1960-air-force-office-of-scientific-research|force]] them into a often-poor fit for the whole, pleasing neither side. It diagnoses a fundamental [[cook-2000-how-complex-systems-fail|flaw in top-dow]]n project management of complex systems.*

> **"The first conviction of the research specialist is that a problem can be factored in such a way that his particular specialty is the only important aspect... If all else fails, he will argue that the problem is not 'appropriate.'"**
> *Analysis: A brutally accurate description of disciplinary tribalism and defensive intellectual behavior. It explains why interdisciplinary meetings often devolve into turf wars or avoidance. The word "ritual" used elsewhere is key here.*

> **"But search is the lifeblood of a research corporation, because new tools come from new frameworks."**
> *Analysis: This is the paper's value statement. It elevates the messy, unproductive-feeling work of framework-building to the status of core institutional function. It argues that the institution's unique purpose is to nurture the very activity that traditional metrics undervalue.*

> **"Our research culture is based upon reward to the individual, not to the team. The perceptive research specialist knows this fact of life perfectly well; hence the interdisciplinary team is unstable."**
> *Analysis: Identifies the systemic incentive problem. It’s not about personality clashes; it’s about rational actors responding to a reward structure that punishes the very collaboration required for "search." This remains the single biggest barrier to interdisciplinary work.*

> **"Frameworks are most difficult to communicate because one quickly runs into fundamental philosophical and cultural issues."**
> *Analysis: Explains why communication between administrators and researchers, or across disciplines, is so hard. They aren't just debating facts; they are debating the validity of the entire lens through which facts are gathered and interpreted. This makes "search" conversations feel existential and threatening.*

> **"The appropriate expectation for interdisciplinary teams appears to be that they will be unstable associations."**
> *Analysis: A realistic, if cynical, management insight. It argues against trying to create permanent, stable interdisciplinary units and instead suggests expecting and managing fluid, temporary groupings that form around specific "search" tasks, acknowledging their inherent fragility.*
