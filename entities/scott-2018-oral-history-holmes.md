# Scott 2018 - Oral History (Holmes)

## Core Thesis
This document is not a traditional academic paper but an oral history interview, a genre of primary source. Its core "thesis" is therefore autobiographical and meta-scholarly: to reconstruct the intellectual biography and motivations of James C. Scott, explaining the development of his distinctive, fieldwork-rooted critique of state power, legibility, and high-modernist ideology. The nuance lies in how Scott frames his own career as a continuous rebellion against disciplinary boundaries and orthodox narratives, from his early studies of peasant resistance to his late-career "deep history" of states. The interview posits that his scholarly trajectory was not a series of discrete projects, but a cohesive, lifelong investigation into the ways subaltern groups evade, subvert, and survive the centralizing impulses of authority. It is a testament to the value of the contrarian intellectual squat.

## Historical Context
Scott's intellectual formation occurred within the specific crucible of mid-20th century [[scientific-american-1966-information-june-1966|American]] political science, which he found dominated by behavioralism and a state-centric, institutional focus. His work serves as a sustained intervention against this paradigm.

*   **Before:** The study of politics, especially in non-Western contexts, often focused on formal institutions, elite behavior, and modernization theory (the assumption that societies "progress" toward Western-style states and economies). Peasants were seen as a relic or a passive mass.
*   **Problem Being Solved:** Scott identified a blind spot in social science: the failure to see politics from below, particularly the sophisticated, everyday strategies of the non-literate and the marginalized. He sought to recover the agency of those historically denied a voice in official records and political theory.
*   **State of the Field:** The dominant frameworks were top-down. Marxist theory focused on class revolution, but often in a teleological way. Modernization theory pathologized non-state societies. Anthropology provided tools for fieldwork but was often siloed from political economy. Scott's work systematically bridged these divides, using anthropological depth to fuel political and historical critique.

## Key Contributions
The oral history illuminates the genesis of several foundational concepts in social science:

1.  **The "Moral Economy" Framework:** Adapted from E.P. Thompson, Scott's *The Moral Economy of the Peasant* (1976) argued that peasant rebellions were not just about poverty, but about a perceived violation of a community's ingrained norms of subsistence rights and paternalistic obligation by landlords and states. It moved analysis from pure economics to moral and cultural politics.
2.  **Infrapolitics and Everyday Resistance:** *Weapons of the Weak* (1985) and *Domination and the Arts of Resistance* (1990) formalized the study of "everyday forms of peasant resistance" – foot-dragging, dissimulation, false compliance, pilfering, satire. He coined "infrapolitics" to describe this hidden transcript of resistance, arguing it is the foundation upon which open defiance is built. This radically expanded what counts as political action.
3.  **The Critique of Legibility and High Modernism:** *Seeing Like a State* (1998) is perhaps his most influential work. It identifies the root cause of many state-made disasters (from Soviet collectivization to failed urban planning) as "high modernism": a combination of administrative ordering of nature/society, authoritarian state power, and a blindness to local, practical knowledge ("metis"). The state must make society "legible" (via censuses, maps, standardized names) to control and tax it, often destroying complex, adaptive systems in the process.
4.  **The Invention of "Zomia" and Escape from the State:** In *The Art of Not Being Governed* (2009), Scott applies his framework to Southeast Asian hill peoples ("Zomia"). He proposes a controversial, revolutionary thesis: that many non-state, non-literate highland societies are not "left behind" but are, in fact, deliberate refugees from lowland state projects, using their culture, mobility, and social fragmentation as tools of evasion.
5.  **A Deep History of the State as Predatory:** *Against the Grain* (2017) extends the critique backward, arguing that early states were coercive, extractive, and terrible places to live for the majority (the enslaved, the conquered). He overturns the civilizational narrative that sees the state as a benevolent organ of progress, showing that non-state "barbarians" often enjoyed better health and more autonomy.

## Methodology
Scott's methodology, as revealed in the interview, is a powerful synthesis:

*   **Radical Empiricism:** He insists on beginning from the granular evidence of fieldwork (e.g., living in a Malay village) or meticulous historical/archaeological detail, not grand theory. Theory must emerge from the ground up.
*   **Triangulation:** He describes using multiple sources – village diaries, interviews, census data, land records, archaeological findings – to triangulate truth, especially for groups that left few written records.
*   **Interdisciplinary Juxtaposition:** His work is a model of synthesis, drawing from anthropology, political science, history, economics, and ecology. The Program in Agrarian Studies was created to institutionalize this method.
*   **Contrarian Interpretation:** He deliberately seeks the "counter-narrative." If the state tells a story of progress, he looks for the evidence of destruction. If a culture is deemed "primitive," he looks for its sophisticated adaptive strategies. This is what he calls the "anarchist squint."

## Influence
Scott's influence is immense, fundamentally reshaping multiple disciplines:

*   **Political Science and Sociology:** It forced the study of politics to incorporate subaltern, everyday, and informal strategies. "Infrapolitics" is now a standard concept.
*   **Anthropology:** His work on the state provided a powerful macro-framework for ethnographers and pushed the discipline toward greater engagement with political economy and historical political ecology.
*   **Development Studies:** *Seeing Like a State* became a canonical critique of top-down development projects, championing local knowledge and humility.
*   **Historical Sociology:** *Against the Grain* has sparked major debates about the origins of social inequality, the nature of early states, and the relationship between agriculture and servitude.
*   **Institutional Impact:** The Yale Program in Agrarian Studies, which he co-founded, became a world-leading interdisciplinary model, training generations of scholars who continue this synthetic, field-based approach.

## Connections to Other Papers in the Collection
While not a direct dialogue, Scott's work resonates with several themes in the WorryDream collection:

*   **[[anderson-1972-more-is-different|Anderson]] 1972 (More is Different):** Scott’s work is a profound social-science analogue. He demonstrates that complex social systems (villages, state-fleeing societies) exhibit emergent properties and practical knowledge ("metis") that cannot be understood or replicated by top-down, reductionist schemes (high modernism). The whole is greater than, and different from, its legible parts.
*   **[[feynman-1974-cargo-cult-science|Feynman]] 1974 (Cargo Cult Science):** Scott's critique of high modernism is a critique of a form of social "cargo cult science." Planners and leaders perform the superficial ceremonies of rationality (plans, maps, statistics) but miss the underlying, complex reality (cultural meaning, local adaptation), leading to "failing" projects.
*   **[[thurston-1994-on-proof-and-progress-in-mathematics|Thurston]] 1994 (Proof and Progress):** There is an implicit parallel. [[thurston-1994-on-proof-and-progress-in-mathematics|Thurston]] discusses the social and human process of mathematical understanding beyond formal proof. Scott studies the vast realm of human social and political interaction that exists beyond the formal, legible structures of the state. Both value the informal, the tacit, and the human process over rigid formalism.
*   **[[kay-1972-a-personal-computer-for-children-of-all-ages|Kay]] 1972 (Personal Computer):** A provocative connection. Scott’s "art of not being governed" and his celebration of adaptive, small-scale, decentralized systems can be read as a socio-political argument for a kind of "personal" or community autonomy against monolithic, centralized architectures (be they states or mainframe computers). His Zomia is a network of small, adaptable nodes.

## Modern Relevance
Scott's work is acutely relevant to contemporary technology and society:

*   **AI and Data Legibility:** The drive for Big Data, algorithmic governance, and "smart cities" represents a new high-modernist project to make all social life legible to a computational apparatus. Scott’s framework warns of the dangers: the loss of privacy, the elimination of nuanced local context ("metis"), the creation of brittle systems, and the potential for authoritarian control.
*   **Platform Capitalism:** Giant tech platforms (like states) demand legibility for control and profit, standardizing human behavior and social interaction. The "infrapolitics" of users—workarounds, data obfuscation, using platforms against their intended design—mirrors the everyday resistance Scott documented.
*   **Decentralization and Anarchism:** Scott’s rehabilitation of anarchist thought speaks directly to contemporary debates about decentralization, peer-to-peer networks, and community self-governance. His work provides a historical and anthropological foundation for understanding the viability and value of systems that operate outside or against centralized hierarchies.
*   **Agrarianism and Food Systems:** The Program in Agrarian Studies' expansion into food connects to modern movements for food sovereignty, agroecology, and critiques of industrial agriculture, which often replicate the high-modernist, simplifying logic Scott critiques.

## Key Quotes

1.  **"The notion of infrapolitics... I meant it to be a term that would cover the whole panoply of low-profile maneuvers that are designed to minimize or to avoid direct confrontation with authority."**
    *   *Analysis:* This is the definitional statement for one of his most enduring concepts. It rebrands "not resisting" as a sophisticated political strategy, forcing a reevaluation of what political agency means for the powerless.

2.  **"The state's attempt to make society legible... involves a simplification and standardization of the phenomena of nature and society that is a prerequisite for state control and taxation."**
    *   *Analysis:* The engine of *Seeing Like a State*. It concisely links the bureaucratic act of census or mapping to the political act of domination, revealing the inherent violence in administrative ordering.

3.  **"The push for order, rationality, efficiency, and productivity in the name of 'progress' is, I think, the most dangerous ideology of the last three centuries."**
    *   *Analysis:* This is Scott's indictment of high modernism. By framing it as a dangerous "ideology" rather than neutral rationality, he exposes its political assumptions and catastrophic potential.

4.  **"Zomia is, in a word, a shatter zone of all the peoples who have fled valley states... for the last three thousand years."**
    *   *Analysis:* This single sentence encapsulates the revolutionary core of *The Art of Not Being Governed*. It redefines hill peoples not as primitives left behind, but as political actors who have actively chosen to exit civilization, turning anthropology and history on its head.

5.  **"Anarchy is a wonderful principle... It's a way of organizing a society that has no state, no formal hierarchy... And it works."**
    *   *Analysis:* From *Two Cheers for Anarchism*, this quote reveals the normative heart of his project. It is not just a critique of the state but a positive argument for the viability and dignity of non-hierarchical, self-organized social forms, a vision he traces from ancient societies to modern social movements.
